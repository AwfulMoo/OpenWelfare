Major damage scenarios – bushfires

Table 1: scenarios for the major damage adversely affected criteria. For more scenarios, see the Operational Blueprint for the event.

Note: to find the Operational Blueprint for the event, see Current emergency management events and select Natural Disasters.

Scenario

Eligible or
not eligible

Reason

A customer's principal place of residence sustained major heat and ash damage due to a bushfire. The damage requires extensive repairs. Repairs include:

  • flooring needs refinishing and possible replacement
  • painted walls have begun to bubble and yellow in places
  • metal has begun to rust, pit and corrode
  • wooden furniture has required refinishing

Eligible

The principal place of residence has sustained major damage. It requires repairs and work.

A principal place of residence sustained smoke damage due to a bushfire. Smoke has damaged a lounge and curtains. They cannot be cleaned and must be replaced.

Eligible

The principal place of residence has sustained major damage and soft furnishings need replacing.

A customer has reported that clothes, curtains and carpets smell of smoke in their principal place of residence. The items could be cleaned to remove the smell. They have reported other superficial damage.

Not eligible

The principal place of residence has not suffered major damage. The items reported by the customer can be cleaned. Major damage does not include cleaning an item or surface from smoke, soot, char or ash.

Embers from the same bushfire were blown into a customer's house. After catching fire, the whole front wall of the customer's house collapsed exposing three rooms in the house.

Eligible

The customer's principal place of residence was damaged. After the fire, the interior was left exposed to the elements.

A customer’s house was damaged by smoke. This required the kitchen, lounge room and bathroom walls and ceilings to be repainted.

Eligible

The customer’s principal place of residence has sustained major damage. The house required repainting due to smoke damage.

A customer lives in a 4 bedroom house, 2 streets back from the edge of a bushfire. The customer left their bathroom window open, which resulted in soot and ash blowing into the bathroom, hallway and the entry to 3 bedrooms. All areas are tiled and needed surface cleaning and mopping only.

Not eligible

The interior of the customer’s house was not damaged. There was no need for repair work or replacement of any items.

Major damage scenarios – major asset(s)

Table 2: scenarios for the major damage adversely affected criteria. For more scenarios, see the Operational Blueprint for the event.

Note: to find the Operational Blueprint for the event, see Current emergency management events and select Natural Disasters.

Scenario

Eligible or
not eligible

Reason

The main shearing shed on a property is damaged and must be demolished. There is no damage to the main residence on the property.

The shearing shed is valued at $30,000.

Eligible

The shearing shed is a major asset valued over $20,000. It is owned by the customer and is at the customer’s principal place of residence.

The disaster destroyed the customer’s storage unit in the high-rise complex that the customer lives in. The storage unit is not within the customer’s self-contained residence but is in the basement of the high-rise complex. The customer owns the storage unit and its market value is $25,000.

Eligible

The storage unit is a major asset valued over $20,000. It is owned by the customer and is at the customer’s principal place of residence.

A water tank located at a customer’s principal place of residence needs to be repaired, cleaned, and have the water replaced after being affected by the major disaster. The market value of the water tank including the water is $22,000.

Eligible

The water tank, including the water, is a major asset valued over $20,000 that has sustained major damage.

A free-standing garage suffers major damage due to the disaster. The garage is separate to the principal place of residence but is on the same property. It has a total market value of $30,000. There is no damage to the main residence.

Eligible

The customer’s garage is detached from the main residence but is on the same property. It has a market value of at least $20,000.

The customer has a second residence on their property that is used as an income source for the family. It is rented out on sites such as Airbnb and Stayz. The family does not use the second residence for accommodation. They live in a separate home on the same property. The property is their principal place of residence.

The second residence is destroyed in the disaster, but their main residence is not damaged.

The market value of the second residence is $150,000

Eligible

The customer's major asset (building) at their principal place of residence has been destroyed. It has a market value of over $20,000.

Note: the holiday home rules do not apply as the destroyed building was at the person’s principal place of residence.

Fencing on a customer’s property is damaged and must be replaced. There is no damage to the main residence on the property.

The value of the fencing is $25,000.

Eligible

The fence is a major asset at the person’s principal place of residence and its value is more than $20,000

A boundary fence has been damaged due to the disaster. The shared boundary fence is damaged and must be replaced. The cost of the fence is $45,000.

Eligible

Both persons who share the boundary fence can claim, as its value is more than $20,000 each.

The customer had hired a bulldozer for a week and it was destroyed by a major disaster. The market value of the bulldozer is $200,000.

Not eligible

The major asset must be the property of the person who permanently lives at the principal place of residence.

A free-standing garage (separate to the residence) suffers major damage due to the disaster. There is no damage to the main residence. The market value of the free-standing garage is $10,000.

Not eligible

Although the garage is at the person’s principal place of residence, it is valued at less than $20,000 and is free- standing.

Note: the customer may have been eligible under the major damage criteria if they:

  • regularly lived in the garage
  • had a degree of settled purpose in the garage, and
  • had a lawful right to live in the garage

A customer has their friend’s caravan and motor vehicle at their principal place of residence. The caravan and motor vehicle are destroyed in the disaster. The combined market value of the assets is $70,000. The customer does not own the major assets.

Not eligible

The major asset must owned by the customer who lives at the principal place of residence.

The friend is also not eligible as the car and caravan were not at their principal place of residence.

A customer’s car has been flooded. The asset value of the car is $20,000.00. The customer has had the car cleaned and continues to drive it.

Not eligible

Although the customer’s car has been flooded, the damage was able to be repaired and the car does not need to be replaced.

Customer does not meet the major damage to asset or assets criteria.

Major damage scenarios – other

Table 3: scenarios for the major damage adversely affected criteria. For more scenarios, see the Operational Blueprint for the event.

Note: to find the Operational Blueprint for the event, see Current emergency management events and select Natural Disasters.

Scenario

Eligible or
not eligible

Reason

Due to a power outage at a sewage pumping station, a customer's principal place of residence was spoiled by sewage as their toilets overflowed.

Eligible

The customer's principal place of residence has suffered major damage due to sewage.

Due to the overflow of floodwater and rainfall from the disaster, the automatic floodgates on a local dam opened. The dam waters entered a customer’s principal place of residence causing water damage to the interior of the residence

Eligible

The floodgates were opened because of flood waters/rainfall from the disaster. The damage to the customer’s principal place of residence is a direct result of the disaster.

Due to falling trees, a customer's house had the paint badly scratched on the outside walls along the back of their house.

Not eligible

The interior of the customer's house was not damaged. Nor was it breached due to the disaster.

A customer is in prison and is expected to be in prison for 3 months. The customer is claiming AGDRP due to major damage to their principal place of residence. The customer advised they lived in the principal place of residence before entering prison and will be returning to the same residence when they are released. The customer has provided a copy of a Lease Agreement, confirmation of their release date from prison and photo evidence of damage to the residence.

Eligible

The customer has a legal right and license to reside in the principal place of residence, which was adversely affected by the major disaster.

The customer and their family are holidaying in a holiday house that is owned by the customer. The holiday house is damaged because of the disaster.

Not eligible

The holiday house is not the customer’s principal place of residence because the customer was not living at the residence with a degree of settled purpose.

The disaster destroyed a tent that was pitched in a designated camping area. The tent is the customer’s only residence and he had resided there for 3 months.

Eligible

The tent was pitched on land to, which the customer had a right or licence to occupy as it was a designated camping area. The tent qualifies as the person’s principal place of residence.

Note: if the customer had no right or licence to pitch the tent on the land, the tent does not meet the definition of a principal place of residence.

A principal place of residence sustained major water damage to the residence, arising from a disaster. The damage needs extensive repairs. Examples to be considered when assessing the residence suffering major damage may be:

  • Flooring requires refinishing and/or replacement.
  • Painted walls have begun to bubble and/or discolour
  • Metal has begun to rust, pit and corrode
  • Wooden furniture has required refinishing

Eligible

The residence sustained major damage and requires repairs and work.

Questions to ask customers

Table 4: consider asking the following types of questions when speaking with a customer. This is not a complete list. There is no need to ask them exactly as written. If a customer needs an interpreter - the 4 digit office code ID is 1897 for Smart Centre Emergency Management.

Item

Questions

1

All events – to assess major damage to a principal place of residence or major asset

  • Can you tell me about that?
  • How did it happen?
  • Where in/on the house did that happen?
  • How was your house exposed to the elements?
  • Is that all that happened?
  • Please describe the damage to your home
  • Was there anything else on your property that was damaged? Please describe the damage
  • Is the interior or exterior of your home damaged?
    Exterior damage is not eligible unless it is damage to a customer’s major asset at their principal place of residence
  • Did you have to repair interior or exterior damage?
  • How could you show the damage occurred?
    For example, could you provide a photo or invoice?
    Customers can upload documents online. (Evidence to support major damage should only be requested for 'structurally unsound' or if there are concerns about the validity of a customer's claim. If there are concerns, see Item 12 in Table 2 on the Process page
  • Where was the major asset located on your property?
  • Can the major asset be repaired?
  • Did you own any major assets on your property that were damaged or destroyed? Could you please describe the damage?
    The customer must own the major asset and it must be at their principal place of residence
  • What do you think is the market value of the damaged or destroyed asset/s?
    Major asset/s must have a combined market value of at least $20,000

2

Flood and rain events - to assess major damage to a principal place of residence or major asset

  • How did the flood water/rain water enter your house?
  • How deep was the water in your house?
  • How did the flood water/rain water damage the major asset located at your principal place of residence?

3

Bushfire events – to assess major damage to a principal place of residence or major asset

  • Did you have to replace contents?
    Soft furnishings or electrical appliances must need repairing or replacing. They do not need to have been replaced or repaired yet
  • Could the smoke/ash damage be cleaned?
    Major damage does not include cleaning an item or surface from smoke, soot, char or ash

4

Bushfire events - to assess ‘reasonably close’ to a bushfire for smoke/ash damage

  • How far away was your home from the bushfire?
  • Did you have to evacuate?
  • Did the bushfires impact your suburb or the suburb immediately next to your suburb?
  • Was the bushfire within 5 kilometres of your home?
  • Was the bushfire within 10 kilometres of your home?

See the event specific Operational Blueprint listing for:

  • supporting documents, including maps to check if a principal place of residence is ‘reasonably close’ to a bushfire
  • escalations for assessing claims outside the 10km radius of a bushfire burn scar
