3

AGDRP eligibility + Read more ...

The customer must meet the following on any day of the major disaster:

  • at least 16 years old, or under 16 and getting a social security payment (see ‘social security payment’ in Definitions), and
  • adversely affected by a major disaster

Note: this means:

  • customers at least 16 years old do not need to be getting a social security payment to meet this eligibility criteria
  • only customers aged under 16 must be getting a social security payment to meet this eligibility criteria

They must also meet at least one of the following:

  • an Australian resident (through legal permanent residence and residing in Australia)
    • to check if a visa is a permanent visa, see the References page for a link to the Social Security Guide, Chapter 9.2, Visa subclasses and payment eligibility for visas issued after 1/9/1994, or
  • the holder of a specified visa for AGDRP and DRA, or
  • getting a social security payment, or
  • an Australian citizen who is not an Australian resident but can qualify under a Ministerial determination

'On any day of the major disaster’ is defined in the Operational Blueprint for the event. See the Start date and End date (not the date a Local Government Area is declared).

Note: to find the Operational Blueprint for the event, see Current emergency management events and select Natural Disasters.

A customer cannot receive more than one payment of AGDRP for the same major disaster.

A customer may be eligible for both AGDRP and DRA if they are activated for the same event.

Residence criteria – manual processing

The system does not assess Australian residence for AGDRP claims processed manually. Check the customer meets residence requirements.

For disaster events where the online claiming channel:

  • has been activated, see Processing AGDRP and NZ claims in Process Direct
  • has not been activated, see Processing AGDRP and NZ DRP claims in Customer First

Note: a customer does not need to be in Australia at the time of making their AGDRP claim.

Residence criteria – automation of unsuccessful AGDRP claims

From 10th April 2022, claims that do not meet the residence criteria for AGDRP and therefore not eligible for payment, will be finalised by the system as unsuccessful. That is, the system will finalise the claim as not eligible for the reason ‘Not Residentially Qualified’ if the customer is not:

  • an Australian citizen or permanent resident, or
  • the holder of a current eligible visa (for AGDRP), or
  • a protected SCV holder (subclass 444)

and

  • an Immigration match is received from Home Affairs as expected and validates the customer’s information, and
  • the customer has not already been paid for the same event

4

NZ DRP eligibility + Read more ...

The customer must meet all the following criteria on any day of the major disaster:

  • at least 16 years old, or under 16 and getting a social security payment (see ‘social security payment’ in Definitions)
  • a New Zealand non-protected Special Category Visa (SCV) subclass 444 holder (not an Australian Resident under the Social Security Act 1991) who is living in Australia
  • participated in the Australian tax system for at least one year in the past 3 financial years, or before the ‘next 12 months’ date in NZ DRP tax participation in the Background page of the Operational Blueprint for the event, and
  • adversely affected by a major disaster in Australia

Note: to find the Operational Blueprint for the event, see Current emergency management events and select Natural Disasters.

'On any day of the major disaster’ is defined in the Operational Blueprint for the event. See the Start date and End date (not the date a Local Government Area is declared).

A customer:

  • can also claim as the primary claimant on behalf of a secondary claimant
  • cannot receive more than one NZ DRP for the same major disaster
  • may be eligible for both NZ DRP and NZ DRA if they are activated for the same event

Residence criteria

The system does not assess residence for NZ DRP claims processed manually. Check the customer meets residence requirements.

For disaster events where the online claiming channel:

  • has been activated, see Processing AGDRP and NZ claims in Process Direct
  • has not been activated, see Processing AGDRP and NZ DRP claims in Customer First

Note: a customer does not need to be in Australia at the time of making their NZ DRP claim.

5

NZ DRP claims only - ‘primary claimant’ claims for a ‘secondary claimant’ + Read more ...

A customer claiming NZ DRP (the primary claimant) can claim on behalf of a secondary claimant if the secondary claimant:

  • was adversely affected by the major disaster
  • has not received, or is not eligible for, NZ DRP or AGDRP for the same major disaster

A ‘secondary claimant’ can be a:

  • spouse
    • has the same meaning as ‘member of a couple’ under the Social Security Act 1991 (see ‘member of a couple’ in Definitions)
    • must be part of a couple with the customer who is claiming NZ DRP
    • does not need to be a participant in the Australian taxation system, and
    • can be any nationality
    • not eligible for NZ DRP or AGDRP in their own right
  • dependent adult at least 16 years of age who
    • lives with the customer claiming NZ DRP, and
    • is financially dependent on the customer claiming NZ DRP
  • a child who lives with the customer claiming NZ DRP or a dependent child, this means that the:
    • NZ DRP customer does not need to be the principal carer of the child
    • child must live with the customer claiming NZ DRP

6

NZ DRP claims only – Australian tax system participation + Read more ...

The customer must:

  • have participated in the Australian tax system for at least one year in the past 3 financial years, or
  • will participate in the Australian tax system in the next 12 months

Note: Participation in the Australian tax system means the customer's income is above the tax free threshold for at least 1 year in the past 3 financial years, or will be in the next 12 months.

To check what is above the tax free threshold, when accessing the link from the Resource page to the ‘Australian Taxation Office’s individual income tax rates’, see the Resident tax rates. The customer’s income under the column ‘Taxable income’ must show an amount of tax payable under the column ‘Tax on this income.’ For example, 19 cents for each $1 over $18,200

Participated in the Australian tax system means a customer’s income is over the tax free threshold, or will be in the next 12 months.

Note: if a customer has allowable deductions that takes their income below the tax free threshold, contact Technical Peer Support who will escalate a payment query template to the Level 2 Helpdesk. These queries will be escalated to the Level 3 Helpdesk.

For the relevant financial years and ‘next 12 months’ date, see NZ DRP tax participation in the Background page of the Operational Blueprint for the event.

Note: to find the Operational Blueprint for the event, see Current emergency management events and select Natural Disasters.

Evidence is required before granting NZ DRP

Evidence may include:

  • a previous tax return or Notice of Assessment as proof of participation in the Australian tax system in the past 3 financial years
  • a payslip or letter from their current employer that they will meet this requirement in the next 12 months

If the customer cannot provide the evidence as they have lost their records in the disaster, they can get a copy of their Notice of Assessment from the Australian Tax Office (ATO). Customers with a myGov account may be able to access this information if their ATO account is a linked service.

Also request evidence if the customer cannot show participation in the tax system for any of the following reasons:

  • their income did not meet income tax thresholds
  • they have lodged non-taxable returns or nil activity statements
  • they have not yet lodged a tax return
  • they did not need to lodge tax returns or activity statements

Evidence may include a payslip or letter from the customer’s current employer that there is a likelihood they will pay tax within Australia in the next 12 months.

When submitting a claim, customers are asked to provide this information and advised their claim cannot be assessed until they provide it. NZ DRP cannot be granted until this evidence has been provided.

To ask for evidence, for disaster events where the online claiming channel:

  • has been activated, see Requesting evidence
  • has not been activated, see Asking for information or evidence (in Customer First)

A customer will also need to provide evidence of Australian tax participation when they are claiming on behalf a secondary claimant.

7

Proof of identity (POI) requirements + Read more ...

AGDRP and NZ DRP can be paid before a customer meets identity requirements. However, Service Officers must be satisfied that the person making the claim is:

  • who they say they are, and
  • eligible for the payment

When processing a claim, see Proof of Identity (POI) requirements for emergency payments.

8

Claims for dependent children + Read more ...

Only one person can claim as a principal carer of a child.

If there is 50/50 shared care, when processing a claim:

  • staff trained in Families Shared Care must decide the principal carer
  • staff not trained in Families Shared Care, for disaster events where the online claiming channel:
    • has been activated, transfer the work item, procedure ends here
    • has not been activated, escalate the claim to a trained Service Officer. See Processing AGDRP and NZ DRP claims in Customer First. Procedure ends here

Note: if a customer is claiming for a biological child of a current relationship, a principal carer assessment is not needed. If the other parent disagrees with this decision, they can request a review and a principal carer assessment can be made as part of that process. Where children need to be linked to a customer's record, staff trained in Families Shared Care must do this.

If an event occurs over a period, the principal care of a child may change from one person to another. If both principal carers are adversely affected by the major disaster at different times and meet all other eligibility criteria, they can both receive AGDRP or NZ DRP for the same dependent child.

The principal carer of a dependent child under 16 years of age can claim for that child even if.

  • the child was not adversely affected by the disaster
  • the child was not in their care at the time of the disaster

For screens to check to determine if a customer is the principal carer of a child, for disaster events where the online claiming channel:

  • has been activated, see Processing AGDRP and NZ DRP claims in Process Direct
  • has not been activated, see Processing AGDRP and NZ DRP claims in Customer First

AGDRP only

An AGDRP customer who is not the principal carer of a child cannot claim for a child they were caring for on the day of the disaster, even if the child was adversely affected.

NZ DRP only

NZ DRP customers do not need to be the principal carer of a child. The child must live with the customer claiming NZ DRP. See Secondary claimants.

If a member of a couple is not eligible for NZ DRP as they do not meet the tax participation criteria and their record shows they are the principal carer of a child:

  • their partner can claim on behalf of the child if they are eligible to receive AGDRP/NZ DRP
  • do not unlink any children from the ineligible NZ DRP customer’s record, contact Technical Peer Support (TPS)

If AGDRP or NZ DRP is paid to the incorrect carer

This will need to be reversed before the correct carer can be paid. For disaster events where the online claiming channel:

  • has been activated, see Processing AGDRP and NZ DRP claims in Process Direct
  • has not been activated, see Processing AGDRP and NZ DRP claims in Customer First

9

AGDRP claims only - customers in prison + Read more ...

The following is not relevant for NZ DRP due to the nature of the visa.

AGDRP can be paid to a person in prison. The customer must meet all eligibility criteria, including adversely affected.

For customers in prison claiming damage to their principal place of residence or major asset(s):

  • assess all eligibility criteria
  • investigate the customer’s record
  • ask for evidence to support the customer’s claim
  • contact Technical Peer Support (TPS), who will then escalate the claim to the Level 2 Helpdesk as per Table 3

Examples of evidence:

  • Lease agreement for the period the customer was in prison
  • Rent receipts or rental statement showing the customer paid rent while in prison at the time of the disaster
  • Evidence of home ownership (council rates, utility bills)
  • Evidence of damage to their principal place of residence or major asset(s)

To ask for evidence for disaster events where the online claiming channel:

  • has been activated, contact the customer to request evidence of their address
  • has not been activated, see Processing AGDRP and NZ DRP claims in Customer First

10

Timeframe for claiming + Read more ...

Customers should lodge claims within 6 months of the date the Minister signs the disaster determination.

For the closing date, see the Operational Blueprint for the event.

Note: to find the Operational Blueprint for the event, see Current emergency management events and select Natural Disasters.

Customers may lodge claims outside the time limit in special circumstances.

11

Recoverable death payments + Read more ...

Services Australia may pay AGDRP into the bank account of a deceased person if:

  • the agency was not aware the deceased person had died and
  • the amount would have been payable to the deceased person if they had not died

The amount paid is a debt due to the Commonwealth by the legal representative of the deceased person. It may be recovered in court.

This does not mean a deceased person cannot be eligible for AGDRP. AGDRP is payable on behalf of the deceased person to their estate if they submitted a valid claim before their death. It cannot go into the deceased person’s account if the agency is aware the person is deceased.

If this occurred for a NZ DRP claim, contact Technical Peer Support (TPS). They will escalate the claim to the Level 2 Helpdesk as per Table 3.

Claims cannot be lodged on behalf of deceased customers or their estate.

Definitions and evidence - adversely affected by a major disaster

Item

Description

1

Adversely affected by a major disaster + Read more ...

This means that as a direct result of the major disaster any of the following applies:

  • the customer is seriously injured
  • the customer is an immediate family member of an Australian resident or citizen who was killed or is missing
    • Note: these claims must be referred to a social worker. See AGDRP and NZ DRP claims for an immediate family member who has died or is missing
  • the customer's principal place of residence was destroyed or sustained major damage. This includes major damage caused by smoke or ash. Not eligible: exterior damage to the residence
  • a major asset owned by the customer was destroyed or suffered major damage
  • the customer is the principal carer of a dependent child to whom one or more of the above points applies, or
  • for NZ DRP claims only, the customer is claiming on behalf of a secondary claimant to whom one or more of the above points applies

See the Resources page for scenarios and questions to ask customers.

2

Seriously injured + Read more ...

This means:

  • the person sustained an injury as a direct result of the major disaster, and
  • because of the injury
    • they were admitted to hospital, or
    • they would have been admitted to hospital in normal circumstances

A person may meet this criteria if unusual circumstances relating to the disaster prevented them from being admitted to hospital.

Evidence

Includes evidence of:

  • admittance to hospital
  • treatment
  • discharge from hospital

For disaster events where the online claiming channel:

  • has been activated, when processing a claim see Requesting evidence (in Process Direct)
  • has not been activated, see Asking for information or evidence (in Customer First)

If evidence is not available, a declaration by the customer may be accepted. Contact Technical Peer Support if no evidence is available or if it is unclear whether the customer meets the eligibility criteria. Technical Peer Support will escalate the claim to the Level 2 Helpdesk as per Table 3.

3

Killed + Read more ...

For disaster events where the online claiming channel:

  • has been activated, these cases will be automatically referred to a social worker
  • has not been activated, check the customer meets all other eligibility criteria first. To refer these claims to a social worker, see AGDRP and NZ DRP claims for an immediate family member who has died or is missing

Note: the claim may be granted without evidence of the death if the customer has been impacted by the event in other ways, for example, damage to home or major assets.

Consider the following when assessing if an Australian resident or citizen was killed as a direct result of a major disaster:

  • the person was killed in a way consistent with the impacts of a disaster, or
  • due to the impacts of a disaster, the person was unable to seek medical attention which resulted in their death, or
  • the disaster made worse an existing condition which caused death, or
  • the person is assessed as a 'missing person'

For more information, see AGDRP and NZ DRP claims for an immediate family member who has died or is missing.

Evidence

Evidence should be provided by the customer to:

  • support the assessment of ‘killed as a direct result of the major disaster’
  • show their relationship to the Australian resident or citizen who was killed, for example, birth certificate, marriage certificate

Social workers, as well as providing specialist support, can help in obtaining evidence.

4

Missing person + Read more ...

For disaster events where the online claiming channel:

  • has been activated, these cases will be automatically referred to a social worker
  • has not been activated, check the customer meets all other eligibility criteria first. To refer these claims to a social worker, see AGDRP and NZ DRP claims for an immediate family member who has died or is missing

Note: the claim may be granted without evidence of the death if the customer has been impacted by the event in other ways, for example, damage to home or major assets.

A 'missing person' is an Australian resident or citizen whose whereabouts are unknown, and there are serious concerns for their safety and welfare due to the disaster.

Consider if the person:

  • was near the disaster at the time of the disaster, and
  • has not communicated with family members, friends, colleagues or significant others in the 14 days following the disaster, and
  • not returned to their principal place of residence or work in the 14 days following the disaster, and
  • not found by police and emergency services

For AGDRP and NZ DRP, a ‘missing person’ will be presumed killed as a direct result of the major disaster.

For more information, see AGDRP and NZ DRP claims for an immediate family member who has died or is missing.

Evidence

The customer should provide a copy of a missing person report. If this is not available, a declaration by the customer may be accepted. A declaration should not be accepted until the social worker has assessed the customer’s circumstances.

Social workers, as well as providing specialist support, can help in obtaining evidence.

5

Principal place of residence + Read more ...

A principal place of residence is where a person:

  • regularly lives with a degree of settled purpose, and
  • has a lawful right to live

Degree of settled purpose in that place means:

  • a regular or usual pattern of living there, and
  • settlement with a degree of permanence

Settlement could include, but is not limited to, living in a place for:

  • caring,
  • education,
  • work or
  • medical treatment or condition.

This is not a complete list.

A principal place of residence can include any of the following, if the customer regularly lives there with a degree of settled purpose and has a lawful right to live there:

  • home that a customer owns
  • home that a customer rents

A caravan or tent can be a principal place of residence if a customer has a right or licence to be on the land where the caravan is parked or the tent is pitched. If the customer has no right or licence to park their caravan or pitch their tent on the land, this does not meet the definition of ‘principal place of residence’.

A person can have more than one principal place of residence but can only make one claim for the same major disaster. For example, a customer may have a second principal place of residence that they live in during the week as it is close to a hospital where they receive on-going medical treatment.

A person may not have a principal place of residence if they do not have a residence they normally live in and have a legal right or license to live in.

Holiday homes, second residences and investment properties generally do not meet the definition of a person's principal place of residence.

The principal place of residence:

  • includes all rooms that are fully enclosed and under a common roof space
  • in most free-standing homes includes a garage or a similar structure (such as a laundry) if it was
    • under the common roof space, or
    • attached to the main residence
  • can include structures apart from the main residence but on the same property if the person
    • regularly lives in the place with a degree of settled purpose, and
    • has a lawful right to live in the place
  • for high density housing (multi-storey unit, apartment block or townhouse) is the self-contained residence only. It excludes all common areas the person may access (for example, staircases, hallways and common car parks or basements). However, it can include common areas that form part of the internal make-up of the self-contained residence (for example, common walls and ceilings)

Evidence of a customer’s principal place of residence is not required, unless there are concerns about the validity of an address a customer has provided (for example advising they have moved into a declared LGA).

If there are concerns:

  • Ask for evidence such as:
    • utility bills
    • current rental agreement
    • bank statements
    • driver’s licence
  • See Item 12

A prison or place of detention (AGDRP only)

A prison or place of detention is not a person's principal place of residence. Although they normally live there, they do not have a license or right to live there. This may mean that some prisoners have no principal place of residence.

However, they may be eligible for AGDRP under other adversely affected criteria. For example, the prisoner:

  • is the immediate family member of an Australian resident or citizen killed, or
  • has been seriously injured

This is not relevant for NZ DRP due to the nature of the visa

6

Destroyed + Read more ...

Destroyed means, as a direct result of the major disaster:

  • for a principal place of residence, it must be demolished
  • for a major asset it has been damaged to the extent that it is unusable

Evidence

A declaration by the customer may be accepted.

Evidence is not needed, unless there are concerns about the validity of information a customer has given.

If there are concerns, see Item 12.

7

Major damage - general + Read more ...

For a principal place of residence, major damage means at least one of the following has occurred, as a direct result of the major disaster:

  • the interior has sustained major damage, or
  • it has been breached, exposing the interior to the elements, or
  • it has been declared structurally unsound from a credible authority such as a builder or engineer’s report, or the interior has been spoilt by sewage

Note: exterior damage to a principal place of residence is not eligible.

The major damage may have been repaired or yet to be repaired. Focus on the actual damage, not the potential for damage.

Be objective when assessing major damage. Use the details and evidence provided by the customer and the definitions above.

See the Resources page for scenarios and questions to ask customers.

Evidence

Evidence is not needed, unless there are concerns about the validity of information the customer has given.

If there are concerns, see Item 12.

8

Major damage - major asset or assets + Read more ...

These must be at the person’s principal place of residence. The person must own the asset.

They can be:

  • one asset with a market value of at least $20,000, or
  • 2 or more assets with a combined market value of at least $20,000

Examples:

  • building
  • large fixed structure
  • motor vehicle
  • caravan
  • water tank and the water it contains
  • large scale machinery
  • heavy equipment
  • fencing (if it separates 2 properties, each customer can claim for the same fence)

Examples do not include items inside a home at a principal place of residence, such as:

  • Carpets
  • Rugs
  • Fridges
  • Televisions, or
  • Beds

To assess major damage, see Major damage – general. Also see:

  • Major damage caused by smoke or ash - bushfire events only
  • Major damage - flood and rain events only

For a major asset:

  • other than a water tank, major damage means to the extent it needs to be replaced as a direct result of the major disaster. This means if the major asset does not need to be replaced the customer does not meet the eligibility criteria
    It may have been replaced, or yet to be replaced
  • that is a water tank, major damage means to the extent it needs to be repaired or cleaned, or the water replaced, as a direct result of the major disaster
    It may have been or is yet to be, repaired or cleaned or the water replaced

Focus on the actual damage, not the potential for damage.

Be objective when assessing major damage. Use the details and evidence provided by the customer and the definitions above.

Note: a driveway on a rural property may be considered a major asset when the:

  • part of the driveway that is destroyed or suffered major damage is wholly located at the person’s principal place of residence and is on private land, and
  • driveway provides the only vehicle access to the person’s principal place of residence, and
  • driveway has a market value of at least $20,000

See the Resources page for scenarios and questions to ask customers.

Evidence

Evidence is not needed, unless there are concerns about the validity of information a customer has given.

If there are concern, see Item 12.

9

Major damage - flood and rain events only + Read more ...

Floodwater

Floodwater is water flowing along the ground at floor level.

As long as floodwaters have entered a person’s principal place of residence, at floor level through any means, as a direct result of the major disaster, they meet the criteria for major damage.

Rainwater

Rainwater is water that falls as rain from the sky.

For heavy rainfall to meet the major damage criteria, rainwater needs to have entered a person’s principal place of residence through a breach, as a direct result of the major disaster. For example, there must have been damage to the residence that exposes the interior of the residence to the elements.

Rainwater that comes through a breach:

  • that is supposed to be there, for example doorway or window (open or closed) - this does not meet the major damage criteria
  • that is not supposed to be there, for example, broken tiles, lifted tin, overflowing gutters, hole in the wall – this does meet the major damage criteria

Note: a breach is a gap in, for example, a wall, window or roof

Mould

If a customer advises they have mould in their principal place of residence, this does not mean they meet the major damage criteria. Flood or rainwater must have entered the customer’s principal place of residence, as a direct result of the major disaster as per Floodwater and Rainwater above, for them to meet this criteria.

Mould growth because of atmospheric moisture (high humidity) for the period of a disaster event does not meet the major damage criteria.

See the Resources page for scenarios and questions to ask customers.

Evidence

Evidence is not needed, unless there are concerns about the validity of the information the customer has given.

If there are concerns: see Item 12.

10

Major damage caused by smoke or ash - bushfire events only + Read more ...

When assessing if smoke and ash from the major disaster have caused major damage, consider if the damage described by the customer:

  • needs repair work or replacement of soft furnishings or electrical appliances, and
  • if the principal place of residence is reasonably close to the major disaster

Consider major damage before assessing if the customer’s principal place of residence is reasonably close to the major disaster.

Repair work may include, as a direct result of the major disaster:

  • repainting walls or ceiling
  • replacing the frame of the house or plasterboard
  • replacing porous surfaces such as stone benchtops or tiles due to discolouration
  • replacing metal hardware which has rusted or corroded
  • replacing air conditioning or heating systems including ducting
  • replacing any parts of the electrical system including wiring, cords, outlets, switches, fuses and breaker boxes

Soft furnishings may include mattresses, lounges, curtains and carpets. It does not include clothing and linens.

Major damage does not include cleaning an item or surface from smoke, soot, char or ash.

The major damage may have been repaired or yet to be repaired.

Focus on the actual damage, not the potential for damage.

Be objective when assessing major damage. Use the details and evidence provided by the customer and the definitions above.

See the Resources page for scenarios and questions to ask customers.

Evidence

Evidence is not needed, unless there are concerns about the validity of information the customer has given.

If there are concerns, see Item 12.

11

Reasonably close – bushfire events only + Read more ...

The Operational Blueprint for the event has tools that help assess if a principal place of residence is reasonably close to the major disaster.

Note: to find the Operational Blueprint for the event, see Current emergency management events and select Natural Disasters.

For bushfires, if the customer’s principal place of residence is outside the 10km burn scar radius, Service Officers can escalate the claim to Technical Peer Support (TPS). First, complete or confirm the following and record any relevant information:

  • the customer meets all other eligibility criteria
  • there has been major damage and the customer has shown how they have met the criteria
  • if the Service Officer doubts the validity of the customer’s claim, ask for evidence before contacting Technical Peer Support (TPS)
