Consideration of distance - assessment of constant care for CP

Information and examples on determining if constant care is being provided when the care and care receiver are not living together

Note: consider each case based on the circumstances. The distances below are a guide only. The assessment of constant care does not apply to CP (XWP). Care requirements for these carers only apply to CA.

Distance of carer living apart from care receiver (kms)

Constant Care

Action

Example

Less than 100 kms

Likely to be able to provide constant care

Distance is unlikely to be preventing constant care. However, consider the other factors such as accessibility of the carer to the care receiver and the level of care provided

The carer lives geographically close to the care receiver but has to use public transport. They live in an area where public transport is limited in amount and times available. The carer may not be providing constant care

100-149 kms

May be able to provide constant care

Consider distance and all other factors. If unsure contact the Carer Helpdesk for assistance

The decision needs to be clearly DOC'd on the carers record

The addresses may be some distance apart but the carer and care receiver are moving between them together

150 kms or more

Unlikely to be able to provide constant care

If there are unusual circumstances which mean the carer may be providing constant care contact the Carer Helpdesk for assistance

The situation is temporary as the carer may be looking for new accommodation to be near the care receiver who has recently moved

Factors to consider when assessing constant care for CP when the carer and care receiver live together in more than 1 private residence

Note: consider each case based on the circumstances. The below factors are a guide only. The assessment of constant care does not apply to CP (XWP). Care requirements for these carers only apply to CA.

It is possible that the carer and care receiver are living together in 2 homes. This usually occurs when they are related, own their respective homes, the homes are a significant distance apart and they stay at each other’s home regularly and for a reasonable time. Often the carer has a partner or children and this prevents them from leaving their home on permanently.

  • How far apart are the homes and how long does it take to travel between them?
  • How long do they spend at each home and how often?
  • When did this start and why?
  • Is it intended to continue?
  • What activities of daily living do the carer and care receiver do when they stay at each home, for example eat, sleep or shower?
  • Are there any activities which either party only does at 1 of the homes, for example laundry?
  • Does either party keep some of their possessions at both homes all the time or do they only take some possessions with them temporarily while they are at 1 of the homes, for example clothes, toiletries, reading material, items related to hobbies?
  • Is there any equipment used as part of the caring which is only at 1 of the homes? Why?

If there is a significant distance between the 2 homes and it would not be reasonable for either the carer or the care receiver to travel between the 2 homes each day and they stay at each home for a reasonable time on an ongoing basis, it would usually be accepted that care is being provided in 2 homes.

It is not possible to define reasonable time, but it would need to support the claim that the carer or care receiver is living in that home.
