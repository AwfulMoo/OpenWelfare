Notification of change in care

Table 1: This table describes the process steps for notification of a change in care.

Step

Action

1

Notification of a care change + Read more ...

Customers or their representatives can notify Child Support of a potential change in the care percentage:

  • in person (via telephone, counter enquiry or Change of Assessment conference)
  • electronically (CSAonline or email)
  • through an overseas Central Authority, see Central Authority Address Listing for Child Support International customers
  • in writing (via form or letter)

When customers notify Child Support of a care change, investigate all notifications of a change in care by gathering relevant information from both customers (even if customers are reporting a reduced level of care for themselves).

If customer reports a care change online or in writing and the information they have provided is unclear or incomplete, contact the notifying customer to ensure all relevant information is gathered and discussed.

If a customer reports a change in care that relates to a one-off event, what constitutes a change to the pattern of care will depend upon the individual circumstances of the case. See References for a link to the Child Support Guide 2.2.2: Care Determinations & Changes in Care - Change in pattern of care.

During discussions explain to both customers what a change in care is. Advise them the change in care decision is Services Australia's decision about the amount of care to be used in the customer’s child support assessment. The agency does not determine where the child should live or any custody arrangements. The agency reflects the percentage of care of the child that each parent actually has, or the percentage of care established by a written care arrangement. A care event is a night of care or several consecutive nights of care that follow a recurring pattern. Confirm the date of event - future changes in care cannot be taken into account and a decision cannot be revoked unless a new care event has occurred.

The circumstances around care changes can be distressing, especially where the care is disputed or not occurring in line with a written care arrangement. Be considerate and sensitive to the customer’s experience when discussing and explaining care changes.

Confirm with the customer:

  • the details of the change in care
  • the date the change applies from

Discuss with the customer:

  • any impacts on the assessment and the amount payable
  • facts the decision will be based on
  • options if they disagree with the decision

Registrar’s discretionary power

Advise the customer that unless the reported care change results in a change in the cost percentage, there will be no effect on the assessment and we may not revoke the existing care or make a new care determination. This is because under Section 54H of the Child Support (Assessment) Act 1989 (see the References page for a link to legislation), we have discretion to revoke care and make a new determination that would not make a material difference to the assessment. In the event a change in care is contested. A care percentage decision will only be made if it would result in a change to the child support assessment. The exception is where a written care arrangement exists that has not yet been recorded or applied by Child Support. In this situation the written care arrangement needs to be recorded in Cuba as:

  • a new care determination with the appropriate written care type if being adhered to, or
  • for information only if the actual care is different to that described in the written care arrangement

For more information, see Investigation of change in child support care (post 1 July 2010).

Note: if it is evident the reported care change will not vary the cost percentage, the WA Ex-nuptial and pre 23/05/2018 Change in care details macro must still be used to guide the customer conversation and accurately calculate the reported care percentage.

2

Check notification + Read more ...

Open the WA Ex-nuptial and pre 23/05/2018 Change in care details macro to guide a conversation throughout the care notification process and create a document for Cuba.

Ask the customer if they are a current or past Centrelink customer. If so, advise them to ensure their Centrelink relationship and income details are correct to reduce the likelihood of incorrect Centrelink benefits or payments occurring when the care decision is transferred. Update these details before finalising the care decision in Cuba. After discussing child support matters, offer to cold transfer the customer to Centrelink to update their relationship and income details if required.

Ask the customer if they have reported the care change details to Centrelink for each child in their care for whom they are claiming Family Tax Benefit (FTB). If the customer has notified Centrelink of the care change, see Centrelink care determination (post 1 July 2010).

If the customer has not contacted Centrelink about the care change, go to Step 4, unless the customer is notifying of:

  • multiple care changes, go to Step 3
  • a change in care of a relevant dependent child, see Updating Child Support customer and assessment information
  • a child not already reflected on the assessment (including any active reverse Cuba case) see Applications for a child support assessment
  • a change in care that needs to be updated on their notional assessment see Notional assessment relating to child support agreements
  • a change in care due to the customers reconciling, see Suspension of child support liability on reconciliation

3

Multiple care change notifications + Read more

Where a parent delays in notifying Child Support or Centrelink of changes in care, they may report multiple changes at once.

Treat each notification as a separate care change using the care period as 12 months from the date of the event. Calculate the care percentage for the first event as though the subsequent event had not occurred. The resulting care percentage for each care change event will be used in the assessment to the day before the date of effect of the subsequent care change (assuming the subsequent event results in a different care percentage and the customer has notified of the change within 28 days).

Note: if the date of notification for a care change is more than 28 days after the event date, follow this process even though the change may not be used in the Child Support assessment. See Child Support date of effect for more information about when a new care percentage will be used in the assessment.

See the Resources page for examples of multiple care change notifications.

4

Determine likely date of event + Read more

The term 'likely' means information gathered from the initial customer only. Identify the date of event by asking the customer what circumstances led to the change in care. When identifying the date of event, consider whether the care that is actually occurring for the child has changed.

The date of event is either:

  • the date both customers confirm the actual care change occurred
  • a date determined by Child Support based on the information and evidence provided by both customers

Although a Child Support care change will only be applied from the date of effect, the date of event may be used by Centrelink if the customer has an FTB assessment.

Note: the date of event used to record the care change will be tentative until the other customer has been contacted.

Questions that may assist in determining the likely date of event include:

  • which child does the change relate to
  • what date did the care/pattern of care change
  • what caused the change in care
  • was the care change discussed with the other parent
  • is the change intended to be permanent
  • if the care change is temporary, what is the period of the change? Note: if a one-off care event is missed that is likely to be made up/the care is likely to resume to the standard pattern, this means a care change may not have occurred

If unable to determine the date of event, see Step 2 in the Investigate change in care table 'Gather information from the other customer'.

5

Determine likely care period + Read more

When a customer notifies Child Support of a change in care, determine the likely care period. A care period is generally the 12 month period starting from the date of event.

6

Calculate care period + Read more

A care period is the 12 month period starting from the date of event. In some circumstances, a shorter or longer care period may be appropriate, however judge each case on its own merits. If determining a care period other than 12 months, seek technical support. See Technical support in Child Support.

Care periods longer than 12 months

A care period of more than 12 months may be appropriate where the care arrangement follows a cycle over a period greater than 12 months, for example every 2 years.

See Resources page for examples of care periods longer than 12 months.

Care periods shorter than 12 months

A care period of less than 12 months might be appropriate where the pattern of care will gradually change over definable periods in a specific and measurable way, for example one parent is to increase their care by one day per week every 3 months over the upcoming period.

A shorter care period may also be appropriate where a carer has unexpectedly gained 100% care of a child for a temporary period (generally 4 weeks minimum). In these situations, the Registrar will determine the care over a short care period related to the unexpected circumstance and make a one-off block of 100% care determination.

See Resources page for examples of care periods shorter than 12 months, and References page in WA Ex-nuptial child support cases - Change in child support care levels (post 1 July 2010) for further information.

Note: Child Support will not give effect to the future events until the customers advise that they have actually occurred. If neither customer advises of the subsequent change, the existing percentage of care will continue.

Determine likely percentage of care

Table 2: This table describes the process on gathering of information from the notifying customer to determine likely percentage of care.

Step

Action

1

Determine likely percentage of care + Read more

The term 'likely' means information gathered from the initial customer only. Care is expressed as a percentage of care (generally based on a number of nights) that a customer is likely to have during the care period as shown in the care percentage table.

See the Resources page for the Care percentage table.

2

Establish pattern of care + Read more

Ask the customer to provide the pattern of care based on what they actually expect to occur during the 12 month care period. In most cases, care is determined by the actual care each parent has. Where a pattern has been established in the past and the Service Officer is satisfied that it will continue in the future, use that pattern to determine the likely percentage of care for the care period.

If the customer:

  • requests that care be calculated in a form other than nights, see Step 2 in the Calculate and determine care percentage table in Child support care outcomes and decision making (post 1 July 2010) - Care other than in nights
  • has unexpectedly gained 100% care of a child for a temporary period (generally 4 weeks minimum), see Step 3 in the Calculate and determine care percentage table in Child support care outcomes and decision making (post 1 July 2010) - One-off block of 100% care
  • notifies Child Support within a reasonable time and informs that the level of care for the other parent (who has at least regular care) has fallen below 14% despite the child being made available to them, see Step 7 in the Calculate and determine care percentage table in Child support care outcomes and decision making (post 1 July 2010) - Below regular care decision

Note: if a customer wants to estimate future or possible care changes themselves, refer them to the Care Estimator available on servicesaustralia.gov.au.

Questions that may assist in determining the likely percentage of care include:

  • have you and the other parent established a pattern of care for the child
  • how many nights do you and the other parent expect to care for your child over the 12 month care period from the date the care changed
  • how did you calculate the expected care (for example, is there a pattern of care)
  • do you or the other parent provide extensive care during the day but little or no overnight care? See Step 2 in the Calculate and determine care percentage table in Child support care outcomes and decision making (post 1 July 2010) - Care other than in nights
  • what is the care based on currently
  • are there any court orders, parenting plans, or written agreements for a different amount of care
  • are either you or the other parent trying to enforce this
  • are you seeking a new court order, parenting plan, or written agreement, or are you aware of the other parent seeking such a new care arrangement
  • is the other parent likely to agree that these are the new care arrangements

If there is a care arrangement to which the parents are no longer adhering, establish whether the parent with the reduced care is taking reasonable action to either ensure the care arrangement is complied with or to seek a new care arrangement. If so, see Step 8 in the Calculate and determine care percentage table in Child support care outcomes and decision making (post 1 July 2010) - Disputed care.

3

Care calculator + Read more

The care calculator must be used to calculate care in all instances, except where the pattern of care being notified results in either:

  • a care percentage of 100% or 0%
  • the number of nights as 365 or 0

In this event, go to Step 5.

For all other notified care changes, open the WA Ex-nuptial and pre 23/05/2018 Change in care details macro and use the care calculator to determine the percentage of care and create a document for Cuba. From discussions with the customer, input the pattern of care or the number of nights the customer will be caring for the child. In the event the calculation is for care using the number of hours, calculate the number of nights first.

4

Percentage of care (special rounding rules) + Read more

Cuba will automatically calculate the percentage of care used in the assessment when the number of nights care has been entered into the Care window.

There may be occasions when Service Officers need to manually calculate the percentage of care, or explain how the figures were calculated, as percentages of care calculations have special rounding rules.

Percentage of care = (number of nights / 365) x 100

To calculate a percentage of care the following special rounding rules must be applied (to ensure the total equals 100%). If the percentage of care is:

  • below 50%, round the number down to the next whole percentage
  • 50% or more, round the number up to the next whole percentage

Where each customer has a care percentage of 50%, record the payee as having 50% care, see the Care Cuba Process Help. Do not record this as a number of nights. Cuba will automatically change this to show the payee has 51% care and the other party has 49% care. The Care Reported as 50% checkbox will be automatically set by Cuba and a care percentage of 50% transferred to Centrelink.

Note: covering letters to customers who share 50% split care will indicate both parties have 50% care of the child, however the assessment notice will show 51% or 49%. This has no impact on the assessed rate as the same cost percentage is used.

See the Resources page for examples of percentage of care (special rounding rules).

5

Child Support date of effect + Read more

Child Support and Centrelink have different rules for the date of effect.

If Child Support or Centrelink is notified within 28 days from the date the care changed, the date of effect will be the date the care changed.

If Child Support or Centrelink is advised more than 28 days after the date of the care change, the date of effect will be the date of notification, unless:

  • a parent's care has fallen below 14% despite the child being made available to them and the other parent notifies Child Support within a reasonable time, see Below regular care decision in the Calculate and determine care percentage table
  • a terminating event has occurred where the child is no longer in the care of any of the parties (parents and non-parent carers who have applied for child support) to the assessment
  • care is changed to reflect a child as either a relevant dependent child or a Claimed For child when a case is suspended upon reconciliation or reinstated due to separation, see Suspension of child support liability on reconciliation

Note: if a child moves from the care of one party to another, a terminating event has not occurred. Amend the assessment to reflect the new care percentage from the date of effect.

See the Resources page for scenarios and examples to determine date of effect.

6

Centrelink date of effect + Read more

For Centrelink, most care changes are applied for FTB purposes from the date of event, regardless of when Centrelink is notified of the care change. Determine and record the exact date the care changed as this information may be used by Centrelink in determining a customer’s FTB entitlement. If a customer requests further information about the impact of the care change on their FTB entitlement, transfer them to Centrelink.

7

Gather information from notifying customer + Read more ...

Ensure all relevant information has been gathered from the notifying customer to determine the likely event date, care period, percentage of care, date of effect and type of care change.

If either customer receives FTB for the child, the care decision will be sent to Centrelink and may affect any Centrelink benefits or payments they receive.

Explain that:

  • the care decision made by Child Support will be sent to Centrelink
  • the customer should ensure their Centrelink relationship and income details are correct to reduce the likelihood of incorrect Centrelink benefits or payments occurring when the care decision is transferred. Update these details before finalising the care decision in Cuba
  • the date of effect for FTB purposes may be different to the date used by the Child Support, so it is important that they provide all information about the date of event to Child Support, even if it was more than 28 days ago
  • Centrelink will advise them (in writing) of any impact on their Centrelink entitlements

If the customer requires additional information, or wants to update their Centrelink relationship or income details, offer to cold transfer them to Centrelink at the end of the discussion.

Document all conversations (including macro documents), contact attempts and information gathered in the Care window notepad, see Documenting Child Support information.

8

Notifying customer indicates the other party is ‘likely’ to dispute the reported change + Read more ...

If the notifying customer advises that the other party is ‘likely’ to dispute the change of care, clarify which facts of the reported change the other party is ‘likely’ to dispute, for example

  • the date of event
  • the details of the care such as number of nights, hours of care or pattern of care

Use the WA Ex-nuptial and pre 23/05/2018 Change in care details macro to guide and document the conversation.

Note: a care change is only disputed if the parties disagree about the facts of the care change. A customer advising that the other party is likely to be unhappy with the change in care does not mean that party will disagree with the facts of the change.

Add new record for the likely care change

Table 3: This table describes the process to add a new care record based on the type of care, care percentages and date of event details provided by the notifying customer.

Step

Action

1

Care types + Read more

Determine the appropriate care 'Type' prior to pending the care change record in Cuba. As different types of care decisions require different levels of authorisation, consult with an authorised officer and obtain relevant approvals prior to making a decision.

2

Actual + Read more

The care type of 'Actual' care is recorded where:

  • there is no court order, parenting plan or written agreement
  • a one-off block of 100% care determination is made
  • there is a court order, parenting plan or written agreement, and
  • care is not occurring in line with the written care arrangement. Note: the care arrangement should still be recorded for information in Cuba
  • an interim determination is not appropriate

3

Below 14% Care + Read more

Where current care is 14% or more and Child Support is notified that the care is not or has ceased to be in accordance with that care, this care type is recorded as 'Actual', see Step 7 in the Calculate and determine care percentage table in Child support care outcomes and decision making (post 1 July 2010) - Below regular care decision.

4

Court Order + Read more

The care type of 'Court order' is recorded where care is:

  • occurring in line with the court order, or
  • not occurring in line with the court order and an interim decision is appropriate (check the disputed care indicator and 14 to 26 week box)

5

CSA decision + Read more

The care type of 'CSA decision' is recorded where:

  • there is an existing court order, parenting plan or written agreement
  • the parent with less care than set out in the order, plan or agreement is seeking a new order, plan or agreement
  • the new percentage of care sought by the parent is greater than their actual care but less than the care percentage provided by the original care arrangement
  • there are special circumstances (check the disputed care indicator and 14 to 26 week box)

6

Parenting Plan + Read more

The care type of 'Parenting plan' is recorded where care:

  • is occurring in line with the plan, or
  • is not occurring in line with the plan and an interim decision is appropriate (check the disputed care indicator and 14 to 26 week box)

7

Written agreement + Read more

The care type of 'Written agreement' is recorded where care:

  • is occurring in line with the agreement, or
  • is not occurring in line with the agreement, and an interim decision is appropriate (and check the disputed care indicator and 14 to 26 week box)

Note: document court orders, parenting plans and written agreements provided by customers in the Care window, even if care is not occurring as per the care arrangement, see Step 6 in the Calculate and determine care percentage table in Child support care outcomes and decision making (post 1 July 2010) - Care arrangement and actual care applies. See Documenting Child Support information.

Do not select the care types of Oral Agreement, Interim Decision or Court Ordered.

Save the pended care changes to generate a Pending Care Decision intray. An indicator will also be sent to Centrelink via data exchange advising a change to the current care for that child has been recorded by Child Support.

8

Pend care record + Read more ...

If the care change notification requires further investigation pend the reported care change in Cuba with the relevant 'Type' and a status of 'Pending'. See Care Cuba Process Help.

If the notifying customer has advised that the other party is 'likely' to dispute the information provided, see Customer referral guidelines for Child Support staff to determine if an Integrated care referral is appropriate.

If discretion to not revoke a previous care determination under Section 54H of the Child Support (Assessment) Act 1989, is to apply without further investigation do not pend the notified care. The References page contains a link to legislation.

Note: a decision not to exercise the discretion to revoke the existing care percentage decision, where there would not be a change in the cost percentage, is not a care percentage decision and does not have objection rights. There is no requirement to pend notifications in this circumstance.

If a change in care has been pended and then the discretion not to revoke the existing care is applied, the pending record should be deleted, not 'rejected'.

The WA Ex-nuptial and pre 23/05/2018 Change in care details macro must still be used to guide the customer conversation, accurately calculate the reported care percentage and document the customer contact.

9

Correcting pending care details + Read more

If the wrong care type has been entered, dates or care percentage, amend the pending care record to reflect the correct care details. Ensure the correct details are recorded for the type of care, notification date and date of event. Seek technical support, see Technical support in Child Support.

See Step 1 in Investigation of a change in care table in Investigation of change in child support care (post 1 July 2010) - Investigate care.
