Care changes notified up to 14 May 2019 for WA Ex-nuptial child support cases must be managed under the legislation that was in place prior to the amendments effective 1 July 2018, see For WA Ex-nuptial cases - Notification of change in child support care (post 1 July 2010).

1

Notification of a care change + Read more ...

Customers or their representatives can notify Child Support of a potential change in the care percentage:

  • in person (via telephone, counter enquiry or Change of Assessment conference)
  • electronically ( CSAonline or email)
  • through an overseas Central Authority, see Central Authority Address Listing for Child Support International customers
  • in writing

2

Discuss and document change in care notification + Read more ...

Open the Change in care macro. Use the macro to record verbal, online and written care change notifications. The macro helps to:

  • guide and record the customer conversation
  • calculate the reported care percentage, and
  • document the outcome

Use effective questioning techniques to investigate all notifications of a change in care by gathering relevant information from both customers (even if customers are reporting a reduced level of care for themselves). This includes the:

  • details of when the care changed (date of event)
  • the pattern of care, and
  • details of any court order or written care arrangement, including the date it commenced

Quality care conversations with customers when they notify of a care change helps to get a timely accurate outcome for both customers.

If a customer reports a care change online or in writing and:

  • the information provided is unclear or incomplete, contact the notifying customer to ensure all relevant information is gathered, clarified and discussed
  • they are notifying of the change more than 26 weeks after the date the care changed, check if a care decision may result in a care terminating event. If so, contact both customers to ask if they wish to apply for a new child support assessment, see Step 8 in Gather information and determine likely percentage of care table.

3

Establish and clarify change in care details + Read more ...

Changes in care arrangement can be a very emotional life event, especially where there is a disagreement about care or it is not occurring in line with a written care arrangement. Be considerate and sensitive to the customer’s experience when discussing and explaining care changes.

Use open ended questions to establish and clarify change in care details including date of event and pattern of care, such as

  • What is the actual care that is occurring?
  • When did the change actually occur?
  • When did you last have overnight care?
  • Why did the care change?
  • How was the care change discussed with the other parent?
  • Who is aware of the arrangements?
  • Which child/ren does the change relate to?
  • Is the change intended to be permanent?
  • If the care change is temporary, what is the period of the change?

Confirm with the customer:

  • the details of the change in care, just discussed
  • the date the change applies from
  • whether the care had been/is occurring in accordance with a court order or written care agreement

Discuss with the customer:

  • any impacts on the assessment and the amount payable
  • the facts the decision will be based on
  • options if they disagree with the decision

If a customer reports a change in care that relates to a one-off event, what constitutes a change to the pattern of care will depend upon the individual circumstances of the case. See the References page for a link to the Child Support Guide 2.2.2: Care Determinations & Changes in Care - Change in pattern of care.

Explain to both customers what a change in care is. Advise them the change in care decision is Services Australia’s decision about the amount of care to be used in their child support assessment. The agency does not determine where the child should live or any custody arrangements. The agency reflects the percentage of care of the child that each parent actually has, or the percentage of care established by a written care arrangement. A care event is a night of care or several consecutive nights of care that follow a recurring pattern. Confirm the date of event. Future changes in care cannot be taken into account and a decision cannot be revoked unless a new care event has occurred.

If a care decision will result in the receiving parent having a care percentage of less than 35% for a child, check if a child support agreement applies to the child. If an agreement applies, transfer the customer to the Agreements and Court Orders team to discuss the impact on their agreement.

If care is being notified more than 26 weeks after the date the care changed, check if a care decision may result in a care terminating event. If so, contact both customers to ask if they wish to apply for a new child support assessment, see Step 8 in Gather information and determine likely percentage of care table.

4

No change to the cost percentage + Read more ...

Advise the customer that unless the reported care change results in a change in the cost percentage, there will be no effect on the assessment and a new care determination may not be made. This is because under Section 54H of the Child Support (Assessment) Act 1989 (see the References page for a link to legislation), a new determination is not required if a material difference to the assessment is not made.

When deciding whether to investigate a care decision that will not affect the cost percentage, consider whether:

  • the notifying customer seeks to have the change reflected, despite understanding it will not affect the cost percentage, or
  • the care is based on a written care arrangement, or
  • the care has ceased to be in line with a written care arrangement, or
  • there are other circumstances that warrant making a care decision. Circumstances that may warrant further consideration include
    • if the notification is for an increase to the other party’s care
    • a history of escalations, objections and dissatisfaction, or
    • Family and Domestic Violence issues

If none of the above circumstances exist, make a refusal decision under s54H or s54HA.

If making a care refusal decision use reject functionality in Cuba and record the reason for decision. Send the automatically generated refusal/rejection letters to explain:

  • the reported care change has been refused as it would not affect the child support assessment, and
  • the customer’s objection rights

The References page contains a link to legislation.

5

Check notification + Read more ...

Ask the customer if they are a current or past Centrelink customer. If so, advise them to ensure their Centrelink relationship and income details are correct to reduce the likelihood of incorrect Centrelink benefits or payments occurring when the care decision is transferred. After discussing child support matters, offer to cold transfer the customer to Centrelink to update their relationship and income details if required.

Ask the customer if they have reported the care change details to Centrelink for each child in their care for whom they are claiming Family Tax Benefit (FTB).

If the customer has not contacted Centrelink about the care change, go to Step 4, unless the customer is notifying of:

  • multiple care changes. Ask the customer if Centrelink has been notified of all care changes. If not, go to Step 3
  • a change in care of a relevant dependent child, see Updating Child Support customer and assessment information
  • a child not already reflected on the assessment (including any active reverse Cuba case) see Applications for a child support assessment
  • a change in care that needs to be updated on their notional assessment see Notional assessment relating to child support agreements
  • a change in care due to the customers reconciling, see Suspension of child support liability on reconciliation
  • a disputed care change - where a court order, parenting plan or written care agreement applies to the care of a child but it is not being followed and the person with reduced care is trying to have it complied with, see Customer Referral Guidelines

6

Multiple care change notifications + Read more ...

Where a parent delays in notifying Child Support or Centrelink of changes in care, they may report multiple changes at once.

Treat each notification as a separate care change using the care period as 12 months from the date of the event. Calculate the care percentage for the first event as though the subsequent event had not occurred. Repeat this process for each subsequent event.

See Child Support date of effect for more information about when a new care percentage will be used in the assessment.

A change in care notified more than 28 days after the event may result in the child support assessment for that child being suspended or terminated.

See the Resources page for examples of multiple care change notifications.

Gather information and determine change in care details

Table 2

Step

Action

1

Determine likely date of event + Read more ...

The term 'likely' means information gathered from the initial customer only. Identify the date of event by asking the customer what circumstances led to the change in care. When identifying the date of event, consider whether the care that is actually occurring for the child has changed.

Do not pend a care change if a determination relating to this care event has already been made (original care decision, objection or appeal). Provide customers with options to seek review of the decision(s).

The date of event is either:

  • the date both customers confirm the actual care change occurred
  • a date determined by Child Support based on the information and evidence provided by both customers
  • the date customers made a new care arrangement for example the made on date for a new court order

Note: the date of event used to record the care change will be tentative until the other customer has been contacted.

Questions that may assist in determining the likely date of event include:

  • which child does the change relate to
  • what date did the care/pattern of care change
  • what caused the change in care
  • was the care change discussed with the other parent
  • is the change intended to be permanent
  • if the care change is temporary, what is the period of the change

If a one-off care event is missed that is likely to be made up/the care is likely to resume to the standard pattern, this means a care change may not have occurred.

If unable to determine the date of event, see Step 2 in the Investigate change in care table in Investigation of change in child support care (post 1 July 2010).

2

Determine likely care period + Read more ...

When a customer notifies Child Support of a change in care, determine the likely care period. A care period is generally the 12 month period starting from the date of event.

3

Calculate care period + Read more ...

A care period is generally the 12 month period starting from the date of event. In some circumstances, a shorter or longer care period may be appropriate, however judge each case on its own merits. If determining a care period other than 12 months, seek technical support. See Technical support in Child Support.

Care periods longer than 12 months

A care period of more than 12 months may be appropriate where the care arrangement follows a cycle over a period greater than 12 months, for example every 2 years.

See Resources page for examples of care periods longer than 12 months.

Care periods shorter than 12 months

A care period of less than 12 months might be appropriate where the pattern of care will gradually change over definable periods in a specific and measurable way, for example one parent is to increase their care by one day per week every 3 months over the upcoming period.

A shorter care period may also be appropriate where a carer has unexpectedly gained 100% care of a child for a temporary period (generally 4 weeks minimum). In these situations, the Registrar will determine the care over a short care period related to the unexpected circumstance and make a one-off block of 100% care determination.

See Resources page for examples of care periods shorter than 12 months, and References page in Change in child support care levels (post 1 July 2010) for further information.

Note: tell the customer Child Support will not give effect to the future events until they notify the change has occurred. If neither customer advises of the subsequent change, the existing percentage of care will continue.

4

Determine likely percentage of care + Read more ...

The term 'likely' means information gathered from the initial customer only. Care is expressed as a percentage of care (generally based on a number of nights) that a customer is likely to have during the care period as shown in the care percentage table.

See the Resources page for the Care percentage table.

5

Establish pattern of care + Read more ...

Ask the customer to provide the pattern of care based on what they actually expect to occur during the 12 month care period. In most cases, care is determined by the actual care each parent has. Where a pattern has been established in the past and the Service Officer is satisfied that it will continue in the future, use that pattern to determine the likely percentage of care for the care period.

If the customer:

  • requests that care be calculated in a form other than nights, see Step 2 in the Calculate and determine care percentage table in Child support care outcomes and decision making (post 1 July 2010)
  • has unexpectedly gained 100% care of a child for a temporary period (generally 4 weeks minimum), see Step 3 in the Calculate and determine care percentage table in Child support care outcomes and decision making (post 1 July 2010)
  • notifies Child Support within a reasonable time and informs that the level of care for the other parent (who has at least regular care) has fallen below 14% despite the child being made available to them, see Step 7 in the Calculate and determine care percentage table in Child support care outcomes and decision making (post 1 July 2010)

If a customer wants to estimate future or possible care changes themselves, refer them to the Care Estimator available on servicesaustralia.gov.au.

Questions that may assist in determining the likely percentage of care include:

  • have you and the other parent established a pattern of care for the child
  • how many nights do you and the other parent expect to care for your child over the 12 month care period from the date the care changed
  • how did you calculate the expected care (for example, is there a pattern of care)
  • do you or the other parent provide extensive care during the day but little or no overnight care? See Step 2 in the Calculate and determine care percentage table in Child support care outcomes and decision making (post 1 July 2010) - Care other than in nights
  • what is the care based on currently
  • are there any court orders, parenting plans, or written agreements for a different amount of care and what was the commencement date of that arrangement
  • are either you or the other parent trying to enforce this
  • is the other parent likely to agree that these are the new care arrangements

If there is a written care arrangement to which the parents are no longer adhering. Establish whether the parent with the reduced care is or has taken reasonable action to comply with the care arrangement. If so, see Customer Referral Guidelines.

6

Care calculator + Read more ...

The care calculator must be used to calculate care in all instances, except where the pattern of care being notified results in either:

  • a care percentage of 100% or 0%
  • the number of nights as 365 or 0

and if so, go to Step 8.

From discussions with the customer, input the pattern of care or the number of nights the customer will be caring for the child. In the event the calculation is for care using the number of hours, calculate the number of nights first.

7

Percentage of care (special rounding rules) + Read more ...

Cuba will automatically calculate the percentage of care used in the assessment when the number of nights care has been entered into the Care window.

There may be occasions when Service Officers need to manually calculate the percentage of care, or explain how the figures were calculated, as percentages of care calculations have special rounding rules.

Percentage of care = (number of nights / 365) x 100

To calculate a percentage of care the following special rounding rules must be applied (to ensure the total equals 100%). If the percentage of care is:

  • below 50%, round the number down to the next whole percentage
  • 50% or more, round the number up to the next whole percentage

Where each customer has a care percentage of 50%, record the payee as having 50% care, see the Care Cuba Process Help. Do not record this as a number of nights. Cuba will automatically change this to show the payee has 51% care and the other party has 49% care. The Care Reported as 50% checkbox will be automatically set by Cuba and a care percentage of 50% transferred to Centrelink.

Note: covering letters to customers who share 50% split care will indicate both parties have 50% care of the child, however the assessment notice will show 51% or 49%. This has no impact on the assessed rate as the same cost percentage is used.

See the Resources page for examples of percentage of care (special rounding rules).

8

Child Support date of effect rules + Read more ...

When Child Support or Centrelink are advised of a care change and determine a new care percentage, the actual Date of Event (DOE) and the date of notification (DON) are recorded. Child Support and Centrelink use these dates to determine when the new care determination affects the child support assessment and FTB eligibility. Go to Step 12 for further details on FTB (Centrelink) date of effect rules.

For information about the effect of multiple care determinations or care advised out of order go to Step 9.

Care advised within 28 days

If Child Support or Centrelink is notified within 28 days of a care change, the date of effect will be the date the care changed for both Child Support and Centrelink purposes.

Care advised outside of 28 days

If a care change occurred on or after 1 July 2018, or before 1 July 2018 and Child Support or Centrelink is notified on or after 30 December 2018, the new care determination will be used in the assessment from the date:

  • the care changed, for the person whose care percentage decreases
  • of notification, for the person whose care percentage increases

If a care change occurs before 1 July 2018 and we are notified before 30 December 2018, the care change will take effect in the assessment from the date we were notified.

Exceptions

There are 4 exceptions to the above date of effect rules when care is notified outside of 28 days:

  • Interim care determinations – when care changes within an interim care period a new care determination cannot take effect until after the interim care period ends. If a care determination would otherwise take effect within the interim period, it will take effect from the day after the interim care period ends. Different rules apply to the first care change notified after the end of an interim period but before the end of the maximum interim period
  • Below regular care determinations – when a below regular care determination is made and the Registrar was advised within a reasonable period of time, the care determination takes effect from either:
    • the date a parent ceased a pattern of care, or
    • the date a pattern of care that was not established is first used in the assessment
  • WA Ex-nuptial children – if the change occurred before 1 July 2018 and the notification date is outside 28 days and prior to 15 May 2019 the effective date for all carers is the date of notification
  • WA Ex-nuptial children – if the change was late notified after 14 May 2019, the system must be enhanced to treat these customers correctly. Late care change
  • Reconciliation- when the care of either a relevant dependent child or a Claimed For child is updated because an assessment is suspended upon reconciliation or reinstated due to separation see Suspension of child support liability on reconciliation

Terminating events

When a change in care causes the assessment to end (terminating event), the assessment will end from the date the care changed.

Note: prior to 1 July 2018 a child moving from the care of 1 party to another was not a terminating event. If the care was notified:

  • within 28 days the assessment was amended to reflect the new care percentage from the date of effect
  • after more than 28 days the assessment was amended to give effect to the new care percentage from the date of notification

See the Resources page for scenarios and examples to determine date of effect.

9

Multiple care determinations or care determinations made out of order + Read more ...

Where multiple care determinations are finalised on the same day or if a care determination is accepted out of order, that is, a care determination for a later care event has already been accepted, the care date of effect rules are applied in the following ways.

Care advised outside of 28 days

If a care change occurs on or after 1 July 2018, or occurs before 1 July 2018 and is notified on or after 30 December 2018 (or 15 May 2019 for WA ex nup children), the new care percentage will be compared against the care used in the assessment on the day the care changed.

If the care percentage has:

  • decreased, it will be used from the date of event of the care change until the day before the next care percentage, relating to a later care change, for that parent/carer takes effect
  • increased, it will be used from the date of notification unless the date of notification is after the effective date of a subsequent care record for that parent/carer. If the date of notification is after the effective date of a care record, relating to a later care change, for that parent/carer the care percentage will not be used in the assessment

Each time a care record/event is finalised/accepted Cuba will determine its effect on the assessment by considering the accepted care determinations in order of the date of the care event (oldest to youngest), regardless of the order they were notified.

10

Care suspension period + Read more ...

A care suspension period will apply if we are advised of a change in care outside of 28 days but within 26 weeks of the care change and as a result of the care change:

  • all carers cease to be eligible carers, that is their care reduces below 35%; and
  • a person who wasn’t an eligible carer would become an eligible carer except for the new date of effect rules

A suspension period will always apply retrospectively. The start date of the suspension period will be the date the care changed and the end date will be the day before we are notified of the care change.

During a care suspension, there is no assessment in force for the child and the child will not be taken into account in the assessment on either:

  • the case with the suspension, that is, if there are multiple children on the case and the suspension does not apply to all children
  • any other case, that is, a carer has multiple cases

During a suspension period a customer will not be considered to be taking Maintenance Action for FTB purposes, by having a child support case. If a customer requests further information about the impact of the care change on their FTB entitlement, transfer them to Centrelink.

Note: a suspension period will not apply to care changes that occur before 1 July 2018 which are notified prior to 30 December 2018 (or 15 May 2019 for WA ex nup children).

If a care change causes a care suspension period to be applied to a child’s assessment check the case for:

  • Prescribed non agency payments, see Non Agency Payments (NAPs) (CS) and
  • An application to extend a child support assessment past a child’s 18th birthday, see Eighteen year old children extending a child support assessment

See the Resources page for example- care suspension period.

11

Care terminating event + Read more ...

A care terminating event will occur if the agency is notified of a care change more than 26 weeks after the date of event, and as a result of the care change:

  • all eligible carers cease to be eligible carers i.e. their care reduces below 35%; and
  • a person who wasn’t an eligible carer would become an eligible carer except for the date of effect rules effective from 1 July 2018

Application for assessment because of a care terminating event

If a parent/carer’s assessment is likely to end due to a care terminating event ask if they would like to apply today for a new child support assessment, to be used in case their care change causes their assessment for a child/ren to end. Advise the parent/carer that if they do not make an application today and their care change causes their assessment to end, the assessment can only be restarted from the day we receive an application for assessment.

If the parent/carer does wish to make an application for a child support assessment, document their response clearly in the same notepad/Pluto process that records the care notification. The restart cannot be processed until the care determination is finalised.

Application to extend the assessment past a child’s eighteenth birthday

Another application to extend the assessment when a child turns eighteen may also need to be taken see Eighteen year old children extending a child support assessment.

See the Resources page for scenarios- care terminating event.

12

Centrelink date of effect + Read more ...

For Centrelink, most care changes are applied for FTB purposes from the date of event, regardless of when Centrelink is notified of the care change. Determine and record the exact date the care changed as this information may be used by Centrelink in determining a customer’s FTB entitlement. If a customer requests further information about the impact of the care change on their FTB entitlement, transfer them to Centrelink

Data exchange

If Centrelink records the correct date of event for the care change and the correct date of notification when applying care decisions, the Child Support system will automatically determine and apply the decision correctly under child support law.

Similarly, the Centrelink system will automatically determine and apply the correct date of effect under Family Assistance law as long as the correct date of event of the change and date of notification are recorded by Child Support.

See Family Tax Benefit (FTB) dates of effect for further details.

13

Gather information from notifying customer + Read more ...

Ensure all relevant information has been gathered from the notifying customer to determine the likely event date, care period, percentage of care, date of effect and type of care change.

If either customer receives FTB for the child, the care decision will be sent to Centrelink and may affect any Centrelink benefits or payments they receive.

Explain that:

  • the care decision made by Child Support will be sent to Centrelink
  • the customer should ensure their Centrelink relationship and income details are correct to reduce the likelihood of incorrect Centrelink benefits or payments occurring when the care decision is transferred
  • the date of effect for FTB purposes may be different to the date the amount of Child Support payable will change
  • Centrelink will advise them (in writing) of any impact on their Centrelink entitlements

If the customer requires additional information, or wants to update their Centrelink relationship or income details, offer to cold transfer them to Centrelink at the end of the discussion.

Document all conversations (including macro documents), contact attempts and information gathered in the Care window notepad, see Documenting Child Support information.

14

Notifying customer indicates the other party is ‘likely’ to disagree with the reported change + Read more ...

If the notifying customer advises that the other party is ‘likely’ to disagree with the change of care, clarify which facts of the reported change the other party is ‘likely’ to disagree with, for example:

  • the date of event
  • the details of the care such as number of nights, hours of care or pattern of care

Continue to use the Change in care macro to guide and document the conversation.

Note: a care change is only disagreed if the parties disagree about the facts of the care change. A customer advising that the other party is likely to be unhappy with the change in care does not mean that party will disagree with the facts of the change.

7

Pend care record + Read more ...

Pend the reported care change in Cuba with the relevant 'Type' and a status of 'Pending'. See Care Cuba Process Help. This:

  • will generate a Pending Care Decision intray
  • may send an indicator to Centrelink advising a change to the current care for that child has been pended.

Note: use the Change in Care macro to generate the document for Cuba and Customer First (where dual-system processing is required).

If the notifying customer has advised that the other party is 'likely' to disagree with the information provided, see Customer referral guidelines for Child Support staff to determine if an Integrated Care referral is appropriate.

8

Correcting pending care details + Read more ...

If the wrong care type, dates or care percentage, have been entered. Amend the pending care record to reflect the correct care details. Ensure the correct details are recorded for the type of care, notification date and date of event. Seek technical support, see Technical support in Child Support.

See Step 1 in Investigate change in care table in Investigation of change in child support care (post 1 July 2010).

Do not select the care type CSA Decision, Oral Agreement, Interim Decision or court Ordered.
