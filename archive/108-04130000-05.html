This page contains information on the documentation required for real estate valuations.

Real Estate valuations

Suburban and rural properties

This table describes information required for real estate valuations.

Item

Description

1

Additional information on title documents + Read more ...

A title document may have more than one parcel of land on it.

For most suburban real estate the certificate of title will usually be made up of one parcel of land or lot for example, 41 Smith St, Jonesville has a certificate of title of DP126543 lot 12.

For rural properties there may be one or more parcels of land on a title document. It is common for rural title documents to include more than one lot or parcel of land. Rural land can be held in single titles that include multiple lots, crown allotments, parcels, etc. These may be referred to as parent titles and are not deemed multiple title properties as they are on one title deed or certificate of title. To create separate title documents for each of these lots, that would allow them to be sold off, involves additional costs to the owner of the land.

Where the customer owns their land via a single title document, even where there are multiple lots included, the whole of the land on that title document may be exempted under the extended land use test. This applies even if the customer is able to sell off one of the lots or parcels by subdividing and having to seek approval from the land titles office or local council.

The approved valuer will value real estate as one single property where there is one title document, even if there are multiple lots within that title.

Valuation requirements

When a customer is assessed as being eligible for the extended curtilage provisions and their total property is made up of one title, a valuation will not be submitted for the property.

A valuation will only be submitted to determine the amount of curtilage to be exempted in cases where:

  • extended curtilage provision will apply but the customer's property is made up of multiple titles, or
  • where the property is greater than 2 hectares in size and the customer is only entitled to a 2 hectare exemption under the private land use test

If a valuation is required, go to Step 2.

2

Documentation for valuation purposes + Read more ...

When a valuation is required the valuer needs detailed title information to carry out the valuation. This should be via a copy of the title document itself. The latest Valuer General Office's Notice of Valuation could be used if the title document is not available (this office may have a different title from state to state).

In the first instance, the customer should be requested to provide this information. This may involve the customer having to contact their bank or solicitor if they do not hold title documents personally.

Generally the council rates notice does not provide enough information for the valuer to make a valuation on multiple title cases. The latest annual council rates notice may be acceptable if this provides sufficient information about the title details.

Note: the council rates notice may help with title details. Property values shown on council rates notices:

  • are used to calculate rates, fire and ambulance services, or other taxes
  • do not always indicate the current market value of a property

A current market valuation shows how much a property might sell for in the current market.

Only in cases where a customer is genuinely not able to provide the relevant information should a title search be requested.
