Examples of multiple changes reported at the same time

Table 2: This table describes examples of when a parent delays in notifying Child Support or Centrelink of changes in care. These multiple changes can be reported at the same time.

Item

Example

1

On 1 July 2016, Marge notifies of 2 changes in care for a child that occurred the previous year:

  • 1 August 2015, Marge started having 2 nights care per fortnight, and
  • 1 December 2015, Marge started having 4 nights care per fortnight

The care percentage for the first event (1 August 2015) is calculated over 12 months from that event date as though the second event had not occurred. That is, 2 nights per fortnight is 14% care (52 nights over 12 months). The care percentage for the second event (1 December 2015) is calculated over 12 months from that event date. That is, 4 nights per fortnight is 28% care (104 nights over 12 months). As both decisions were notified outside of 28 days, only the later change affects the assessment from the date of notification 1 July 2016.

2

On 15 June 2016 Child Support was notified of a care change for the child that occurred on 1 May 2016. Under the new arrangement, each parent will have 50% care. The care period begins on 1 May 2016 for both Child Support and Centrelink. As the date of notification was more than 28 days after the date the event changed, the child support assessment is amended from 15 June 2016 (date of notification), however the amendment to FTB takes effect from the date the care changed (date of event) being 1 May 2016.

Where care changes occur close together and are notified within 28 days of the first event, or the below regular care rules apply, there is a greater retrospective effect as more than one care change may affect the child support assessment.

3

John’s parent contacts Child Support on 5 September 2016 to advise on:

  • 12 August 2016 the care provided for child John increased to 60%
  • 31 August 2016 the care provided for child John increased to 75%

As both changes were notified within 28 days of the change occurring, the following will occur:

  • the first care period begins on 12 August 2016, being the first date of event. The care percentage is calculated as 60%. The care period is calculated as though it is going to run for a full 12 months, regardless of the second care change
  • the second care period begins on 31 August 2016, which is the second date of event. The care percentage is calculated as 75%
  • the child support assessment will be changed from 12 August 2016 to reflect 60% care and amended again from 31 August 2016 to reflect 75% care. As both decisions were notified within 28 days, both changes affect the assessment from the respective dates of event. The customers’ FTB payments will be calculated using the same care percentages for the same periods

Care period 12 months and over

Table 3: This table describes an example of a care period 12 months and over.

Item

Example

1

Calculate care period longer than 12 months example

John and Karen have a parenting plan that provides John with care of their child, Laura, every Saturday night, Fathers' Day, and half of each of the school holiday periods except Christmas. John and Karen alternate care of Laura over Christmas such that Laura stays with John for all of the Christmas school holidays one year and then with Karen all of the Christmas school holidays the next year. Depending on which parent has care of Laura during the Christmas school holidays, both John and Karen’s care percentage would change from one year to the next if 12 month care period was used. In this case, it would be appropriate to consider a care period of 24 months to properly account for the alternating pattern of care for the Christmas school holidays.

Care period shorter than 12 months

Table 4: This table describes an example of a care period shorter than 12 months.

Item

Example

1

Calculate care period shorter than 12 months example

Marcia and Eric have one child, Emerson. There is no care arrangement in place for Emerson, however, until recently Marcia had 100% care of Emerson. Marcia’s circumstances changed such that Marcia is no longer able to care for Emerson full time and so has asked Eric if they could look after Emerson until Marcia is able to resume caring responsibilities. Eric agreed but cannot commit permanently due to travel commitments for work.

Marcia and Eric come to an informal arrangement that Marcia will look after Emerson one night per week for the next 2 months, and then will increase care of Emerson by one night per week every 2 months until full time care is resumed.

Eric calls Child Support to advise of the new arrangement. As the parents have agreed on the specific arrangements to increase Marcia’s care in a staggered but foreseeable way over a definable period, it would be appropriate to use a care period of 2 months for each of the periods that Marcia is due to increase the care of Emerson. This ensures the care percentage accurately reflects the care that each parent is providing during each 2 month period.

Calculate care period shorter than 12 months due to one-off block of 100% care example

Applying special rounding rules examples

Table 6: This table describes two examples of applying the percentage of care (special rounding rules).

Item

Example

1

Mahlee has 134 nights care per year. Mahlee's percentage of care is (134/365) x 100 = 36.71%. This is less than 50% and is therefore rounded down to 36%.

2

Tristan has 231 nights care per year. Tristan’s percentage of care is (231/365) x 100 = 63.28%. This is greater than 50% and is therefore rounded up to 64%.

Determining Child Support date of effect for a change in care

Table 7: This table provides scenarios and examples to determine the date of effect for change in care.

Item

Scenarios and Examples

1

Scenario

Child moves from 100% care of one parent to 100% care of the other parent. This is a change of care not a terminating event, as the child is in the care of a party to the assessment. The change will take effect from the date Child Support was notified of the change unless notified within 28 days of the change.

Example

Jill is Fran and Dave's child. Jill moved from Fran’s 100% care to Dave’s 100% care on 2 February 2016. Fran and Dave do not have a written care arrangement for Jill. Fran advised Child Support of this change in care on 11 July 2016. This is a change in care and will take effect from 11 July 2016, the date of notification.

2

Scenario

Child moves from 60% care of their parent to 100% care of grandparent. The grandparent was already a party to the assessment as previously had 40% care and had applied for child support. This is a change of care not a terminating event as the child is in the care of a party to the assessment. The change will take effect from the date Child Support was notified of the change unless notified within 28 days of the change.

Example

Child Tommy moved from Kate’s (parent) 60% care to Suzie’s (grandparent) 100% care on 3 March 2016. Prior to the change in care Tommy was in Suzie’s 40% care. Kate advised Child Support of the care change on 19 April 2016. This is a change in care and will take effect from 19 April 2016, the date of notification.

3

Scenario

Both parents equally share the care of one child (50% care each). The child then moves into 100% care of a relative, who is not a party to the current assessment. This is a terminating event for the current assessment as both parents cease to be eligible carers as their percentage of care has fallen to 0%. When a terminating event occurs, the assessment must change from the date of event even if notified after 28 days.

Example

Peter moves from the shared care (50% each) of Susan and Steven (parents) to non-parent carer Nicole’s 100% care on 19 December 2015. Steven advises Child Support of the care change on 29 March 2016. As Nicole was not a party to the assessment and both parents now have a care percentage of 0%, this is a terminating event. The terminating event takes effect from 19 December 2015, being the date of event. See WA Ex-nuptial child support cases - Terminating events (Stage 2) notified before 15 May 2019. If Nicole wishes to claim child support from the parents for Peter, a new application must be made.
