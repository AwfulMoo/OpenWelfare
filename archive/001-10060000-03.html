Identifying voluntary unemployment or dismissal due to misconduct

Table 1

Step

Action

1

Check reasons for unemployment + Read more ...

If a job seeker claiming or receiving income support payment becomes unemployed, the reasons for separation from employment must be established. This information may be provided via:

  • an Employment Separation Certificate (SU1)
  • Single Touch Payroll (STP) information on the STP Employer Wage Items (EMGI) screen in Process Direct, or
  • the job seeker's statement at new claim

Review the reason for unemployment. Where both the job seeker's and employer's statements indicate unemployment was not voluntary or due to misconduct, a compliance investigation does not need to be generated. For example:

  • Shortage of work
  • Unsuitability for this type of work
  • End of seasonal contract
  • Redundancy

If the employer has indicated any of the following reasons for unemployment, more information may be required to identify if compliance action should be generated:

  • Unsatisfactory work performance
  • Misconduct as an employee
  • Employee ceasing work voluntarily
  • The employer has not indicated the reason for separation
  • Other

If:

  • the job seeker and employer statements differ or it is unclear if the unemployment was voluntary or due to misconduct, go to Step 2
  • the job seeker and employer statements both support that unemployment was voluntary or due to misconduct, go to Step 3
  • both the job seeker's and employer's statements indicate unemployment was not voluntary or due to misconduct, do not generate a failure. Procedure ends here

2

Clarify reasons for unemployment + Read more ...

A compliance investigation is only generated where it is likely that the job seeker is unemployed due to a voluntary act or dismissed due to misconduct.

Advise the job seeker further information is required to establish their reasons for unemployment. Explain that income support payments for job seekers are intended to provide support to those who are unable to support themselves. Leaving suitable work voluntarily or due to misconduct can affect when income support payments can be paid.

Annotate the Progress of Claim DOC, where required, with the jobseeker's statement outlining their reasons for leaving work. Include any additional information that the job seeker has been asked to provide to support their statement and the due date. Check the reason for unemployment on the SU1, the EMGI screen in Process Direct or the job seeker statement.

Further contact with an employer is required where:

  • the employer has provided additional information or free text that contradicts the reason for separation selected on the SU1
  • the information recorded via STP on the EMGI screen in Process Direct, and within the compliance investigation workflow differs from the discussion with the job seeker and gives rise to considerable doubt as to the nature of separation, or contradicts statements made by the employer
  • 'unsatisfactory work performance' is recorded but there is no further explanation as to what this means
  • 'other' reason is recorded, however the SU1 does not include adequate information to establish the reason for separation
  • the employer did not indicate a reason for separation

If contact with the employer is successful, or is not required:

  • Annotate the Progress of Claim DOC, where required, with any further information collected that clarifies the reasons for unemployment
    • If satisfied the reason for separation was not a voluntary act or due to misconduct, a compliance investigation does not need to be generated. The decision to not generate a compliance event should be recorded on the Progress of Claim DOC. Procedure ends here
    • If unemployment was due to a voluntary action or misconduct, advise the employer that a Service Officer may need to contact for more information. Identify the best contact person for this contact. Go to Step 3

If contact with the employer is required but was not successful:

  • A compliance investigation is required. For customers:
    • claiming a participation payment, see Step 1 in Table 2
    • currently receiving a participation payment, see Step 1 in Table 3

Reason for unemployment due to voluntary act or misconduct + Read more ...

If the job seeker is:

  • claiming a participation payment, go to Step 4
  • receiving a participation payment, go to Step 7

4

Check if new claim will be rejected + Read more ...

Consider the information provided by the job seeker in the claim and if there are any other reasons why the job seeker will not be eligible for payment.

Based on information provided by the job seeker, is the new claim to be rejected?

  • Yes, go to Step 5
  • No, go to Step 6

5

New claim will be rejected + Read more ...

For compliance action to be applied, a participation payment must be payable before it can be affected by a non-payment or preclusion period. If the claim for payment does not meet eligibility criteria, a compliance investigation does not need to be generated.

Code a Display on Access DOC to advise that if the claim had not been rejected, compliance investigation would have been required. Include in the DOC:

  • date of unemployment
  • date the SU1 was scanned (where applicable)
  • whether unemployment ceased as a result of voluntary unemployment or unemployment due to misconduct as a worker, and
  • any other relevant details

This Display on Access DOC is to have an expiry date equal to the expected end of the potential penalty period end date. See Requested documentation not provided resulting in rejection of new claim for more information.

Procedure ends here.

6

Check if the potential penalty period has been self-served + Read more ...

The job seeker has fully self-served the potential penalty period if the end date of the potential penalty period is before the potential claim start date. This will depend on whether the job seeker received relocation assistance for the job, and the job seeker compliance system the job seeker is managed under at the time the compliance action is generated.

The potential penalty period will be fully self-served for a job seeker who is managed:

  • under the Targeted Compliance Framework, if the date of unemployment is more than:
    • 4 weeks before the potential claim start date, or
    • 6 weeks before the potential claim start date where the job seeker was paid relocation assistance
  • under the CDP Job Seeker Compliance Framework, if the date of unemployment is more than:
    • 8 weeks before the potential claim start date, or
    • 12 weeks before the potential claim start date where the job seeker was paid relocation assistance

Has the job seeker fully self-served the potential penalty period?

  • Yes, a compliance investigation does not need to be generated. Annotate the Progress of Claim DOC, clearly documenting the reasons why a compliance investigation is not being generated and continue with normal claim processing. Procedure ends here
  • No, go to Step 7

7

Check for waiting periods that may apply + Read more ...

Identify other waiting periods or preclusion periods that will extend beyond the potential penalty period end date, including:

  • Liquid Assets Waiting Period (LAWP)
  • Income Maintenance Period (IMP)
  • Seasonal Work Preclusion Period (SWPP), or
  • Newly Arrived Residence Waiting Period (NARWP)

If the job seeker has moved or plans to move to a new location that is subject to Moving to an Area of Lower Employment Prospects (MALEP) provisions, and has left employment voluntarily or due to misconduct, both MALEP and a compliance investigation must be generated. See MALEP employment related exclusion period.

Do not undertake any MALEP assessments. The Department of Social Services (DSS) have paused all Moving to an Area of Lower Employment Prospects (MALEP) assessments.

Note: the potential penalty period end date will depend on the job seeker compliance system the job seeker will be managed under and whether relocation assistance was paid.

Will the job seeker serve a waiting period that extends beyond the potential penalty period end date?

  • Yes, go to Step 8
  • No:
    • job seeker claiming a participation payment, see Step 1 in Table 2
    • job seeker receiving a participation payment, see Step 1 in Table 3

8

The job seeker will be required to serve another waiting period that is continuous and will end after the potential penalty period + Read more ...

For job seekers not currently receiving a participation payment, compliance action is not to be generated if concurrent waiting periods will extend beyond the potential penalty period end date.

The Service Officer is required to:

  • record in the Progress of Claim DOC the reason for not generating compliance action (free text field limited to 75 characters in Process Direct), and
  • record a Display on Access DOC in Customer First detailing why compliance action was not generated and that if the customer's payment reclaims before the end of any potential penalty period, consider generating compliance action before any payment is issued. This Display on Access DOC is to have an expiry date equal to the expected end of concurrent preclusion period(s)

If a waiting period review results in payment starting before the end of a potential penalty period, a compliance investigation must be (re)generated.

Continue with normal processing. Procedure ends here.

8

E488PB – Provider not known/Provider not linked error when generating failure at new claim + Read more ...

An error 'E488PB – Provider not known / Provider not linked' may occur in Process Direct when attempting to generate a failure as part of the new claim process.

This error may occur when the job seeker has:

  • not been referred to a provider, or
  • been referred to a provider but there is a delay in the system processing the referral

This error affects failures generated when a job seeker is claiming JobSeeker payment, Youth Allowance – Job Seeker, Special Benefit – Nominated Visa Holders and Parenting Payment Single with compulsory requirements.

Before attempting to generate the failure, confirm correct action has been taken to refer the job seeker to a provider, see Participation Interview.

If the appropriate action has been taken to refer the job seeker and the error is preventing generation of the failure, the claim should be assessed without investigating the failure. Service Officers must annotate the progress of claim DOC advising E488PB error is preventing failure generation.

Do not hold claims pending resolution of this problem.

Procedure ends here.

Investigating unemployment due to voluntary act

Table 5

Item

Action

1

Primary focus of the compliance investigation + Read more ...

The compliance investigation focuses on establishing the facts relevant to the compliance event under consideration. This will enable an evidence-based decision in line with the legislative framework and policy intent.

When investigating whether a job seeker voluntarily left suitable employment without a reasonable excuse, relevant matters that must be considered include:

  • the circumstances that led the job seeker to leave their employment
  • whether the employment was suitable work, and
  • whether there were any factors that made it reasonable for the job seeker to leave employment voluntarily

2

Starting point for the investigation + Read more ...

The Service Officer records the compliance investigation and decision using the Participation Compliance workflow in Process Direct.

To access the workflow:

  • select Customer Summary from the Process Direct Landing page
  • key the customer's CRN, select Go
  • key 'BIPCW' in the Super Key field, select [Enter]
  • the Participation Compliance Hub will display
  • select Compliance Investigation button from the Outstanding compliance actions table for the event date of the compliance action

At the beginning of the investigation, a clear explanation of the non-compliance event that is being investigated and the specific matters that need to be considered to reach a decision on the potential penalty must be discussed with the job seeker.

During the course of the conversation, the expectations that job seekers remain in suitable work should be reinforced.

The Service Officer must keep the conversations focused to ensure all of the key facts and evidence to support these are collected.

The information recorded when the compliance investigation was generated should be used to initiate the discussion. This may include:

  • information recorded by the Service Officer in the workflow when generating the compliance investigation including the job seeker's explanation for leaving employment and any additional contact with employer or information that has been reported by the provider
  • Employment Separation Certificate (SU1) - the nature of the employment, type of work being offered/performed and where the job seeker ceased work voluntarily, consider how long the job seeker had been working for the company
  • STP information recorded on the STP Employer Wage Items (EMGI) screen within Process Direct
  • provider recorded information, if applicable
  • notes in the Progress of Claim DOC

3

Event date + Read more ...

The event date is generally the date last worked plus 1 day.

If the Service Officer has established that the failure was not committed on the event date that has been recorded, the event date on the key information page must be changed to reflect the correct event date.

See the Resources page for more information. All queries about the event date must be directed to an SO5 or the Level 2 Policy Helpdesk

Note: the Participation Compliance workflow event date must be changed prior to the decision page being completed. If the decision page has been completed, any subsequent change of event date on a decision to apply an unemployment failure will result in an incorrect penalty period being applied.

If the decision page has already been completed when an incorrect event date is identified, the workflow must be rejected and a new failure investigation must be generated with the correct event date.

For more information, see Rejecting a compliance action.

4

Job seeker's explanation of reasons for voluntarily ceasing employment + Read more ...

During the investigation, the job seeker must be given opportunity to respond to any adverse claims that have been made against them and disclose any further information they would like considered.

As a result of this discussion the Service Officer should record:

  • employer details including company name and contact details (where they are not already recorded in the generate compliance workflow)
  • the nature of the employment and how long they had been working for the company
  • the job seeker's explanation for leaving employment voluntarily
  • any efforts made by the job seeker to retain their employment
  • any other relevant information provided by the job seeker

Job seeker does not make supplementary contact

If the job seeker does not contact to provide further evidence after being given an opportunity to provide evidence, the Service Officer (UNPP or WNPPD) can make a decision based on the available evidence.

5

Voluntary actions + Read more ...

Voluntary unemployment refers to a situation where a job seeker becomes unemployed as a result of a voluntary action, which is, leaving employment where their actions are not considered reasonable.

Voluntary unemployment does not include leaving work as a result of:

  • an employment contract ending
  • no further work available (for example, factory closures or a voluntary redundancy is accepted), or
  • being within 6 weeks of the date of confinement but not having access to Maternity Leave

6

Suitability of work + Read more ...

Compliance action only applies if the work was suitable for the job seeker.

Additional considerations are required to assess suitability of work for job seekers who have a partial work capacity or are a principal carer parent.

PST-skilled Service Officers must reject unemployment failures if:

  • the job is more than 15 hours per week, and
  • the job seeker:
    • has a partial capacity to work, or
    • is a principal carer parent

For further information on suitability of work, see Suitable/unsuitable work.

If the voluntary unemployment was reported by a provider, they would have considered suitability before reporting the failure to Services Australia. Service Officers should assume the work was suitable for the job seeker based on the provider report unless they identify information during the investigation that indicates the work was unsuitable and/or this requires further investigation.

7

Considerations when assessing if job seeker has reasonable excuse for leaving suitable work + Read more ...

If work was considered suitable and the job seeker has left voluntarily, consider their reasons for leaving. If they have a reasonable excuse, a penalty will not be applied.

All factors that significantly and directly impacted the decision to leave work are relevant matters for consideration. These include, but are not limited to:

  • age
  • mobility
  • qualifications
  • language proficiency
  • stability of accommodation
  • personal issues
  • behavioural issues
  • work history
  • geographical location
  • family and caring responsibilities

Leaving work voluntarily and accessing or increasing the reliance on a participation payment is a serious consideration. It is generally not reasonable for a job seeker to leave work for factors within their control.

For these reasons, the assessment of whether the voluntary unemployment was reasonable is usually related to whether the work was untenable or not, that is, the work had become unsuitable or it was unreasonable to remain in that job due to work related factors.

Further considerations to determine if the job seeker left work voluntarily may include:

  • any efforts made by the job seeker to retain their employment, for example, did they discuss issues they were experiencing with management and if so what action was taken
  • did they notify anyone of their decision to leave employment (include names, position and contact details)

Factors related to personal reasons outside of the job that do not make the work unreasonable include:

  • wishing to relocate to be near children, a new partner or re-join an ex-partner
  • wishing to relocate to a more agreeable climate or location, or
  • wanting to take up a sporting commitment or relocate pending retirement

Such reasons are not generally regarded as being reasonable for leaving work. A person would usually be expected to obtain new work to accommodate their external interests without having to rely on a participation payment.

Similarly, leaving work because the work or contract was going to be ceasing in the near future is not regarded as reasonable where the job seeker did not have another job to go to.

For more information, see Assessing reasonable excuse for non-compliance with mutual obligation requirements.

8

Contacting employers for further evidence + Read more ...

Considering any previous contact by another Service Officer, contact with the employer may be required to establish the facts. This applies particularly when extra evidence from the job seeker contradicts statements made by the employer.

The Service Officer should attempt to contact the employer by phone. This ensures a timely investigation and provides opportunity for matters raised to be clarified with additional questions if required.

Further contact with an employer may be required:

  • where no reason for separation was established before generating compliance action
  • to clarify inconsistencies in the evidence gathered

If phone contact cannot be established with the employer, and evidence is likely to have an impact on the outcome of the decision, further evidence should be requested from the job seeker.

9

Job seekers who regain their job + Read more ...

If during an investigation, it is identified that:

  • the job seeker has voluntarily left suitable employment, without reasonable excuse, and has subsequently recommenced with their employer without a break in continuous service, voluntary unemployment has not occurred. In these cases, compliance action would not be applied
  • the job seeker has voluntarily left employment, without reasonable excuse, and there has been a break in their continuous service with their employer, compliance action should be applied if the work was suitable and reasonable excuse did not apply

The compliance investigation should not be delayed on the basis that the job seeker is attempting to regain their job.

10

Determining if the job seeker is unemployed due to a voluntary act without a reasonable excuse + Read more ...

If the work is assessed as unsuitable work for the job seeker or the job seeker establishes a reasonable excuse for ceasing work voluntarily, compliance action is rejected.

If the job seeker leaves suitable work due to a voluntary act and reasonable excuse is not established, the penalty imposed depends on the compliance system the job seeker was managed under at the time the compliance action was generated.

For more information about finalising the compliance action, go to:

  • Targeted Compliance Framework, see Unemployment failures
  • Job Seeker Compliance Framework, see Applying an Unemployment Non-Payment Period (UNPP) and determining hardship provisions

Job seekers claiming or in receipt of Special Benefit (SpB), the treatment of voluntary unemployment differs depending on which mutual obligation requirements apply to the job seeker:

  • Under Mutual obligation requirements for SpB, job seeker compliance action may be applied for voluntary unemployment (as per JobSeeker Payment (JSP)
  • For all other job seekers receiving SpB, voluntary unemployment will mean that the job seeker is not qualified for SpB

Investigating dismissal due to misconduct

Table 6

Action

1

Primary focus of the compliance investigation + Read more ...

The compliance investigation focuses on establishing the facts relevant to the non-compliance event under consideration. This will enable an evidence-based decision in line with the legislative framework and policy intent.

When investigating whether a job seeker was dismissed due to misconduct, the relevant matters that must be considered include:

  • whether the employment was suitable work, and
  • whether an incident of misconduct occurred

The investigation does not consider whether the employee was correctly terminated or if the job seeker had a reasonable excuse for the misconduct. This is because misconduct is a behaviour within the job seeker's control. Where misconduct has occurred this is never considered reasonable.

2

Starting point for the investigation + Read more ...

Record the compliance investigation and decision using the Participation Compliance workflow in Process Direct.

To access the workflow:

  • select Customer Summary from the Process Direct Landing page
  • key the customer's CRN, select Go
  • key 'BIPCW' in the Super Key field, select [Enter]
  • the Participation Compliance Hub will display
  • select Compliance Investigation button from the Outstanding compliance actions table for the event date of the compliance action

At the beginning of the investigation, a clear explanation of the non-compliance event that has been reported and the specific matters that must be considered to reach a decision must be discussed with the job seeker.

During the course of the conversation, the expectations that job seekers remain in suitable work and demonstrate appropriate work behaviours should be reinforced.

The Service Officer must keep the conversations focused to ensure all key facts and the evidence that supports these is collected.

The information recorded when the compliance investigation was generated should then be used to initiate the discussion. This may include:

  • information recorded by the Service Officer in the workflow when generating the compliance investigation including the job seeker's explanation of the events leading to dismissal and any additional contact with employer
  • Employment Separation Certificate (SU1) - the nature of the employment, type of work being offered/performed, reason for separation, how long the job seeker had been working for the company
  • provider recorded Information, if applicable
  • any notes on the Progress of Claim DOC

3

Event date + Read more ...

The event date is generally the date last worked plus 1 day.

If the Service Officer has established that the failure was not committed on the event date that has been recorded, the event date on the key information page must be changed to reflect the correct event date.

See the Resources page for more information. All queries about the event date must be directed to an SO5 or the Level 2 Policy Helpdesk

Note: the Participation Compliance workflow event date must be changed prior to the decision page being completed. If the decision page has been completed, any subsequent change of event date on a decision to apply an unemployment failure will result in an incorrect penalty period being applied.

If the decision page has already been completed when an incorrect event date is identified, the workflow must be rejected and a new failure investigation must be generated with the correct event date.

4

Job seeker's explanation of reasons for dismissal + Read more ...

During the investigation, the job seeker must be given opportunity to respond to any adverse claim that have been made against them and disclose any further information they would like considered.

As a result of this discussion the Service Officer should record:

  • the nature of the employment and how long they had been working for the company
  • the job seeker's explanation for the dismissal
  • any efforts made by the job seeker to retain their employment, including any further legal action or discussions with the union officials since leaving
  • whether the job seeker received training or information about expected behaviour and whether any warnings were issued before the dismissal
  • any other relevant information provided by the job seeker

5

Suitability of work + Read more ...

Compliance action only applies if the work was suitable for the job seeker.

Additional considerations are required to assess suitability of work for job seekers who have a partial work capacity or are a principal carer parent.

PST-skilled Service Officers must reject unemployment failures if:

  • the job is more than 15 hours per week, and
  • the job seeker:
    • has a partial capacity to work, or
    • is a principal carer parent

For further information on suitability of work, see Suitable/unsuitable work.

If the unemployment due to misconduct was reported by a provider, they would have considered suitability before reporting the failure to Services Australia. Service Officers should assume the work was suitable for the job seeker based on the provider report unless they identify information during the investigation that indicates the work was unsuitable and/or this requires further investigation.

6

Misconduct as an employee + Read more ...

When assessing the employer's reason for terminating employment, it is important to ensure the reason for termination was for misconduct at work or in relation to the employment and not because of the employee's general behaviour.

It would generally not be appropriate to apply compliance action if:

  • the job seeker's individual circumstance led to an inability to produce satisfactory work
  • the job seeker's personal circumstances resulted in an inability to control their behaviour or to understand that such behaviour was not appropriate
  • dismissal was due to a very minor breach of conduct (such as being minimally late for work on a single occasion), the job seeker was not warned of dismissal, and was genuinely and reasonably unaware that their action or inaction would result in dismissal. However, some incidents that may seem minor, such as being late, can have significant repercussions for businesses and their reputations

It would be appropriate to apply compliance action where there is sufficient evidence to establish that dismissal was a result of:

  • a deliberate failure to produce/deliver a reasonable amount of work
  • unauthorised absences from duty without good reason
  • improper behaviour or practices, such as theft, assault or harassment of other employees or customers
  • actions that cause serious risk to the health or safety of other employees or customers
  • actions that threaten the reputation, viability or profitability of the business

If the job seeker is seeking an independent investigation by an external body into the reasons for the termination of employment, this process is outside the unemployment failure investigation and decision.

PST-skilled Service Officers or Service Officers with a UNPP security resource must complete their investigation using the evidence available at the time of the decision.

If the external body subsequently changes the facts in relation to the termination of the job seeker's employment, the customer can apply for a formal review of the unemployment failure decision.

7

Contacting employers for further evidence + Read more ...

Considering any previous contact by another Service Officer, contact with the employer may be required to establish the facts. This is particularly when extra evidence from the job seeker contradicts statements made by the employer.

The Service Officer should attempt to contact the employer by phone. This ensures a timely investigation and provides opportunity for matters raised to be clarified with additional questions if required.

Further contact with an employer may be required:

  • to determine that the reason for dismissal was for misconduct at work and not because of the employee's general behaviour
  • to clarify inconsistencies in the evidence gathered

If phone contact cannot be established with the employer, and evidence is likely to have an impact on the outcome of the decision, further evidence should be requested from the jobs seeker.

Where the job seeker was dismissed from suitable work due to misconduct as an employee, the penalty imposed depends on the compliance system the job seeker was managed under at the time the compliance action was generated.
