Assessing reasonable excuse

Table 1

Item

Description

1

Factors to consider when assessing a reasonable excuse + Read more ...

The legislative instrument specifies factors that must and must not be considered when assessing if a job seeker has a reasonable excuse for their non-compliance.

The following must be taken into account:

  • access to safe, secure and adequate housing, see Item 3 in Table 3
  • literacy and language issues, see Item 4 in Table 3
  • illness, impairment or conditions that require frequent treatment as described in Item 5 in Table 3. This includes:
    • cognitive or neurological impairments
    • psychiatric or psychological impairments or mental illness
  • drug or alcohol related issues, see Table 2
  • personal crisis including:
    • death of an immediate family member
    • recent release from gaol
    • Family and Domestic Violence or criminal violence see Item 7 in Table 3
  • unforeseen family or caring responsibilities, see Item 6 in Table 3
  • undertaking paid work at the time of the failure, see Item 2 in Table 3
  • any other relevant factors (excluding those which must not be considered) directly affected the job seekers ability to meet their mutual obligation requirements. More information is in Table 3. This includes where the job seeker:
    • was attending a job interview at the time, see Item 10 in Table 3
    • advises they already have a job, see Item 11 in Table 3
    • was complying with another compulsory requirement included in their Job Plan, see Item 12 Table 3

2

Factors which must not be considered when assessing reasonable excuse + Read more ...

The legislative instrument identifies factors that must not be considered:

  • factors that did not directly prevent the job seeker from meeting the requirement
  • drug and/or alcohol misuse or dependency for a job seeker subject to the Targeted Compliance Framework (TCF) model and:
    • has used this as a reasonable excuse for a previous failure, or
    • was referred to treatment and refused or failed to participate in the treatment, see Table 2

3

Other relevant factors to consider when assessing a reasonable excuse + Read more ...

Participation Solutions Team (PST)-skilled Service Officers should consider all other relevant factors when assessing a reasonable excuse, for example:

  • What reason/s has the job seeker provided for failing to comply with the requirement?
    • Have they discussed these reasons with their provider?
  • Did the reason/s for failing to comply directly prevent the job seeker from meeting the requirement?
  • Was the reason for failing to comply with a requirement foreseeable or within the job seeker's control?
    • When did the job seeker become aware they could not comply?
    • What did the job seeker do to try to comply?
  • Would the reason/s provided for non-compliance be considered acceptable by the general public? For example, would an employer accept the reason from an employee?
  • Did the job seeker make prior contact to advise they could not meet the requirement?
    • If not, what prevented prior contact?
    • Check the job seeker's attempts to make prior contact
    • Was it unreasonable to expect the job seeker to make prior contact?
  • When drug and/or alcohol misuse or dependency issues directly impacted non-compliance, more information is needed. See Table 2

4

Factors to consider when assessing prior contact + Read more ...

Job seekers must contact their provider beforehand if they are unable to attend or comply with a term in their Job Plan, including attending an appointment.

Prior contact is met when the job seeker contacts:

  • their employment service provider, or
  • another organisation or person identified in their Job Plan, for example the Job Plan may require a job seeker to contact their Work For the Dole (WFD) co-ordinator if they can't attend WFD

When reasonable excuse is established, a financial penalty will:

  • not be applied if
    • prior contact occurred, or
    • it was unreasonable to expect the job seeker to make prior contact
  • be applied if:
    • prior contact did not occur, and
    • it was reasonable to expect the job seeker to have done so

Further details about information recorded by the provider is available in the Participation Compliance workflow

5

Identifying circumstances impacting a job seeker's capacity to comply + Read more ...

All compliance investigations focus on the job seeker's reason/s for not complying with the reported non-compliance event.

During the investigation, allow the job seeker a chance to explain why they did not meet the requirement.

If the job seeker indicates their circumstances prevented them from complying, PST-skilled Service Officers need to question further to ensure they have a clear understanding of:

  • how the circumstance directly affected their ability to comply, and
  • if prior contact was not made, how did the circumstance prevent prior contact

For assistance assessing impacts of a circumstance it may be appropriate to consult with:

  • a PST Service Officer (SO5)
  • a PST-skilled social worker, or
  • other specialist officers
  • See Accessing a PST-skilled social worker

6

Impact of decisions by other tribunals or courts on the compliance investigation + Read more ...

Under no circumstances should a compliance investigation be delayed pending the outcome of an external process. Compliance investigations are a point in time determination, based on available facts and evidence.

When the outcome of an external decision changes the facts of the incident after a decision has been made, the decision should be reviewed see: Non-compliance with compulsory requirements - review and appeals.

7

Conflicting compulsory requirements + Read more ...

It is the provider's responsibility to ensure compulsory requirements in the Job Plan do not conflict. If the non-compliance event conflicts with another compulsory requirement, the failure must be rejected.

Record the following in the Participation Compliance workflow.

Investigation page

  • Category: Requirement/job is not suitable or has good reason - current commitments
  • Reason: Other conflicting requirement in Job/Participation Plan

Decision and Penalty page

  • Have you confirmed the requirement is not suitable: No
  • Are you satisfied there is reasonable excuse for the failure: Yes
  • Rejection reason: Other rejection reason
  • Other rejection reason: Undertaking other activities required in Job Plan - unable to comply

8

Study as a compulsory requirement + Read more ...

Job seekers with an approved full-time short course in their Job Plan are fully meeting their mutual obligation requirements. These job seekers must:

  • stay connected to their provider
  • attend appointments when advised, and
  • accept suitable offers of work

Providers will schedule compulsory appointments and offers of work around the job seekers course requirements.

Job seekers with a full-time short course/study compulsory requirement in their Job Plan can't be considered as failing to comply with mutual obligation requirements if the course/study prevented:

  • compliance with the requirement, or
  • accepting an offer of suitable work

If the non-compliance event conflicts with approved course/study requirements, reject the failure.

Record the following in the Participation Compliance workflow.

Investigation Page

  • Category: Requirement/job is not suitable or has good reason - current commitments
  • Reason: Study commitments

Decision and Penalty page

  • Have you confirmed the requirement is not suitable: No
  • Are you satisfied there is reasonable excuse for the failure: Yes
  • Rejection reason: other rejection reason
  • Other rejection reason: Undertaking other acceptable activity - unable to comply

If the job offered prevents the job seeker completing the approved course/study, reject the failure.

Record the following in the Participation Compliance workflow.

Investigation Page

  • Category: Requirement/job is not suitable or has good reason - current commitments
  • Reason: Study commitments

Decision and Penalty page

  • Have you confirmed the job is not suitable: Yes
  • Rejection reason: other rejection reason
  • Other rejection reason: Unsuitable work - other legislated reasons

Job seekers must continue to meet all of their mutual obligation requirements including job search, attending appointment and accepting suitable work when their Job Plan includes:

  • a part-time short course
  • a part-time course and part-time employment

Targeted Compliance Framework - special rules about drug and/or alcohol related reasons for non-compliance

Table 2

Item

Description

1

When drug and/or alcohol dependency can be used as a reasonable excuse + Read more ...

Community Development Program (CDP) job seekers are not subject to the Drug and/or Alcohol reasonable excuse provisions described in this table.

Job seekers outside of CDP can use drug and/or alcohol dependency as a reasonable excuse in limited circumstances.

When a job seeker advises they couldn't comply with their requirement as a direct result of drug and/or alcohol dependency, this excuse can be accepted under reasonable excuse provisions when:

  • evidence of their dependency exists via a current Employment Services Assessment (ESAt) or medical/other evidence of dependency, and
  • this is the first time the job seeker has used drug and/or alcohol dependency or
  • they are participating in treatment or
  • they have agreed to participate in treatment but are unable because
    • appropriate treatment options are unavailable, or
    • they are unable to participate in treatment for other reasons

Where drug and/or alcohol dependency is accepted as the main reason for the failure, record the following in the Participation Compliance workflow:

  • Category: Requirement/job is not suitable or they have a good reason - personal
  • Reason: Substance or alcohol use affected compliance

Reject the failure using: Drug or Alcohol related reasons

Ensure the job seeker understands and is advised:

  • 'A penalty won't be applied today due to your drug and/or alcohol barriers, your provider will discuss this with you and advise you if treatment options are available in your area'
  • 'If you refuse to start treatment offered by your provider, we cannot accept drug and/or alcohol barriers as a reason for not meeting your requirements in future'
  • 'If you are having difficulty meeting a requirement, you need to discuss this with your provider before the requirement or appointment starts'

PST-skilled Service Officers must consider updating the job seekers Circumstances impacting Compliance to provide additional information to their provider.

2

When drug and/or alcohol dependency is not able to be used as a reasonable excuse + Read more ...

Job seekers outside of CDP cannot use drug and/or alcohol dependency as a reasonable excuse when:

  • they have previously used this as a reason for non-compliance, and
  • they have refused or failed to participate in appropriate treatment options arranged by their provider

The Participation Compliance workflow will not display Drug or Alcohol related reasons as an option to reject a mutual obligation failure if the reason was recorded previously.

Ensure the job seeker understands and is advised:

  • 'A penalty will be applied today; because we can't accept drug/alcohol problems as an excuse for not meeting your requirements'
  • 'If you want to know more about drug/alcohol treatment options available in your area, talk to your provider'
  • 'If you start treatment, we may be able to take drug and/or alcohol issues into account in the future if they have directly affected your ability to meet your requirements'
  • 'If you are having difficulty meeting a requirement talk to your provider before the requirements or appointment starts'

Reasons for non-compliance

Table 3

Item

Description

1

Transport issues + Read more ...

It is reasonable for job seekers to commute from their home to attend a requirement using available forms of transport for up to:

  • 90 minutes for a job seeker with full time mutual obligation requirements
  • 60 minutes for a job seekers with a part-time mutual obligation requirement

Job seekers who have part-time requirements are:

  • job seekers receiving Parenting Payment with mutual obligation requirements
  • job seekers receiving JobSeeker Payment (JSP), Youth Allowance (YA) and Special Benefit (Nominated Visa Holder) and identified as principal carers
  • job seekers with a partial capacity for work

If a job seeker says transport affected their ability to comply, consider:

  • Was enough notice given to allow the job seeker to arrange transport?
  • What caused the transport arrangement to fail?
  • Was the job seeker able to make other transport arrangements once they knew their original plans had fallen through?
  • Was it reasonable for the job seeker to contact and reschedule the requirement for a time when transport was available?
  • Would the reasons provided be accepted by a member of the public? For example, would an employer accept the reason from an employee?

2

Job seeker working + Read more ...

If a job seeker says their employment affected their ability to comply, consider:

  • if working hours or income received in the instalment period (see Employment Income Paid Details (EAPP) screen) has been or will be declared
    • Did they work at the time of the requirement?
    • Was the work for the full day or were they still able to comply with the requirement?
  • when did the jobseeker know they had to work?
    • Would it have been reasonable for them to contact to advise of their inability to attend due to work commitments?
  • is evidence of work required including hours worked? For example pay slips or employment contact details relating to the work in this period

Reasonable excuse does not exist if the job seeker:

  • has not declared hours worked in the instalment period where the non-compliance event occurred, or
  • is unable to provide their employer contact details or the employer can't confirm the job seeker was working at the time of the requirement

3

Accommodation + Read more ...

A job seeker's ability to comply may be impacted if they:

  • did not have access to safe, secure and adequate housing, or
  • were using emergency accommodation or a refuge at the time the compliance incident occurred

A circumstance impacting compliance or Homelessness Indicator may have been recorded as a result of a previous specialist assessment or contact. These indicators do not automatically provide a reasonable excuse for the circumstances of a specific failure.

If a job seeker advises their accommodation situation has affected their ability to comply, consider:

  • When did the job seeker's accommodation circumstances change?
  • Did the job seeker move to a new area?
  • Did the accommodation circumstances directly affect the job seeker's ability to comply the requirement at the time?
  • What impact did the accommodation circumstances have on the job seeker's capacity to make prior contact?

4

Literacy and language + Read more ...

The literacy and language skills of the person can directly impact their capacity to comply with provisions of the Social Security Act 1991.

A circumstance impacting compliance may have been recorded as a result of a previous assessment of literacy and language skills. These indicators do not automatically provide a reasonable excuse for a specific failure.

Note: if the job seeker has language barriers, use an interpreter during the compliance investigation, or record the reasons why not.

If a job seeker advises their literacy and language skills affected their ability to comply, consider:

  • What impact did the job seeker's literacy and language skills have on their ability to comply?
  • If the requirements were notified to the job seeker in a way they could understand?
  • If the job seeker requested support to understand the requirements. For example, do they have a nominee?
  • If an interpreter is required, was one used when the job seeker was notified of their requirements?

If the job seeker is unable to understand a requirement or an instruction, despite attempts made by the provider or Services Australia, reasonable excuse may still apply.

5

Illness or impairment + Read more ...

A job seeker's capacity to comply may be affected by:

  • illnesses, impairments or conditions for which the job seeker requires frequent treatment, including illnesses that are episodic or unpredictable in nature
  • any cognitive or neurological impairments i.e. the job seeker lacks understanding of what is required or is unable to undertake their requirements
  • any psychiatric or psychological impairments or mental illnesses
  • any other illnesses

A circumstance impacting compliance may have been recorded as a result of a previous specialist assessment resulting in a reduced work capacity due to an illness, impairment or addiction. This does not automatically provide a reasonable excuse for the circumstances of a specific failure.

Where the job seeker advises they are unable to comply due to illnesses, impairments or conditions, consider:

  • if the incapacity directly affect the job seeker's ability to comply with the requirement at the time?
  • If the incapacity affected the job seeker's ability to call beforehand if this is required? (use similar principals to what is expected by an employer if a worker was unable to attend due to illness or impairment)
  • A current ESAt report where it exists for further information on the job seekers cognitive or neurological impairment

Where a job seeker did not make prior contact and it was reasonable to do so, it is not necessary to request a medical certificate. The failure should be applied.

A medical certificate should not be requested for a date in the past where a job seeker did not attend a doctor at the time of the non-compliance event.

Before making a decision about the failure, consult a specialist officer with the relevant expertise if:

  • medical evidence is unavailable
  • assistance is required to understand the ESAt report
  • a job seeker has a lack of insight into their condition before making a decision about the failure

A failure will be automatically revoked when:

  • a medical certificate is accepted after the failure has been determined, and
  • the exemption period covers the incident date

Consider an ESAt referral when new a new circumstance(s) has been identified during the investigation to ensure future servicing is appropriate for the job seeker's capacity.

6

Family or caring responsibilities + Read more ...

Family and caring responsibilities can affect the person's capacity to comply with mutual obligation requirements.

Job seekers who are principal carers have flexible participation requirements in recognition of their caring responsibilities.

A circumstance impacting compliance may be recorded as a result of a previous assessment of significant caring responsibilities. This does not automatically provide a reasonable excuse for the circumstances of a specific failure.

If a job seeker advises their family and/or caring responsibilities affected their ability to comply, consider:

  • Does the job seeker have regular caring requirements?
  • Did pre-arranged caring arrangements break down?
  • Were the caring requirements unforeseen?
  • Was anyone else available to provide care?
  • How did caring responsibilities directly impact the job seeker's capacity to comply with the requirement at the time they failed to do so?
  • What impact did the caring responsibilities have on the job seeker's capacity to make prior contact?

Verification using a medical certificate is preferred where caring responsibilities relate to an illness of a child. However, in circumstances where the child is expected to quickly recover, the job seekers word can be accepted. If there is a history of non-compliance due to similar reasons, a medical certificate should be requested.

7

Personal crisis + Read more ...

A personal crisis may impact the job seeker's capacity to comply with a requirement. For example:

  • the death of an immediate family member
  • a relationship breakdown
  • family and domestic violence
  • family dislocation
  • physical, emotional or sexual abuse, or
  • a recent release from prison

Where a job seeker advises they were unable to comply due to a personal crisis consider:

  • when did the personal crisis occur?
  • how did the personal crisis significantly and directly impact the job seeker's capacity to comply at the time of the requirement?

Generally, the job seeker is required to provide evidence to support their explanation if there is no record of the crisis on the job seeker's record.

PST-skilled Service Officers can consult with an appropriate specialist to confirm the impact of the disclosed personal crisis.

If family and domestic violence concerns are identified or help is needed in identifying if it is occurring, see:

  • Challenges faced by parents, and
  • Family and domestic violence

Note: a PST-skilled Service Officer must consult with a PST-skilled Social Worker where the job seeker presents with family and domestic violence or has a current Family relationships/Domestic violence circumstance recorded. See Accessing a PST-Skilled social worker.

8

Ability to contact to advise non-attendance prior to the time of the appointment or requirement + Read more ...

A job seeker may contact shortly after a requirement. This may show genuine intentions of the job seeker and can be considered when making the decision that contact prior to the requirement was not possible.

When deciding if it was possible or reasonable for a job seeker to make contact prior to a requirement, the following should be considered:

  • If the job seeker claims to have run out of phone credit:
    • Was the mobile phone their only means of communication?
    • What other phones or forms of communication were available at the time, including friends, family, public phones, access to email etc?
    • When did the job seeker become aware they had run out of credit?
    • What contact arrangement do they have in place for contacting friends and family, their doctor, etc. when this happens?
  • The job seeker claims to be out of mobile reception:
    • Where was the job seeker at the time?
    • If the job seeker is often in a non-reception zone, how far do they need to travel to get coverage and was this possible in this instance?
    • The reasonable availability of other fixed line and mobile phones?
  • In the case of working:
    • Whether the employment allowed for the job seeker to contact after their shift started, for example during a break
    • When did the job seeker become aware they would need to be at work?
    • Why didn't this allow for contact prior to the start of the shift?

Where a customer is experiencing a major personal crisis, an assessment by the PST-skilled Service Officer is required to discuss the impact that the crisis had on the job seeker.

  • In the case of a major personal crisis:
    • Was the major personal crisis affecting prior to the failure?
    • Was it reasonable to have expected the job seeker to contact
    • Would the job seeker have actually remembered the requirement at the time

9

Job seeker forgot or confused requirements + Read more ...

When a job seeker claims to have confused their requirements, the following should be considered:

  • How recently were they notified?
  • How similar are the requirements they confused?
  • Did the job seeker attend either requirement? For example, it is not an excuse to say you thought the appointment was at the time of another appointment if they did not attend either appointment.
  • Whether the job seeker has recently moved to the area/changed provider?
  • How long has the job seeker been attending their various requirements?

When a job seeker claims to have forgotten their requirement, the following should be considered:

  • How recently the notification occurred
  • How often and for how long has the job seeker been required to meet the requirement?
  • Is there any physiological reason for forgetfulness? For example:
    • an acquired brain injury affecting mental illness
    • a drug and/or alcohol dependency?
  • If the job seeker has a history of forgetfulness, what steps do they take to remind themselves of requirements?
    • Do they keep a calendar or diary, or set an alarm on their mobile phone?
  • How often has the job seeker offered this excuse?
  • Did the job seeker contact to reschedule as soon as they became aware of the missed requirement?

10

Job seeker attending a job interview at the time of the requirement + Read more ...

When a job seeker claims to have attended a job interview at the time of the requirements, consider the following:

  • When did they know about the job interview?
    • Is it reasonable for the job seeker to advise of their inability to attend before the requirement?
  • Did the provider arrange the job interview? If the provider:
    • was involved, is the compliance action suitable? The provider can be contacted to discuss
    • was not involved, did the job seeker attempt to contact their provider before the requirement to advise of the job interview?

11

Job seeker advises they have a job + Read more ...

As a general rule, job seekers must accept any suitable job offer. This includes circumstances where the job seeker is able to start the job on offer but will likely leave the job within a short period of time, for example, the job seeker is a principle carer and their childcare arrangements are temporary.

If a job seeker advises they have a firm job offer starting in the future, the reasons why the job seeker has refused suitable work must be assessed on a case by case basis.

Job seekers who are declaring either hours worked or employment income received must continue to look for and accept suitable work, unless all of the following apply:

  • the job seeker is not able to undertake both jobs, due to overlapping work hours
  • the job on offer hourly rate is lower
  • the hours on offer mean the employment income is less than their current job

12

Job seeker has an approved short course included within their Job Plan + Read more ...

Job seekers with an approved full-time short course within their Job Plan are considered as meeting their mutual obligation requirements. These job seekers must:

  • stay connected to their provider
  • attend appointments when advised, and
  • accept suitable offers of work

In return the providers will schedule compulsory appointments and offers of work around the job seekers course requirements.

Job seekers with a full-time short course/study compulsory requirements in their Job Plan can't be considered to be failing to comply with mutual obligation requirements if their study commitments prevented them from:

  • complying with the requirement, or
  • accepting an offer of suitable work

Job seekers who have a part-time short course in their Job Plan must continue to meet all of their mutual obligation requirements, including job search, attending appointments and accept suitable work.

It is up to the provider to ensure compulsory requirements within the Job Plan do not conflict.
