For Complex Assessment Officer (CAO) use only

This page contains information about the process for attributing the correct amount of income to a customer from their involvement in designated and controlled private trusts or companies, taking into account who the money has been distributed to.

Assessing income attribution to a customer

Item

Description

1

General information + Read more ...

For many customers, assessment of income from private companies and trusts changed on 1 January 2002. See also Assessment of income from trusts and companies for the treatment of income from private companies and trusts.

The pre 1 January 2002 rules (which do not include attribution of income) apply to the assessment of income from private companies and trusts for:

  • all customers prior to 1 January 2002
  • customers who are non-attributable stakeholders of private companies and trusts on or after 1 January 2002, and
  • customers who receive income from private companies and trusts that do not meet the definition of 'designated' or 'controlled' private companies and trusts

2

Private companies and trusts dividends and distributions + Read more ...

Dividends and distributions received from private companies and trusts are included in the customer's income. The gross amount of the dividend payment, including any imputation credit, is the amount to be assessed as income.

The assessed income includes the actual amount of any individual payments, and any franking credits attached to them.

Explanation: Franking credits are also known as imputation credits.

Prior to 1 July 2000, a taxpayer obtained a tax rebate equal to the amount of imputation credits received. The rebate reduced income tax payable, but once tax payable was reduced to zero, no benefit was obtained from further excess credits and was lost.

From the 2000-01 financial year, a person without a tax liability can lodge an 'Application for Refund of Imputation Credits for Individuals' with the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) and receive a payment from the ATO equal to the amount of imputation credits. A person with a tax liability will continue to lodge an income tax return to obtain a rebate to reduce income tax payable.

From 1 July 2001, a person without a tax liability may receive the gross amount of the dividend from the source of the dividend, that is, eligible taxpayers will be able to receive early refunds of excess imputation credits.

3

Dividends and imputation credits + Read more ...

A company is a taxpayer in its own right. This means that any dividend received from its investments, both from shares in other companies and from managed investments may have imputation credits attached to them.

These imputation credits are income in the 'hands' of the company, but will not show up in the profit and loss statements of the company because they may be offset against any tax payable by the company. Therefore, the imputation credits attached to dividends received by a company have to be added to the accounting profit of the company.

A trust does not pay tax in its own right, but can be assessed separately on income it does not distribute. This means that the tax return of a trust will include the imputation credits attached to dividends received even though the profit and loss may not, because usually they are distributed to the beneficiaries and the beneficiaries are the ones who take advantage of the imputation credits.

As such, imputation credits need to be added to any dividends paid to shareholders by a company, but are already included in any distribution paid to beneficiaries by a trust until the 2011/2012 financial year.

Tax Ruling 2012/D1

As a result of this Tax Ruling, the trust tax returns from the 2011/2012 financial year may no longer include imputation credits under Division 6E of the Taxation Assessment Act in the Income of the Trust Estate field of the return.

The ATO Trust Income Tax Return Instructions state that franking credits are included in the field: Franked distributions from trusts.

This means that if the trust income is assessed based on ‘Income of the Trust Estate’ the CAO may need to add the franking credits back into the income, depending on the terms of the Trust Deed.

However, if the income is being assessed based on ‘Total net income or loss’ which includes ‘Franked distributions from trusts’, the CAO will not need to add the franking credits back into the trust income.

4

Basic rules of imputation in practice + Read more ...

The basic rules of imputation are:

  • (a) for a company (private or public) the amount of franked dividend declared does not include the imputation credit and the two will need to be added together. For example: if $700 franked dividend is declared as income, and a $300 imputation credit, then the income to be assessed is $700 + $300 = $1000
  • (b) however for a trust (private and managed fund) the distribution amount on the trust's tax return (for financial years prior to 2011/2012) and the client's tax return are already grossed up to include the imputation credit, that is, the imputation credit is already included in the distribution amount declared. For example: if $1000 is declared as a distribution and a $300 imputation credit, then the income to be assessed is $1000
  • (c) from the 2011/2012 financial year onward trust tax returns will no longer show any imputation credits and will need to be added to the customer's distribution amount from a private trust

(i) For a controlled private company/trust, the income may include amounts from other public companies and trusts and Centrelink should include the imputation credits in the income of the entity as outlined above in (a) and (b) for the controllers.

(ii) for non-controllers assessed only on distribution income or controllers who receive income greater than their attributed amount:

  • for a private company dividend, the imputation credit has to be added on to the dividend
  • for a private trust, the distribution may no longer include the imputation credit (2011/12 tax year onwards) and have to be added to the distribution if using the Income of the Trust Estate field for the assessment

Note: be careful here because it is possible the client received more than their share because they received a payment from prior year profits.

5

Foreign tax credit system + Read more ...

Under the foreign tax credit system, income derived by an Australian entity outside of Australia is assessable, but a 'direct' credit is allowable for foreign tax paid on foreign income, up to the amount of the Australian tax payable in respect of that income. That is, excess tax paid is not refundable, but can offset tax paid and is therefore not income of a trust or a company.

6

Fraud Investigation Branch - Undeclared business structures + Read more ...

Note: the following information relates to reviews undertaken by the Fraud Investigation Branch only.

Where a customer does not provide any of the requested documents to enable an assessment of attributable income, and all other avenues to obtain the information are exhausted, entity income can be determined by bank account deposits in the following circumstances:

  • The Complex Assessment Officer (CAO) must be satisfied that the customer has a controlling interest in the entity, and:
    • the company has only issued ordinary shares
    • the customer has attributed control of more than 50% of the issued capital

Note: due to the nature of private trusts, it is unlikely that the CAO will be able to determine control without full documentation and a module PT or trust deed.

  • The customer must have been given the opportunity to voluntarily supply the requested documents. Before raising any overpayment, the customer must have been contacted by the investigator and advised what information the agency has in its possession and invited to provide any contrary information they may have
  • The attributable income will be determined by the total annual deposits to entity bank accounts and assessed from the start of the financial year
  • The customer is issued a written notification outlining the details of the overpayment and the information used to determine the overpayment and an opportunity to provide new information

Note: an assessment of attributable assets will also be necessary. The attributable assets will equal the known entity assets, for example, as determined by bank account balances or title search at point in time.

7

Cash flow boost payments + Read more ...

Cash flow boost payments paid by the Australian Tax Office to eligible small and medium sized businesses as part of the Australian Government COVID-19 stimulus response are exempt from assessment under section 8(8)(zt) of the Social Security Act 1991, and should not be included when assessing business income for a customer or their partner. Payments will be made for the March to June 2020 and June to September 2020 quarters.

If these payments appear as income on the profit and loss statement for an entity, they will need to be removed and disregarded in assessing the net income.
