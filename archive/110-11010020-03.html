Starting the investigation using the IMS

Description

Creating an investigation plan for cases in the IMS + Read more ...

After completing the receipt of case procedure:

  • Create a new Investigation Plan activity and save it
  • Edit data then:
    • Code Planned End Date/Time field and time taken to create the plan in the Duration: field
    • Set Status to 'In Progress'
    • Brief comments can be recorded in the Extra details section. Select Activity Notes to add more detailed notes
  • From the Investigation Toolkit, Criminal Investigations locate the Investigation Plan template
  • Complete components of the Investigation Plan and create an Evidence Matrix
  • Save and upload the Investigation Plan to the Investigation Plan activity

2

Amending investigation plan in IMS + Read more ...

Amendments can only be made up until approval by the Team Leader is recorded. If approval has been recorded, go to Step 4.

If approval not recorded:

  • Open the newest version of the Investigation Plan activity
  • Make amendments to the Investigation Plan Word document
  • Save the document and attach the amended version to the Investigation Plan activity under Attachments

3

Approval of investigation plan in IMS + Read more ...

Investigator

The investigator must seek approval of the plan before proceeding with any of the planned actions.

Update the status of the Investigation Plan activity to 'Awaiting Approval TL'.

Senior Investigator/Approver

The Senior Investigator/Approver is responsible for approval of the investigation plan. If any element of the investigation plan is not agreed, discuss this with the investigator. The investigation plan can be amended by the investigator and resubmitted for approval

Record details of the decision by adding activity notes to the investigation plan activity.

4

Reviewing investigation plan in IMS + Read more ...

Once approval by the Senior Investigator/Approver has been recorded, the investigator is required to update the plan as actions in relation to enquiries are made and as evidence is obtained. This includes making a determination in relation to whether an element is proven.

The investigator can add further enquiries as the investigation progresses. An investigation plan will only require further approval when a substantial change in the direction of an investigation is proposed.

Where there is a substantial change in the direction of the investigation, a revised version of the plan must be submitted for approval in a new Investigation Plan activity.

All previous versions of the investigation plan must be retained.

5

Allegation + Read more ...

This is a declaration of conduct identified as allegedly, apparently, or potentially breaching the law. The allegation states the assertion that the investigation will set out to prove or disprove.

Communication of the allegation outlines the reason for the investigation and provides scope for the relevant avenues of enquiry.

The allegation does not need to be stated in legal or technical terms. It should be clear and easily understood. It should enable the reader to obtain a workable understanding of the reasons for the investigation, person(s) involved, alleged activities and the level of complexity.

6

Objective and possible outcomes + Read more ...

The objective is a simple statement detailing what the investigation seeks to achieve.

Default objective, sufficient for most investigations:

'To undertake an investigation in accordance with Australian Government Investigations Standards to determine the facts in relation to the allegation, and whether prosecution referral is appropriate.'

The possible outcomes detail the potential results that the investigation may achieve. These outcomes may include:

  • administrative debts
  • change in payment rate
  • suspension/ cancellation of payments
  • referral of a brief of evidence to the Commonwealth Director of Public Prosecutions (CDPP)
  • referral to another agency, and
  • referral for Proceeds of Crime Act action

7

Expected completion date + Read more ...

The expected completion date for the investigation will be the estimated date that a brief of evidence will be referred to the CDPP (or a decision made to finalise the case and not refer to CDPP).

Reviewing the plan - risks and sensitivities

Table 2

Item

Description

1

Risks or sensitivities + Read more ...

A risk is the chance of something happening that will have an impact on the stated objectives of the investigation.

A formal risk management process is not required, but it is important to consider and document the risks identified. Risks that may be identified when planning investigations include:

  • significant time elapsed since commission of offences, impacting on the availability of information/ evidence and the likelihood of successful prosecution action
  • information source/ witness may notify the customer of enquiries, compromising further information gathering
  • problematic or contentious policy or legislation
  • occupational health and safety or security concerns
  • access to assistance from other agencies or experts

A sensitivity is any element of the investigation that may impact negatively on the customer or the reputation of Services Australia. Sensitivities have the potential to attract media or ministerial interest.

Sensitivities that may be identified when planning investigations include:

  • the subject of the investigation has a high profile or is well known at a local, state or national level (or is associated with a high profile person)
  • the customer is over 60 years of age
  • customer has issues with illness or addiction
  • customer is homeless, has family and domestic violence issues or the presence of any other vulnerability
  • aspects of the case are unusual or topical and may generate media interest

2

Mitigation of risks or sensitivities + Read more ...

If risks and sensitivities are identified, the investigation plan should include how they will be mitigated.

Any extreme risks that cannot be mitigated should be discussed with the Senior Investigator or Assistant Director Investigations to determine whether it is appropriate to proceed with an investigation.

The mitigation for most sensitivities will include some form of management/ executive briefing at various stages of the investigation and potentially social worker consultation.

See the Resources page for an example risks/ sensitivities and mitigation strategy.

A Critical Case Report (CCR) should be completed by the investigator if they identify a customer who matches one or more critical case report criteria. Preparation of the CCR will be part of the mitigation strategy.

3

Information collection plan + Read more ...

Identify and record:

  • Sources of information to be used to determine the facts of the matter and to make a decision in relation to any prosecution referral
  • Method of obtaining the information. This may include requesting information voluntarily, obtaining evidential material through requests for Witness Statements, or obtaining evidential material through the use of search warrants
  • The reason for the enquiry being made. Explain the relevance of the request
  • An expected delivery date

The collection of information must be in accordance with the relevant social security law and Australian Privacy Principles (APPs), 1 - 5.

The legislative information gathering powers in the Social Security (Administration) Act 1999 (and other payment enabling legislation) must not be used in an Intelligence and Investigations Branch criminal investigation. The information gathering powers cannot be lawfully used to answer administrative questions of qualification, payability and rate of payment at the same time as conducting a criminal investigation.

See the Resources page for an example of an information collection plan.

4

Possible offences + Read more ...

Detail the offences identified within the allegation.

Offences will usually be identified from the following legislation:

  • Criminal Code Act 1995 (offences from 24 May 2001)
  • Crimes Act 1914 (offences prior to 24 May 2001)
  • Social Security (Administration) Act 1999
  • Health Insurance Act 1973
  • Human Services (Medicare) Act 1973
  • Child Support (Assessment) Act 1989
  • Child Support (Registration and Collection) Act 1988

5

Evidence matrix + Read more ...

The identification of relevant offences allows for an evidence matrix to be prepared listing:

  • elements of the offence
  • facts in issue (what is required to be proven)
  • avenues of inquiry that will enable the collection of admissible evidence to prove each element of the offence

Many avenues of inquiry identified in the information collection plan will be duplicated in the evidence matrix, although what is being sought will differ, for example, a response to a questionnaire versus a witness statement.

The evidence matrix will help to identify gaps that may exist in the investigation case. It is a standalone document and must be submitted with the investigation plan.

The evidence matrix is a living document and should be maintained as the investigation progresses. It should be reviewed at critical decision points in the investigation and should support critical decisions to be made.

6

Proceeds of Crime Act (POCA) consideration + Read more ...

Proceeds of crime are:

  • any property or benefit that is wholly or partially derived or realised, directly or indirectly, from the commission of an offence
  • any property that is used or is intended to be used in the commission of an offence, that is, it is an instrument of the offence, or
  • literary proceeds derived from the commercial exploitation of notoriety from the commission of an offence

Investigators are required to consider POCA action as part of investigation planning and during the investigation. At the initial planning stage, the Intelligence Assessment will provide an indication on potential proceeds of crime.

7

Specialist assistance + Read more ...

The need for specialist assistance may be identified from the avenue of inquiries proposed in the information collection plan and evidence matrix.

They may also be identified based on complexities expected to arise during the case.

This specialist assistance may be sought from within the Fraud Investigation Branch, from other Branches within Services Australia, or from external agencies.

At the initial planning stage it may not be possible to identify all specialist assistance required.

Examples of specialist assistance that may be required:

  • Australian Federal Police - provide assistance with investigative casework, training and execution of search warrants
  • Complex Assessment Officers - cases involving a Trust, Company or complex business structures
  • Financial Analysts - matters involving unexplained/understated income and complex financial activity
  • Information and Integrity Group Social Workers if the customer has identified vulnerabilities
  • Surveillance providers if surveillance is required

8

Additional resources/costs + Read more ...

In addition to the investigator and any specialists identified, identify any other resources or costs that are expected as part of the investigation.

Additional resources or costs may include:

  • additional investigator (a complex investigation may warrant an additional investigator being allocated)
  • travel
  • interpreters
  • information searches
