Customers can still claim CCS even if their family income estimate is equal to or above the higher income limit. If they actually earn less than this, the subsidy they are entitled to receive will be paid at reconciliation.

Note: the above percentages are Standard Rates. From 7 March 2022, families with multiple children in care aged 5 or under may receive an increased CCS rate up to 30% higher for eligible children, capped at 95%. This is known as Multiple Child Subsidy. For more information, see Entitlement to Child Care Subsidy (CCS).

Scenarios - applying the annual subsidy cap for the 2020/2021 financial year

Table 2: this table provides examples of how and when the annual subsidy cap was applied. The figures quoted below are correct for 2020/2021 financial year and are used for demonstration purposes.

^ Hourly rate caps, annual subsidy caps and income thresholds are subject to CPI changes prior to the commencement of each new CCS year.

From 10 December 2021, the CCS annual cap for the 2021/22 financial year (1 July 2021 - 30 June 2022) onwards was removed. Families who reached the annual cap prior to 10 December 2021 were paid any CCS entitlement owed to them in December 2021.

Note: families may still be subject to the annual cap for financial years prior to the 2021/22 financial year

Item

Examples

1

Combined income above the annual subsidy cap threshold (cap reached)

Both parents work full time and have a combined income of $280,000. They are entitled to a 41.43% subsidy for 100 hours of child care. They have one child who attends a centred based child care 5 days a week, with each being a 10 hour session costing $150 ($15 per hour).

  • Hourly CCS = $5.05 (41.43% of $12.20^)
  • Fortnightly CCS = $505.00 (100 hrs x 5.05)

The annual subsidy cap would apply to this family as their income is above $189,390^. CCS payments to their provider will stop once they have reached the $10,560^ cap (26 fortnights x $505.00 = $13,130).

2

Combined income above the annual subsidy cap threshold (cap not reached)

Both parents work full time and have a combined income of $310,000. They are entitled to a 31.43% subsidy for 100 hours of child care. They have one child who attends a centre based child care 5 days a week, with each day being a 10 hour session costing $150 ($15 per hour).

  • Hourly CCS = $3.37 (31.43% of $12.20^)
  • Fortnightly CCS = $383.00 (100 hrs x $3.83)

The annual subsidy cap would apply to this family as their income is above $189,390^, however they would not reach the $10,560^ cap (26 fortnights x $383.00 = $9958).

3

Couple with an annual subsidy cap separate

Jono and Cara have two children attending child care and a combined family income of $250,000 (Cara $190,000 and Jono $60,000), therefore they are subject to the annual subsidy cap. During the financial year, Jono and Cara separate. Cara is the eligible individual for the purposes of CCS and has been paid $7,000 for each child at the time of separation.

When Cara notifies of their separation, CCS is reassessed based on Cara's individual circumstances. As Cara's income is above the salary cap threshold, Cara can be paid the remainder of the annual subsidy cap ($3,650^) per child. If Cara updates the income estimate and the new estimate falls below the annual cap threshold, there will be no cap on the amount of CCS Cara can be paid for the rest of the year.

If Jono wishes to claim CCS, Jono will need to make a separate claim and Jono's entitlement to CCS will be based on Jono's estimated income and activity test. If Jono’s income is above the annual salary cap threshold, Jono could receive up to $10,560^ for the remainder of the CCS year. If the income is below the threshold, the annual cap will not apply. That is, previous amounts paid to Cara are not relevant to Jono’s entitlement.

4

Family reached annual cap prior to 10 December 2021

Paris and Marc have two children attending childcare, their combined family income is $280,000. Therefore they are subject to an annual cap for the 2021-22 financial year. As Paris and Marc’s children regularly attend childcare, they reach the annual cap on 7 October 2021 and are required to pay full childcare fee’s from this date. On 10 December 2021, the annual cap is removed for the full financial year (2021/22) onwards.

Paris and Marc receive an arrears payment for any CCS entitlement they are owed for the period 7 October to 10 December 2021. This arrears payment is paid to them in December 2021.

5

Multiple children using In Home Care (pre 10 December 2021)

Sam and David are shift workers and have been connected with an in-home carer through an IHC support agency. They have a combined annual income of $201,000 so are subject to the CCS annual subsidy cap.

Sam lodges a claim for CCS for their three children Zander, Ryder and Holly, and is assessed as eligible for all three children. As IHC is provided at the family level the enrolment notice submitted by the IHC service will only be for one child, in this instance Zander. The session reports submitted to the department at the family level will be linked to Zander’s record.

As Sam and David are subject to the annual subsidy cap, once this is reached for Zander, the IHC service will lodge a new enrolment for Ryder. Sam will need to confirm the enrolment notice for Ryder before CCS can again be paid. The family attendance information will continue to be submitted and will now be linked to Ryder’s record.

The same process would apply for Holly once the annual subsidy cap is reached for Ryder.
