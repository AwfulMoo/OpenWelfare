Scenario 1st/2nd deadline

Pamela was paid CCS during the 2019/20 year but has been unable to lodge tax returns because relevant documents were lost in a natural disaster which affected Pamela's local area.

Pamela contacts Services Australia on 15 June to advise she will not be able to meet the income confirmation requirements by the 1st deadline.

The staff member assesses Pamela as meeting the requirements for an exceptional and unforeseen circumstance extension, which is granted until 1 October 2021.

This would allow Pamela’s CCS entitlement to continue at the income-tested percentage until the extension end date, instead of dropping to 0% from 1 July 2021.Pamela must still confirm 2021/2022 income after 30 June 2022 and prior to that years deadlines to retain the extended entitlement.

Once this date has passed and Pamela still hasn’t lodged the tax return, the 1st deadline rules would be applied and Pamela's CCS would drop to 0% and a debt will be raised with recovery temporarily paused. Pamela would not receive any CCS arrears for any periods between where CCS has reduced to 0% and where income requirements are met even once reconciliation occurs for that period.

If Pamela still failed to meet income requirements by the 2nd deadline, CCS would cancel and the debt (where recovery was temporarily paused) would become recoverable.

If Pamela, meets the reconciliation after the 2nd deadline, the non-lodger debt will be finalised no debt. Pamela will be reconciled, however if entitled to a top up, this will not be paid.

Note: if this customer’s circumstances warranted the maximum allowable extension, the 1st income confirmation deadline could only be moved as far as 30 June 2022.

This is because the 1st deadline extension cannot be dated past the 2nd income confirmation deadline.

The 2nd deadline extension may be stretched indefinitely depending on what is being experienced by the customer. This would only be granted under very limited circumstances.

Scenario: Annual cap

Table 6

Example

Description

1

Annual Cap 2021-22 CCS year:

Family reached annual cap prior to 10 December 2021

Paris and Marc have two children attending childcare, their combined family income is $280,000. Therefore, during the 2021-22 CCS year they were subject to the annual cap. As Paris and Marc’s children regularly attend childcare, they reach the annual cap on 7 October 2021 and are required to pay full childcare fees from this date. On 10 December 2021, the annual cap was removed for the full financial year (2021-22) onwards.

Paris and Marc are entitled to receive arrears for any CCS entitlement they were owed for the period 7 October to 10 December 2021. They will receive this arrears payment automatically in December 2021. Marc and Paris are no longer subject to an annual cap and will continue receive CCS.

2

Annual Cap 2019-20 or 2020-21 CCS year

Jon and Trish are partnered and have a combined income of $150,000. Trish is CCS current.

Jon and Trish separate. Trish continues to be assessed for CCS based on an individual income estimate of $100,000.

Trish gets back together with Jon. They provide a combined income estimate of $170,000.

Trish separates again from Jon and updates the estimate again to $100,000.

CCS reconciles, based on Trish’s actual income of $110,000 and Jon’s actual income of $85,000. Their combined incomes exceed the annual cap threshold.

Trish’s actual income for single periods is below the income threshold.

Jon and Trish’s actual combined income exceeded:

  • the estimate they provided, and
  • the annual cap threshold for the period that they were partnered

This means for partnered periods any CCS received in excess of the annual cap and due to incorrect estimation of income will be raised as an overpayment. For single periods, Trish would have no portion of the debt attributed to the annual cap, but will have a debt due to the incorrect estimation of her income.

3

Annual Cap 2019-20 or 2020-21 CCS year

Allan is CCS current and starts the year single, with an income estimate of $90,000.

Allan meets Julie and they move in together. Allan contacts and advise of the new relationship. Allan provides a new individual estimate of $100,000, and an estimate for Julie of $60,000. Allan and Julie’s new combined income estimate is now $160,000.

Allan and Julie separate. Allan updates the individual estimate back to $100,000.

Reconciliation occurs and both Allan and Julie earned more money in the year than anticipated, pushing the family’s income over the annual cap threshold for periods when they were together. But for periods where Allan was single, Allan is below the cap.

Because Allan and Julie’s total family income exceeded the annual cap threshold during their partnered period, any CCS received above the cap amount during the partnered period is raised as a debt.

For the period after where Allan’s income was under the annual cap threshold, Allan can continue to receive CCS even when the total amount received in the year exceeds the annual cap. This is because Allan's individual income is under the annual cap income limit. Allan is only subject to assessment on his own income during single periods and is therefore entitled to the CCS received.

Example

Description

1

Part year partnered calculation

Tegan partnered to James from 1 July 2019 to 26 January 2020.

Tegan’s Estimate $45,000 - Actual income was $40,000

James’ Estimate $50,000 - Actual income was $50,000

Throughout the 2019-20 CCS year, they are assessed on the income estimates as provided.

I.e. $95,000 during the partnered period (Tegan $45,000 + James $50,000).

After they separate, Tegan is assessed on an individual income estimate of $45,000.

Reconciliation occurs when Services Australia receive both Tegan and James’s actual income.

Their respective incomes are combined and the corresponding CCS percentage applied for the fortnights they were together. Tegan’s own individual income and corresponding CCS percentage is used to calculate entitlement for the CCS fortnights during the periods that Tegan was single.

For Tegan, the income used for the period partnered to James1 July 2019 until 26 January 2020), was $90,000. Income used for period 27 January 2020 until 12 July 2020 was $40,000. Tegan has two different income-based assessments during the year instead of only one income assessment applied to the whole year.

Under the new changes from the 2019-20 year onwards, there is no apportioning or annualising of James’ income.

2

Deceased customer calculation

Saul and Peta are married. Saul is receiving CCS, but passes away on 3 September 2019. CCS cancels from the CCS Monday after this date.

Saul’s estate lodges the tax return for period 01 July 2019 until 2 September 2020. Saul’s actual income is assessed as $40,000 as advised by the Australian Tax Office (ATO).

Saul was alive for 63 days within the current financial year and his income for this time was $40,000.

This amount is annualised in the following calculation:

ATI x 365 / number of days the person was alive in financial year:

$40,000 x 365 / 63 = $231,746.03

This annualised amount is only applied from 1 July until the end of the CCS fortnight of Saul's date of death and used to reconcile his CCS.

Note: the annualisation of the income amount is the same whether the customer or their partner passes away during the year.

3

Deceased partner calculation

Leigh and Corey are partnered and Leigh is receiving CCS. Leigh’s income estimate is $45,000 and Corey’s income estimate is $90,000. Corey passes away on 12 October 2019. CCS is assessed on Leigh’s estimate of $45,000 only from the CCS Monday after this date.

Corey’s estate lodges the tax return for period 1 July 2019 until 11 October 2019. Corey’s actual income is assessed as $25,000 as advised by the Australian Taxation Office (ATO).

Corey was alive for 103 days within the 19/20 financial year and his income for this time was $25,000.

This amount is annualised in the following calculation:

ATI x 365 / 103 = $88,592.23

This annualised amount is compared to the estimate provided for the period Corey was alive and the most beneficial amount is used by the system to complete reconciliation. As Corey’s estimated income of $90,000 is higher than the annualised actual income amount $88592.23, the annualised amount is used in the calculation of reconciliation.

Note: This most beneficial amount comparison only applies to deceased partners, not to deceased customers.

3

Partner has passed away

The rules are slightly different in cases where the CCS customer’s partner passes away during the year; the deceased individual’s income is annualised before it is apportioned.

Customer was alive from 1 July 2018 to 27 January 2019.

This is 210 days.

Step 1 - Annualise income (based on the number of days)

Deceased individual Adjusted Taxable Income (ATI) $50,000

50,000/210 x 365 = $86,904

Step 2 - Assess income based on the number of CCS fortnights the customer was partnered/alive in the CCS year

The Deceased partner’s income is then assessed using the Ex-partner rules for CCS:

Deceased ATI $86,904

CCS fortnights in the period 1 July 2018 - 27 January 2019 = 15

15/26 x 100 = 57.69%

57.69% of $86,904 = $50,135

Total Income used for Reconciliation

Customer ATI = $30,000

Deceased Partners ATI = $50,135

Total ATI used for 2018-19 year = $80,135

4

Customer has passed away

Customer granted CCS 16 July 2018.

Customer income $80,000

Partner income $40,000

Customer deceased 1 October 2018.

This calculation is a two-step process:

Since the deceased was the CCS customer ($80,000), this ATI must be calculated by the deceased individual formula (as per Schedule 3, Clause 2(2) of A new Tax System (Family Assistance) Act 1999).

Income of individual to date of death x (Number of days in income year/Number of day individual was alive during income year).

$80K x (365/ 93) = $20,383.

Then the $40,000 for the partner must have the ex-partner formula applied (as per Schedule 3, Clause 3AA of the Assistance Act) because the partner was a TFN determination person.

A check of the calendar shows they were partnered for 5 CCS fortnights.

5/26 X $40,000 = $7962.30

Therefore, CCS claimant ATI ($20,383) plus Ex-partner ($7962.30) = $28,345

5

Customer has passed away and surviving partner claims CCS

Customer granted CCS 16 July 2018.

Customer income $80,000

Surviving partner income $40,000

Customer deceased 1 October 2018.

Surviving partner claims CCS 1 November 2018

This scenario is also a two-step process:

Income of individual to date of death x (Number of days in income year/Number of day individual was alive during income year).

$80K x (365/ 93) = $20,383.

The deceased CCS claimants partner’s ATI is $40K. This would be a new claim with no TFN determination person for the rest of the financial year, so there is no requirement for the part year partner formula, or any other apportionment to be applied.

Total income: $60,383
