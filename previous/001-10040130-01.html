This document outlines information for identifying, gathering and weighing evidence to establish the facts which are relevant to a compliance investigation.

Conducting an evidence-based compliance investigation

Compliance investigations are conducted by Participation Solution Team (PST)-skilled Service Officers and Service Officers with UNPP security resource. The aim of the compliance investigation is to determine whether a financial penalty should be applied following a reported incident of non-compliance with a compulsory requirement. During the investigation the Service Officer gathers sufficient evidence to determine the facts and considers relevant matters to make an evidence-based decision that applies legislation and policy consistently.

All compliance investigations include evidence from both the person who reported the failure or generated the investigation, and the job seeker. Whether any further evidence is required to reach a decision, depends on whether the 2 forms of primary evidence are:

  • clear and consistent, and
  • sufficient to support a decision about what is most likely to have occurred

Responsibility for gathering information and weighing evidence

The Participation Solution Team (PST)-skilled Service Officer or Service Officer with the UNPP security resource is responsible for:

  • identifying the information relevant to the reported non-compliance event and conducting an investigation focused on the relevant facts
  • giving the job seeker the opportunity to respond to any adverse claim that has been made against them and to disclose any further information they would like considered
  • making contact with the provider or relevant third party to assist in establishing the one singular version of events, where evidence provided by the job seeker contradicts the information reported in the non-compliance event
  • assessing and weighing the available information to reach a conclusion about what occurred, or in the case of conflicting information, what is more likely to have occurred
  • consultation with a PST-skilled Service Support Officer (SSO), social worker, specialist officer or Indigenous Cultural Consultant may be appropriate to establish the impact of job seeker circumstances on their capacity to comply and may be considered evidence to inform the decision

Substantiating facts

Information that is relevant when considering a non-compliance event should be substantiated with evidence. Evidence collected as part of any compliance investigation should:

  • be specific to the non-compliance event that has been reported
  • focus on the relevant matters that can be considered to make a determination under the applicable legislation and policy
  • be from a reliable source
  • be documented clearly and concisely, being sure to include how the evidence collected has informed the decision and if appropriate, the reasons certain facts were given more weight than others

Weighing the evidence

Assessing and weighing the evidence will help determine how much impact the evidence has on the investigation. Generally, evidence given the most weight in an investigation will be:

  • relevant to the incident
  • relevant to the rules governing the decision
  • specific
  • provided by a reliable source
  • able to be substantiated, and
  • would be viewed as credible by others

Gathering sufficient evidence

When information reported by a provider as a part of the non-compliance event and the statement made by the job seeker are consistent, the job seeker's verbal statement should be all that is required to establish what occurred and make a decision. This is because the events are not disputed.

When information is inconsistent, or the decision maker has concerns about what the most likely version of events is, more concrete evidence may be required.

Attempts to gather further evidence should only be made if the potential evidence is relevant and will influence the outcome of the decision. For example, when prior contact was required but did not occur, evidence to support non-compliance with the requirement would not be required if it was reasonable to expect prior contact as this would not change the outcome of the decision.

The compliance investigation should be completed on the day of contact, where possible. This is particularly important for decisions that may result in a financial penalty for a mutual obligation failure as job seekers cannot complete their reporting until the decision has been finalised. Mutual obligation failures cannot be held pending the return of evidence.

Generally evidence should be collected during the contact with the job seeker. This helps deliver timely decision making and prevents the job seeker from having to contact again. Preferably, third party contact should be made by phone during the initial contact with the job seeker to discuss relevant matters and clarify discrepancies. When the decision maker requires assistance assessing the impact of job seeker's circumstances on their capacity to meet a compulsory requirement, consultation with a Participation Solutions Team (PST)-skilled Service Support Officer (SSO), PST-skilled social worker or other relevant specialist is encouraged and can also be used as evidence to support the decision.

Balance of probability

If there is conflicting information and no further evidence is available, a decision must be based on the balance of probability.

That means, if it is more probable than not that the non-compliance event, including failure to make prior contact, occurred without good reason then a failure has been committed and compliance action will apply.

Compliance action will not apply if, based on the established facts, it is more probable:

  • a non-compliance event did not occur, or
  • a job seeker had reasonable excuse, and
  • prior contact requirements were met, or it was unreasonable to expect prior contact to be made

Recording evidence relevant to the decision

All information relevant to the investigation and decision must be documented in the Participation Compliance workflow. This is an essential part of transparent decision making and ensures the integrity of the job seeker compliance system.

Documentation must include a sufficient level of detail to ensure someone reviewing the decision will be able to identify:

  • the facts that were considered relevant to the decision and the supporting evidence, and
  • the manner and rationale used in weighing the evidence
