Considering if a customer is a MoC - 5 factors

Table 1: Further help for staff to guide staff in determining if a person is a MoC/SUOR or not.

  • Not all factors need to be present and a customer’s holistic circumstances need to be considered after assessing the five factors
  • In determining that customer is a MoC, assessors must be satisfied that indicators of a relationship outweigh the indicators that no relationship exists
  • It is also important to remember that the presence of family and domestic violence may indicate a customer is not a MoC
  • An interview may be required to give the customer and the other person/ex-partner to provide additional information, their perspective on the relationship and any other supporting information they may have. For example, where:
    • the customer and other person/ex-partner have provided conflicting information in forms, or
    • there is doubt about the validity of information received if the same handwriting has been used on both forms

Item

Description

1

Financial aspects of the relationship

Financial aspects relate to the degree of financial interdependence. A degree of financial interdependence may be indicative of one person providing financial support for the other.

SS284/SS293 form(s) - review responses to the set of questions asked in the ‘Financial arrangements’ section.

Points to consider

Joint ownership of real estate or other major assets and any joint liabilities:

  • If the customer and other person/ex-partner jointly own real estate or other major assets, are there plans to separate these? If not, is the continuation reasonable, or indicative of a MoC relationship
  • For SUOR assessments, consider if property settlement proceedings have begun. This may demonstrate the customer is taking steps to sever the relationship
  • If the customer has joint accounts with the other person/ex-partner, what is the purpose of the joint account and are there plans to separate this account.
    Note: a joint account for the sole purposes of paying rent and utilities is reasonable and does not necessarily indicate a MoC relationship exists
  • Is there any outstanding joint debts. If yes, consider the arrangement(s) for repayment and/or separating the debt

Financial support for each other:

  • Does the customer pay rent or board and/or lodgings where they currently live? If not, why not - are they being supported by the other person/ex-partner. If so, how are the accommodation expenses split
  • Does the other person have access to the account into which the Centrelink payment is paid
  • Check how loans are repaid. Whose money is used. Bank statements may be helpful here if direct debit from an account is used and may demonstrate transfer of funds between two people; if funds are being transferred, why is this occurring
  • Is there evidence of sharing or splitting bills. For example, mortgage repayments, rent, utility bills, food. Note: if it’s indicated one person owns the home, it would be reasonable to expect the other person is paying for board and/or lodgings; if it’s a joint mortgage, it’s reasonable to expect the repayments are equally split. If not, consideration on why would be required to assist in forming an opinion about the relationship.
  • If joint assets were purchased, why did this occur. Is this a reasonable expectation or is the purchase indicative of a MoC relationship.

Legal obligations owed by one person for the other:

  • If joint insurance policies exist, why does the customer share the insurance policies. Are there plans to cancel or alter them. This may demonstrate the customer is taking steps to severe the relationship (for SUOR circumstances)

Sharing day to day expenses:

  • Are the household bills split equally
  • Who pays for food expenses

General

Financial aspects can be important, but financial independence does not rule out a MoC relationship. There is an increasing trend for couples to maintain separate finances. However, most couples are likely to share finances in some way.

2

Nature of the household

Nature of the household includes the physical set-up of the household e.g. shared quarters, arrangements for domestic tasks, arrangements for joint care of children.

SS284/SS293 form(s) - review responses to the set of questions asked in the ‘Nature of the household’ section.

Points to consider

Joint responsibility for providing care and support for children:

  • If there are children regularly living or staying at the address, do both parties look after the children? For SUOR assessments, is there a child support arrangement in place? If not, consider the customer’s response as to why they haven’t made any child support arrangements

Living arrangements:

  • If the parties share a bedroom, consider the customer’s explanation on why
  • Responsibility for household task: How are the household chores arranged. Does one person do all the cleaning or washing for the other person
  • Who does the general home maintenance and gardening
  • Are the household chores rostered? People who are not in a relationship will often have a roster or formal agreement for sharing tasks

For responses on the forms, it is reasonable to expect the following:

  • I do = customer does it all
  • Other person = the sharer/ex-partner does it all
  • We both do = split equally
  • Third party = neither do it and a third party does it all

3

Social aspects of the relationship

These details take into account how the parties present themselves to society, how others in society view their relationship.

SS284/SS293 form(s) - review responses to the set of questions asked in the ‘Social Aspects’ section.

Points to consider

Presenting themselves as a couple:

  • Has one or both of them described themselves as a partner or de facto to the other person when completing any application forms (loans, insurance, employment etc.)
  • Do they present themselves to the community as a couple or friends
  • Have they presented themselves as being in a relationship to third parties such as landlords or business owners/other agencies

Assessment of friends and regular associates about the nature of the relationship:

If the customer indicates relatives, friends or regular associates consider them to be partnered, consider why. There may be cultural or religious factors to consider, for example, it is shameful for the customer to be considered separated. They may not want their children to know about the separation.

Joint social activities:

  • Do they share social and leisure activities. If yes, how often
  • If interviewing the customer, ask why they share these activities. Are they simply on the same sporting team, or is it because they take both attend their children’s sporting activities. This is to help obtain as much objective information as possible

4

Any sexual relationship

The presence of a sexual relationship does not prove or disprove a MoC relationship. This aspect of the relationship must be taken into account with the degree of emotional support and other forms of interdependence and commitment as demonstrated with the other 4 factors.

SS284/SS293 form(s) - review response to the question asked in the ‘Sexual relationship’ section

Points to consider

Admission of a sexual relationship

  • Is there an admission of a sexual relationship between the parties to the relationship
  • Check the record for known information which may indicate the presence of a sexual relationship, such as if the customer has had any children with the other person whilst claiming they are not a member of a couple

5

Nature of the commitment

SS284/SS293 form(s) - review responses to the questions asked in the ‘Nature of commitment’ section.

Determine if there is an emotional attachment that differs to the commitment of either party to anyone else at that time. Or, that differs to relationships with close relatives, friends or co-tenants:

  • The SS284 or SS293 may provide details of the nature of the commitment, but the customer's responses may need verifying

Points to consider

Each person's perception of the relationship

  • How does each person describe their relationship. This can be checked by their response to the question in the form
  • How does each person think their relationship differs with that of a married, registered or de facto couple

Length of the relationship:

  • Consider how long the parties intend to share accommodation with the other person
  • Is there a history of sharing accommodation (check historical Accommodation Details, Sharer Details and Living Arrangements pages)? If so, is any explanation reasonable. For example, affordability or is there indications there may be a MoC relationship
  • Consider if the length they intend to share is ‘reasonable’. For example, if the customer indicates intent to share accommodation until the end of lease however the lease term is less than the length noted, this may need to investigation. Or it may be reasonable to continue sharing because they can’t afford to move out; they may continue to share for extended periods due to affordability issues
  • If interviewing the customer, ask how long the parties have known each other

Companionship and emotional support:

  • Is one of the parties listed as an emergency contact person for employment. If so, is the explanation reasonable. For example, there may be no one else available locally
  • Do the parties have a mutual commitment to the relationship? What is the strength of emotional ties
  • Is there evidence of companionship and emotional support for each other? This includes caring for the other person when they are ill or in a personal crisis. Note that people in share households and those caring for a person with a disability may provide different levels of companionship and emotional support which may not be indicative of a MoC relationship.
  • In Fraud cases only: if interviewing third parties such as family and friends, obtain their views

Stability of the relationship:

  • Is there any evidence of family and domestic violence. For example, court documents, social worker DOCs which may indicate the absence of commitment or emotional support
  • Is there evidence that either party exerts influence over the other person's long term plans or decisions
  • For SUOR assessments, has either party indicated they may get back together? If so, this may need further investigation to determine there’s been a genuine separation
  • If interviewing the customer, ask how long do they intend to continue the relationship? Have they shared addresses or separated in the past? Check their records for evidence

Guidelines - recording a MoC decision DOC

Table 2

Item

Description

1

What must be included?

Details should be as brief as possible. Outline key details that explain the basis of the decision.

Details of any conversation held with a person (including a third party) must be a true reflection of the discussion.

The MoC decision DOC must include the following details:

  • outcome of the decision, e.g. 'A decision has been made that (Person 1) and (Person 2) are Separated Under One Roof/are in a Member of a Couple relationship'
  • date of the MoC decision
  • evidence used to support the findings and basis of the decision. Include details of any conversations with the customer or ex-partner)
  • any further action/follow up required

See Creating documents (Fast Notes, Callbacks and ACTDOCs) for more help.

2

What must not be included?

MoC decisions are based on details provided by a customer (and other parties) on the SS293/SS284 forms and supporting information.

So it is important not to repeat at length details available on the record.

If details on the record are relevant to the decision, it is enough to include a key statement referring to them. For example: 'The claimant and other person do not have any joint financial commitments as per details in SS293 forms from both parties and the SIS screen on both records.'

The MoC decision DOC does not need to include:

  • repeated (word for word) details that can be viewed on the record via Document Tools. This includes scanned SS284/SS293 forms
  • sensitive or personal details not related to the enquiry
  • personal bias

See Creating documents (Fast Notes, Callbacks and ACTDOCs) for more help.

Scenario - determining a person's relationship

Table 3

Item

Description

1

Member of a couple

Jenny is a single Parenting Payment (PP) customer, separated from partner Rodney and has with 2 dependent children.

Rodney does not pay Jenny any child support and gets JobSeeker Payment (JSP). Rodney gets occasional work and has just lodged an Application for Payment (SU19) with the same address on it as Jenny's.

At the interview, Jenny states that Rodney stays over most nights to see the kids. Rodney's name is still on the lease because it is too hard to change it with the real-estate agents. Rodney also takes the children to school and watches them play sport on the weekends. When staying over, Rodney sleeps in Jenny's bed as it is only a 2 bedroom house.

Jenny does not consider they are living as a member of a couple as they have no joint bank accounts and Jenny pays all the bills. Rodney gives Jenny some money occasionally towards the bills.

They do not go out much, but when they do it is usually down to the local club with some friends. Jenny does not think that they are back together and does not want to pressure Rodney too much.

Considering the information relevant to the customer's relationship, it would appear that a member of a couple relationship does exist. There is a joint lease, the couple have a joint social life and both take responsibility for the custody and control of the children.

Examples - past period assessments

Table 4

Item

Description

1

Claim for payment

1 January 2017

John applies for JobSeeker Payment and advises he and Alicia are no longer a couple. However, they both live in the family home as they share caring responsibilities for their son Barron.

15 February 2017

John returns the Relationship details - Separated under one roof (SS293) form.

28 March 2017

John advises that Alicia and Barron have moved out of the family home 3 days ago.

1 April 2017

  • Decision 1 - John is a member of a couple from 1 January 2017 to 27 March 2017, and
  • Decision 2 - John is not a member of a couple from 28 March 2017

Therefore, John is granted JobSeeker Payment:

  • At the partnered rate from 1 January 2017, being the customer’s start day under the usual rules
  • At the single rate from 28 March 2017:

This is a favourable decision. It results in a rate increase and starts on the date John advised the change

2

On payment at member of a couple rate

1 January 2017

John and Alicia each get JobSeeker Payment as members of a couple.

1 February 2017

Alicia advises they have separated but they are still living together and caring for their son Barron.

15 February 2017

John returns the Relationship details - Separated under one roof (SS293) form.

28 March 2017

Alicia advises they (Alicia and child Barron) moved out of the family home 3 days ago.

1 April 2017

  • Decision 1 - Alica and John are a member of a couple from 1 February 2017 to 24 March 2017, and
  • Decision 2 - Alicia and John are not a member of a couple from 25 March 2017

Therefore, John and Alicia’s rate of JobSeeker Payment is:

  • Continued at the partnered rate between 1 February 2017 and 27 March 2017
  • At the single rate from 28 March 2017. This is a favourable decision. It results in a rate increase. Alicia advised within 14 days (in accordance with her section 68 notice). As per Notification Handler (NOHL) rules, the date of effect for the rate increase is the date of receipt (the date the change was advised)

3

On payment at single rate

1 January 2017

Alicia gets JobSeeker Payment at the single rate and lives with child Barron.

13 February 2017

Alicia advises of moving in with a former partner and other parent of the child on 1 February 2017.

18 February 2017

Alicia returns the Relationship details - Separated under one roof (SS293) form.

28 March 2017

Alicia advises they (Alicia and Barron) moved out of the family home today.

1 April 2017:

  • Decision 1 - Alicia is not a member of a couple from 1 February 2017 to 27 March 2017, and
  • Decision 2 - Alicia is not a member of a couple from 28 March 2017

Therefore, Alicia can continue on JobSeeker Payment at the single rate.

This is a favourable decision. Alicia rate will continue at the single rate.

Past period assessment not needed - new claim lodgement

2 January 2019

Margaret has recently became unemployed and moved in with some friends to reduce accommodation costs.

6 January 2019

Margaret is getting FTB and lodges a claim for PPS. When lodging the claim it is identified that Margaret has lived at a previous address with one of the friends. Therefore, a Member of a Couple assessment (SS284 form) is required.

11 January 2019

Margaret contacts to advise the other person has moved out of the home they were sharing on 11 January 2019

There is now no need for a MoC assessment as the change occurred within 14 days of the claim lodgement.

5

Past period assessment needed - new claim lodgement

2 January 2019

John separates from partner Tyrell and moves in with some friends pending more permanent accommodation.

6 January 2019

John lodges a claim for JobSeeker Payment (JSP). A Member of a Couple assessment (SS284 form) is required because John previously shared accommodation with one of the friends.

25 January 2019

John contacts to advise of moving out of the home they were sharing on 22 January 2019.

A MoC assessment is needed for the period 2-22 January 2019 as it has been more than 14 days since claim lodgement and moving from the address.

6

Past period assessment not needed - customer getting payments

1 February 2019

Michael contacts within the notification period to advise changing address. As Michael indicated sharing with other people, the LA Question Set is completed. This triggers a MoC assessment because Michael has lived at a previous address with one of the sharers, Jill.

There are no safety concerns identified, so a request to complete and return the SS284 form is issued to both Michael and Jill.

5 February 2019

Jill moves out of the address and is no longer sharing with Michael.

13 February 2019

Michael contacts again to advise neither of them have completed or returned their SS284 forms because Jill is no longer living at the same address and moved out on 5 February.

There is now no need for a MoC assessment because Jill moved out of the house and Michael advised the change within 14 days of the date they started living together.
