Examples of multiple SpB rejections and reasons

Table 1

Item

Reason for rejection

1

Single customer who holds a Bridging Visa 050 lodges a claim for Special Benefit (SpB). They have available funds of $10,000.

Hierarchy of rejection will be NTV (No Permanent Visa) as customer is not residentially qualified.

Irrespective of the available funds the customer has, the claim will be rejected NTV as the customer is not a resident and holder of a temporary visa.

2

Partnered customer who holds a subclass 189 Skilled Independent Visa serving Newly Arrived Resident’s Waiting Period (NARWP) lodges a claim for Special Benefit (SpB).

Both customer and partner have available funds of $15,000 at the date of claim.

Partner is a full-time employee and earns gross income of $2,000 per fortnight.

The system determines the most appropriate rejection reason (based on hierarchy).

If a Service Officer notes more than one reason could result in the rejection of SpB claim, they must:

  • explain to the customer all of the reasons their claim would be rejected
  • clearly document all the rejection reasons on the customer record

Within this scenario there are four possible rejection reasons that could be applied:

  • The primary rejection reason would be REJ/INE (partner exceeds income). This is an auto rejection
  • HAR (Not Considered in Hardship) - customer has available funds of more than $5,000 at the claim lodgement date
  • NWA (NARWP ASSESSMENT NOT MET *AUTO), and
    NW2 (RESIDENCY LESS THAN 104 WEEKS *AUTO)
  • NCC - Migrant (2 year WP) - No circ change
    NCA - Newly Arrived Resident's Waiting Period (NARWP) assessment not met (4 year WP) this will be a manual rejection

Substantial change in circumstances

Examples where SpB would not be payable include:

  • If a customer's available funds were depleted due to the purchase of a house or vehicle, the customer has not suffered a change of circumstances beyond their control
  • If a person is granted a permanent visa offshore and has been offered employment in Australia to start upon arrival. If the offer of employment is withdrawn before arriving in Australia, this is not considered a substantial change of circumstances
  • While there is an expectation that a claimant (and their partner) are responsible for the decisions affecting their individual situations, mitigating circumstances may exist that need more deliberation when assessing an individual's claim for SpB. The delegate has the authority to consider all available information regarding the claimant's individual circumstances before deciding if there was a substantial change in circumstances that was beyond the person's control
  • The SpB claimant and the partner decide to have a child during the NARWP period. The birth of a child (including multiple births) is not considered to be a substantial change in circumstances. However, discretions should be applied if there are homeless considerations and each case should be reviewed individually

Changes not considered substantial for SpB

The following situations would not be considered a substantial change in circumstances for the purpose of qualification for SpB - where no other extenuating circumstances are involved.

  • Claimant and partner are separated but living under one roof
  • Partner and the visa holder separate due to breakdown of the relationship and there is no domestic violence involved
  • Partner misrepresents their circumstances (financial, physical, material) and the partnered provisional visa holder chooses to leave because of this
  • Partner leaves work to study or train, engages in an unprofitable business, resorts to excessive gambling or makes another lifestyle choice that impacts on the couple financially
  • A claim is lodged after 1 January 2012 and the person states they are unaware of the legislative change that removed family member as an exemption from the NARWP for partnered provisional visa holders

The amount of $2,500 is considered committed funds as they are to be used for immediate future exceptional costs.

Victims of disasters

If a SpB claimant appears to be residentially qualified for the Disaster Recovery Allowance (DRA) and their principal home is within the area of a declared natural disaster, the SpB claimant must have a claim for DRA determined before an assessment of SpB can be completed.

If eligible for DRA, the customer's claim for SpB must be rejected.

If the customer's claim for DRA is rejected, the claim for SpB may continue to be assessed.

An appropriate reason for which SpB might be granted when the customer's claim for DRA is rejected is where a customer's principal home has been significantly damaged or destroyed but does not fall within an area officially declared a natural disaster zone.

The short-term available funds test and the direct deduction income test need to be applied to determine eligibility, however, the assets test is applied with consideration of whether the person's assets are accessible at short notice. Minimum or alternative identity confirmation requirements are to be met.

SpB for illegal maritime arrivals

People whose immigration status is being resolved may be eligible for the Status Resolution Support Services (SRSS) Payment. Services Australia administers the SRSS payment on behalf of the Department of Home Affairs. The SRSS payment stops when a temporary protection visa (for example, 785 and 790) is granted.

In these circumstances, customers should be encouraged to test their eligibility for SpB.

See Item 5 in Table 2 in Intent to claim and vulnerable customers.

If the SRSS customer is granted a permanent visa, for example 866, they are not eligible for SpB, as they should meet the residence requirements for all other social security payments when the SRSS payment stops.
