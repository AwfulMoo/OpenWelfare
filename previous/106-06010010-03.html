Assessing a customer's legal status

Table 1: assessing if a customer has the required legal residence status for payment.

Step

Action

1

Obtaining and recording residence information + Read more ...

All claim forms, including online claims, contain the necessary residence questions. Customers may need to answer those questions and provide verification such as a passport or Australian citizenship certificate.

The Immigration Datalink provides information to Services Australia about a customer or child's visa, citizenship (if available) and movement information. If the Immigration Datalink has provided the information, it will be on the customer or child's record on the relevant screens. If applicable, the system will request the Immigration Datalink for the partner, any children and care receivers.

Information provided by the Immigration Datalink will take precedence over any conflicting information provided by the customer.

The system will assess whether the customer meets the residence rules as long as the residence information is recorded correctly. This applies for all payments and concessions except for AGDRP, NZ DRP, NZ DRA and PLDP. For those payments, staff must manually check if the customer meets the residence rules and manually grant or reject the payment.

While the system can determine the customer's legal resident status based on information from the Immigration Datalink and coded by the Service Officer, Service Officers will still have to manually assess and record what country the customer is residing in.

Make sure the following screens have been updated correctly (for help, see Residence and Portability screens):

  • Immigration Enquiry (RSIMME/CHIMME) screen
    • Country of birth
    • Lived or travelled outside Australia since 1 Sept 1994: field
    • Passports and travel documents
  • Legal Residence (RSLEG/CHRSLEG) screen
    • Citizenship
    • Visas (if applicable)
    • New Zealand residence status (if applicable)
  • Immigration Advised Movements (RSIM/CHRSIM) screen
    • If linked with immigration, check the Immigration Limiting Date (ILD) field to see if the default date needs to be adjusted
    • Immigration advised movements (if applicable)
  • Country of Residence (CRES/CHCRES) screen
    • Country where the customer/child is living and countries they have lived previously. Note: if a child was born overseas to Australian resident parents, the country of residence will default to the child's country of birth. See Recording residence details for dependent children for correct coding)
    • Check and update if necessary

If the residence information was recorded from a previous claim, only update it if new information is available.

If a link with immigration is expected but not yet made, and there is no related Immigration Datalink MFU on the AL screen, request an immigration datalink via the RSIMME/CHIMME screen.

If the information supplied by the datalink appears incomplete or incorrect, see Referring inconsistencies in overseas departure and arrival information to Centrelink International Services (CIS).

The customer may have already provided necessary documents when confirming their identity or if asked to provide proof of their Australian residence status (e.g. citizenship papers, passport or other documentation) as part of their claim.

Has all necessary residence information been obtained and coded?

This includes visa, passport/travel documents and citizenship details.

  • No, go to Step 2

3

Check citizenship + Read more ...

If verification of Australian citizenship is required, error E520RS will display. Exception: Family assistance payments will not display an error, a manual check is needed.

Verification can be received in some cases through the Immigration Datalink. For example:

  • If the customer has travelled on an Australian passport this will be updated via the datalink and recorded on the Immigration Enquiry (RSIMME) screen with IMM channel
  • If they have been granted Australian citizenship since 2001, this will be updated to the Legal Residence Details (RSLEG) screen with IMM channel

When datalink information does not verify Australian citizenship, check the documents the customer supplied for identity or that were requested as part of their claim. These can include:

  • Australian birth certificate
  • Australian passport
  • Citizenship (naturalisation) certificates

For more information, see Proof of Australian citizenship.

Note: it may not be necessary for a customer/partner to be Australian citizens to meet the legal permanent resident part of ‘Australian Resident’. If verification is required but has not been provided by the customer, consider if Australian citizenship is required. For example, if the customer also holds a permanent visa or a protected SCV, this may be enough for them to qualify, keeping in mind the NARWP. If so, remove Australian citizenship from the RSLEG screen.

Is the customer an Australian citizen?

  • Yes, the customer holds permanent legal resident status
    • If born in Australia, see Step 1 in Table 4 to assess if they are residing in Australia
    • if born overseas, date of arrival/start of living in Australia needs to be verified. See Step 1 in Table 2
  • No or unsure, go to Step 4

Check if the customer is the holder of a permanent visa + Read more ...

Does the customer hold an Australian permanent resident visa?

For information about visas, see the Social Security Guide, Chapter 9 Visas & Entitlements.

  • Yes, the customer holds permanent legal resident status
    • If born in Australia, see Step 1 in Table 4 to assess if they are residing in Australia
    • if born overseas, date of arrival/start of living in Australia needs to be verified. See Step 1 in Table 2
  • No or unsure, go to Step 5

5

Check if the customer is a protected Special Category visa (SCV) holder + Read more ...

See, Australian Residence Rules for New Zealand citizens for more information.

New Zealand passport holders are generally issued an SCV subclass 444 when they enter Australia. SCVs are not stamped in passports. In most cases, the system will determine if the customer is a protected or non-protected SCV holder during a new claim when the Immigration Datalink is activated.

The customer's visa (subclass 444) and New Zealand Residence Status (protected or non-protected) will be recorded on the RSLEG screen. If the status is:

  • GRA (grandfatherred) or TRA (transitional), the customer has a protected SCV
  • NOT, the customer is not a protected SCV holder

If the datalink is not linked, or New Zealand residence status needs to be assessed manually:

  • The system sends an activity to Centrelink International Services (CIS). They will check the status and code the relevant details
  • Code a manual review for 14 days to check coding of the New Zealand residence status by CIS. When the detail is coded, continue the process.

Is the customer a protected SCV holder?

  • Yes, the customer has the equivalent of a permanent visa
    • If born in Australia, see Step 1 in Table 4 to assess if they are residing in Australia
    • If born overseas, date of arrival/start of living in Australia needs to be verified. See Step 1 in Table 2
  • No, the customer is a non-protected SCV holder or holds a different temporary visa. Go to Step 6

6

Check if the customer is a non-protected SCV holder (for certain claims) + Read more ...

Is the customer the holder of a non-protected SCV and only claiming parental leave payments, family assistance or concession cards (other than Ex-Carer Allowance Heath Care Card (EHC), NZ DRP or NX DRA?

  • Yes, the customer meets legal residence status for those payments
    • If born in Australia, see Step 1 in Table 4 to assess if they are residing in Australia
    • If born overseas, date of arrival/start of living in Australia needs to be verified. See Step 1 in Table 2
  • No,
    • For a non-protected SCV holder claiming JSP or YA, go to Step 7
    • For all other customers, go to Step 8

Note: a non-protected SCV holder may also qualify for a payment under the Agreement with New Zealand.

7

Check if the customer has lived in Australia continuously for at least 10 years and is now residing in Australia + Read more ...

Is the customer currently residing in Australia and have they been living in Australia continuously for at least 10 years at the time of claim?

  • Yes, the customer may qualify for JSP or YA under the New Zealand 10 year residence exemption. Note: a non-protected SCV holder can only receive this exemption for JSP/YA once, and the NARWP does not apply under this exemption
    • If born in Australia, see Step 1 in Table 4 to assess if they are residing in Australia
    • If born overseas, verify the date of arrival/started living in Australia. See Step 1 in Table 2
  • No, go to Step 8

8

Check residence in an agreement country + Read more ...

Has the customer lived or worked in an agreement country, or is currently living in an agreement country?

For information on agreement countries, see International Social Security Agreements.

  • Yes, the customer may qualify for a social security pension under an international agreement, but they must still be residing in Australia or an agreement country. See Step 1 in Table 4 to assess what country the customer is residing in
  • No, go to Step 9

9

Check if the customer holds a temporary visa that qualifies them for some payments + Read more ...

Is the customer the holder of a temporary visa that qualifies customers for some payments and claiming Special Benefit (SpB), Child Care Subsidy (CCS), parental leave payments, family assistance or a Low Income Card (LIC)?

  • Yes, the customer meets legal residence requirements. See Step 1 in Table 4 to assess if they are residing in Australia
  • No, go to Step 10

10

Eligibility for Child Care Subsidy (CCS) + Read more ...

A customer claiming CCS only may qualify if:

  • they have a partner who is:
    • an Australian resident
    • a Special Category visa (SCV) holder, or
    • the holder of a temporary visa that qualifies customers for some payments either in Australia or temporarily absent less than 6 weeks for an approved reason
  • they or their partner is a temporary visa holder who is suffering temporary hardship, or
  • special circumstances exist

For help assessing hardship or special circumstances, see Residence assessment for customers claiming CCS.

Is the customer claiming CCS only and one of the above applies?

  • Yes, the customer may qualify for CCS. See Step 1 in Table 4 to assess if they are residing in Australia
  • No,
    • If the customer or their partner is undertaking a course of study in Australia for which they receive financial assistance from the Commonwealth, the customer may still satisfy residence requirements. See Residence assessment for customers claiming CCS
    • Otherwise, the customer is not residentially qualified for any payment. See Step 2 in Table 5 to finalise the activity

Assessing residing in Australia

Table 4: assessing if a person is living in Australia.

Step

Action

1

Consider the balance between the person's ties in Australia and their ties with another country + Read more ...

Contact the customer when considering their circumstances to make a correct assessment. While a factor may indicate they are not residing in Australia, exceptional circumstances may prove otherwise. Contacting the customer will give them the chance to comment. It will also give the chance to advise them of any adverse decision and the potential impact on their payment.

Determining if a customer is a resident of Australia is a discretionary decision. Document the reasons for the decision, including any discussion with the customer. Do not change discretionary decisions unless there is evidence the customer misrepresented their circumstances when the original decision was made, or a significant error about facts requires correction. Changes in circumstances or discovery of factual error may be a reason to make a new decision about where the customer is residing. However, do not backdate these changes unless there is evidence the customer misrepresented their circumstances when the original decision was made.

A customer's presence in Australia or their statement they intend to reside in Australia is not sufficient evidence that they are residing in Australia. If their intent is to stay for a period to lodge a valid claim and meet qualification criteria for a social security payment and then leave Australia to resume residence in another country, do not regard them as being an Australian resident.

Consider the customer's answers in each of the steps below as well as any other relevant information when making a decision.

For help, use the Residence assessment templates on the International Programme home page under the Residence heading. The Resources page contains a link and examples of decisions.

2

Frequency and duration of travel outside Australia + Read more ...

Where is the person spending most of their time?

What is the reason for being overseas for long periods?

A person does not have to be continuously present in Australia to be residing here. A person holidaying or working temporarily overseas does not necessarily cease to reside in Australia while they are away.

Find the reason for being overseas and look closely at the pattern and time spent outside Australia to decide if a person continues to reside in Australia. To maintain Australian residence during an absence, a person must demonstrate continued physical ties to Australia:

  • the absence must be for a short time
  • there must be a purpose for the absence, and
  • there must be a proposed end date for the absence

For example, a person working on a 2 year overseas contract posting is considered to reside in Australia as long as they have demonstrated ongoing physical ties to Australia and a commitment to return to Australia at the end of the posting.

Note: long periods outside Australia do not preclude a person from still being a resident of Australia. However, taken in isolation a 3 year continuous absence is regarded as an upper limit to still being considered residing in Australia, unless there are special circumstances delaying a return. When looking at the pattern and duration of time spent outside Australia, if a person regularly spends more than 6 months a year outside Australia, their residence in Australia is questionable.

3

Nature of the accommodation + Read more ...

What is the nature of the accommodation used by the person in Australia compared to that outside Australia?

Consider, for both accommodation in Australia and outside Australia, if the customer:

  • has their own home
  • rents accommodation
  • lives with family members
  • lives with friends

For example, owning a home outside Australia that is not being rented, and staying in hostel accommodation in Australia could indicate the customer is living in the other country.

4

Nature and extent of the family relationships + Read more ...

What is the nature and extent of the family relationships the person has in Australia compared to those outside Australia?

For family ties in Australia and outside Australia, consider:

  • where the customer's immediate relatives live (give extra weight to where dependent children and/or their partner live); and
  • with whom they spend the most time

For example, siblings or grown up children in Australia and a partner and/or dependent child outside Australia could indicate stronger ties to the other country.

5

Employment in Australia + Read more ...

Does the customer or their partner have employment in Australia or in the other country?

If so, do they have any proof, e.g. a tax return?

Ongoing work is a very strong indicator they are living in the country of employment.

6

Operating a business + Read more ...

Is the customer or their partner operating a business in Australia or in the other country?

Consider if members of their family jointly operate the business with them in Australia or in the other country.

A business or similar (e.g. partnership, farm) is a very strong indicator they are living in the relevant country.

7

Investments + Read more ...

Where does the customer hold their investments?

Obtain details of shares, bank accounts and other financial investments. Consider the location of the institutions and the currency of the investments.

Extensive assets outside Australia can indicate residence in the other country.

For example, if a customer has $1,000 in an Australian bank account and US$45,000 in a Thai bank account, it indicates the customer's residence is in Thailand.

8

Comparison of assets + Read more ...

What assets are owned in Australia compared to the assets owned outside Australia?

Consider whether the customer:

  • owns or partly owns any real estate in either country
  • owns property from which rent is received
  • owns property in which children live rent free

9

Travel between Australia and another country + Read more ...

The Immigration Datalink will provide details of travel since 1 September 1994 for most customers. The system will record movement information on the Immigration Advised Movements (RSIM) screen.

Check the Time Inside/Outside Australia (TIO) screen in Customer First to compare time spent in Australia to time spent overseas. Move the Immigration Limiting Date back far enough to include all the movements of interest on the RSIM screen.

Things to consider include:

  • the time spent in Australia on each trip
  • the time spent outside Australia
  • the number of times the customer travels
  • the reasons, if any, for periods spent outside Australia
  • whether the customer appears to spend much more time in one country than the other

10

Any other details relevant to the customer's intention to remain in Australia + Read more ...

The following examples may help to determine where a customer is living.

  • During the conversation the customer may have indicated they will only be staying in Australia long enough to qualify for a portable social security payment and then leave Australia. As the customer does not intend to live in Australia (make Australia their 'home') they would not be regarded as a resident for social security purposes. The customer is therefore not qualified for many social security payments
  • The customer may have arrived in Australia on a return ticket, indicating they may leave Australia soon. A return ticket will usually indicate lack of intention to remain in Australia
  • The person may have committed themselves or their children to long-term education in Australia or in another country
  • Anything the customer says about their long term intentions can be useful. If they have returned to Australia and state an intention to live here, what arrangements have they made to support this?

Note: regard customers who have been long term residents when they lodged their claim (such as residing in Australia for the past 20 years) as residing in Australia, even if they intend to leave Australia to live in another country in the near future.

Record the country in which the customer is residing + Read more ...

Record the country on the CRES screen.

Finalise residence activity

Table 5

Step

Action

1

The customer has either legal resident status or a residence exemption and is residing in Australia + Read more ...

The customer can therefore lodge a valid claim for payment (subject to meeting the lodgement requirements). However, the customer must also satisfy payment specific qualifications, e.g. qualifying residence period, NARWP, principal carer of a dependent child and/or income and assets tests.

Note: a customer who is a non-protected SCV holder who qualifies for JobSeeker Payment (JSP) or Youth Allowance (YA) under the New Zealand 10 year residence exemption is not subject to a NARWP.

Once the RSIMME, CRES, RSLEG and RSIM screens are complete, the system will generally assess if a customer is a legal resident and/or has any NARWP or qualifying residence period, including if certain qualifying residence exemptions apply.

However, the system will not assess all the qualifying residence exemptions for a NARWP or qualifying residence period. A special field must be coded if the customer is:

  • the family member of a refugee or a former refugee
  • claiming Parenting Payment Single (PPS), JSP, FHA or YA (Job seeker) and became a lone principal carer of a dependent child since the start of the current period as an Australian resident
  • claiming SpB and have had an unforeseen change in circumstances
  • claiming Mobility Allowance (MOB) and the event occurred while an Australian resident, or
  • claiming Carer Payment (CP) and is a special needs relative

Go to Step 3.

2

Customer is not residentially qualified for any payment + Read more ...

The customer is either not a legal resident of Australia or is not residing in Australia.

Note: residence qualifications and associated exemptions are complex. Encourage the customer to lodge a claim for payment. Record the information and reject the claim if the customer is not qualified for payment from a residential perspective.

Record details on the RSLEG and CRES screens. The system will reject the claim for the correct residence related reason if the customer does not meet residence qualification rules.

If the customer may be entitled to payment under an international agreement, refer the claim to CIS:

  • code all residence screens
  • Confirm the customer's identity
  • Ensure only required work items are left open
  • Take claim off hold and categorise in Work Optimiser to a CIS new claim work item

No separate referral is necessary.

Procedure ends here.
