For Services Australia social workers only

This page contains a table and information about SpB claim types, where social worker involvement is mandatory or recommended.

Process when SpB has been claimed

This table describes when social worker involvement is required for a SpB claim.

Item

Description

1

Customers under 16 years of age + Read more ...

All SpB claims for customers under 16 years are to be referred to a social worker for an Unreasonable to Live at Home (UTLAH) Special Benefit assessment. Customers who are referred for this assessment but assessed as not eligible and/or their claim is rejected may still be referred to a social worker for further support and/or referral to external agencies.

Social workers are to refer customer to a State or Territory Welfare Authority for a protective assessment if the customer is:

  • under 15 years and is homeless or unsupported
  • under 18 years and in in the guardianship, care or custody of a state/territory welfare department, or
  • aged 15 to 17 years (inclusive) and is considered to be at risk of abuse, serious harm or violence

Social workers are to seek the consent of the young person for this referral. Where consent is not provided, the social worker must seek the approval of the Services Australia delegate to release information to the State or Territory Welfare Authority in the public interest. This requirement will be discussed with the customer at the time of the appointment.

The Resources page contains a link to information about Youth Protective Assessments and the forms used to refer customers to State or Territory Welfare Authorities.

Customers can be paid Independent Youth Allowance (YA) from 15 years of age, unless they are:

  • under the minimum school leaving age for the State or Territory in which they reside, and
  • do not have a formal exemption from attending school

SpB may be paid to a young person aged 15 to 17 years (inclusive) who is considered to be at risk of abuse, serious harm or violence prior to the completion of a protective assessment by a state/territory welfare department.

Customers who are under 15 or under the school leaving age may be assessed for SpB.

For under 15 year olds who are at risk of homelessness or are unsupported, SpB should only be paid if, after completion of a protective assessment, the state/territory welfare department does not intend to provide support to the young person, or it is determined that the support provided by the state/territory welfare department is inadequate. The young person must still meet all eligibility requirements for SpB.

If SpB is granted to a young person under the school leaving age, their qualification for YA must be considered when they reach school leaving age.

Consideration should also be given to whether a parent or guardian, or Approved Care Organisation (ACO) is eligible to claim Family Tax Benefit (FTB) on behalf of the young person. An appropriate parent or guardian is one where there is an established relationship, where the adult/organisation are able to meet the young person's basic needs, and where the adult/organisation is seen to have the young person's best interests in mind.

For customers aged under 15 years, it is mandatory for social workers to consult with their Social Work Manager throughout the UTLAH assessment.

Social workers are to offer the young person a referral to social casework. To refer the customer see the Social casework referral table on the Process page in Social work service referral.

See Assisting a customer aged under 16.

2

Australian Citizen Children (ACC) & Australian Permanent Resident Children in the custody of a Non-permanent Resident + Read more ...

In certain circumstances, SpB may be paid to a child under 16 years who is an Australian citizen or an Australian permanent resident, but whose custodial parent(s) is not a permanent resident of Australia and is therefore precluded from receiving an income support payment.

SpB can only be paid if the family is in genuine hardship and it has been established that the parent(s) or guardian has no means of adequately supporting the child. Such cases should be considered on their merits and referred to a social worker. Where a social worker determines the child is at serious risk a referral to a state/territory welfare department for a protective assessment should be made.

Social workers are to seek the consent of the customer for this referral. Where consent is not provided, the social worker may seek the approval of a Services Australia delegate to release information to the state or territory welfare department in the public interest. This requirement will be discussed with the customer at the time of the appointment.

SpB is only payable to full-time students under 16 years (including Australian citizens or permanent resident children) if they are a SpB homeless person. An Australian citizen or permanent resident child in the custody of a non-permanent resident parent/s is a SpB homeless person if:

  • the child cannot live at the home of their parent/s because the parent/s is unable to provide the child with a suitable home owing to a lack of accommodation, and
  • the child is not receiving continuous support, whether directly or indirectly and whether financial or otherwise, from a parent or from another person who is acting as the child's guardian on a long-term basis

SpB is not payable to a child who is a full-time student if the family is living in stable accommodation and/or a parent or long-term guardian is providing the child with continuous support.

A child can be regarded as not being able to live at the home of their parent/s owing to a lack of stable accommodation if the child, or their custodial parent/s, is judged to be homeless. The child or the custodial parent/s can be judged to be homeless if rejection or cancellation of SpB would place the child and their parent/s at severe risk of homelessness. Social workers should ensure that there is supporting evidence that the family is homeless or at severe risk of homelessness.

Where the custodial parent has no means of adequately supporting the child who is over school age, and the child is assessed not eligible for SpB, referral to a social worker is also strongly recommended.

The social worker can assist in determining if the child is in genuine hardship by:

  • offering advice about help that may be available through external agencies and/or link the custodial parent to appropriate external agencies for assistance
  • assessing the domestic situation e.g. presence of family and domestic violence and whether the customer is eligible for an exemption from maintenance actions to receive the full rate of the Family Tax Benefit (FTB) instead

3

Assurance of Support (AOS) + Read more ...

If the claim for payment has resulted from a relationship breakdown, referral to a social worker is mandatory:

  • where family and domestic violence is not a factor, a social worker must be involved for the purpose of exploring the possibility of mediation and/or reconciliation, or
  • where the claimant is suspected to have experienced or be experiencing family and domestic violence, the assuree claimant must be referred to a social worker before any contact with the assurer is made

The social worker is to:

  • explore the possibility of mediation and/or reconciliation if family and domestic violence is not identified
  • complete an assessment outlining whether it is appropriate to contact the assurer or recommending that correspondence be delayed due to family and domestic violence

4

Newly Arrived Resident (NAR) + Read more ...

Where a Newly Arrived Resident claims to be in severe financial hardship due to a change in circumstances, they must substantiate their claim.

A social work referral is strongly recommended where:

  • there has been a change in complex circumstances, or
  • an exploration of community support options is required before SpB can be considered (as a last resort)

A social work referral is mandatory where:

  • there has been a change in circumstances and family and domestic violence has been identified

The social worker is to:

  • provide consultation on complex cases
  • complete an assessment (especially if a Maintenance Exemption is required)

The social worker is to consider that:

  • For any Special Benefit claim lodged on or after 1 January 2012, the exemption from the Newly Arrived Resident's Waiting Period (NARWP) for family members of an Australian citizen or long term Australian resident will no longer apply to customer who are holders of a Partner Provisional Visa (Subclass 309, 310, 820, 826)
  • These customers may be able to receive an exemption from the NARWP for other reasons, for example if they suffer a substantial change in circumstances for reasons beyond their control

5

Partner Provisional Visas (PPV) + Read more ...

A PPV holder claiming SpB due to a substantial change in circumstances beyond their control must substantiate their claim with documentation.

A social work referral is strongly recommended where:

  • there is a change in complex circumstances, or
  • an exploration of community support options is required before SpB can be considered (as a last resort)

A social work referral is mandatory where:

  • there has been a change in circumstances and family and domestic violence has been identified

The social worker is to:

  • provide consultation on complex cases
  • complete a generic Social Work Report in the Social Work Information System

The information provided by a social worker should contribute to a decision about qualification for SpB, however it is the delegate, not the social worker, who makes the final decision about qualification.

Note: there is a group of young people who may arrive in Australia on a spouse visa via their parents. In this case, although they hold a partner provisional visa (because that is the visa their parent entered the country on) they are not a spouse or partner. Their circumstances will need to be determined on a case-by-case basis.

6

Socially Marginalised (SOM) + Read more ...

A Socially Marginalised customer is generally considered to be someone who:

  • has poor prospects of finding work, and/or
  • would not be considered by employers to be suitable for employment

SpB is a last resort after eligibility for other payments has been explored.

A social worker should:

  • Assess whether the person meets the eligibility criteria for SpB,
  • Explore alternatives to granting SpB, e.g. the customer may qualify for Disability Support Pension (DSP) but be reluctant to claim or seek professional help. Social workers can assist the customer to receive treatment and claim the appropriate payment, and/or
  • Explore possible assistance available through external agencies

The social worker is to:

  • provide consultation on complex cases
  • complete a generic Social Work Report in the Social Work Information System

7

Not residentially qualified for Disability Support Pension (DSP) + Read more ...

This category only applies to customers who do not qualify for DSP for the sole reason that they do not meet residency requirements for DSP because their continuing inability to work occurred when they were not a resident of Australia and they do not have 10 years qualifying residence in Australia.

Note: DSP is payable to some customers under certain International Agreements. If the customer has lived or worked in an Agreement country that covers DSP, the customer should test their eligibility for DSP.

Referral to a social worker is recommended in all cases. Social workers can provide case consultation and offer advice regarding assistance that may be available through external agencies.

The social worker is to:

  • Provide consultation on complex cases
  • Complete a DOC on the customer's record briefly outlining their intervention. If required, a generic Social Work Report is to be completed in the Social Work Information System

8

Caring for a child (CFC) + Read more ...

A social worker may be able to assist the delegate in making a decision or in suggesting alternative support options to the customer.

A social work referral is strongly recommended where:

  • there are complex circumstances,
  • an exploration of community support options is required before SpB can be considered (as a last resort), or
  • the care situation requires further assessment (such as determining a carer's personal care or supervision needs, or information about the level of care or supervision and who is providing it)

The social worker is to:

  • provide consultation on complex cases
  • complete a generic Social Work Report in the Social Work Information System

9

Caring for an incapacitated person (CIP) + Read more ...

If the carer is incapacitated, confirmation of circumstances is required (such as medical evidence from the treating doctor). The evidence should outline the nature of the incapacity, extent to which the person concerned requires assistance with daily activities, and likely or estimated period for which care will be required.

A social work referral is not compulsory for this category, however is strongly recommended where:

  • there are complex circumstances
  • an exploration of community support options is required before SpB can be considered (as a last resort), or
  • the care situation requires further assessment (such as determining a carer's personal care or supervision needs, or information about the level of care or supervision and who is providing it)

The social worker is to:

  • provide consultation on complex cases
  • complete a generic Social Work Report in the Social Work Information System

10

Other cases- short term (OST) + Read more ...

For this category, it may be necessary to involve a social worker before a decision can be finalised. The social worker can for example, explore referral options in the community before SpB is considered (as a last resort).

The social worker is to:

  • Provide consultation on complex cases
  • Complete a DOC on the customer's record briefly outlining their intervention. If appropriate, a generic Social Work Report is to be completed in the Social Work Information System

11

Recording requirements + Read more ...

Social workers are required to record their involvement in SpB claims as follows:

  • All contacts, referrals, and reports are to be recorded in the Social Work Information System (SWIS).
  • Social worker DOCs are to be created on customer records.
