FAQs - welfare fraud and corruption

Table 1

Item

Description

1

Question: What is the difference between the Report Suspected Fraud application and Report Suspected Fraud and Corruption system?

Answer: The Report Suspected Fraud and Corruption system is to report fraud identified by staff during the course of their regular duties. Staff working in Centrelink, Medicare and Child Support programmes can report suspected fraud and corruption against all Services Australia programmes.

The Report Suspected Fraud application is for members of the public or staff submitting a report on behalf of a member of the public.

2

Question: How much time do I have to complete the report?

Answer: The Report Suspected Fraud and Corruption system times out after 30 minutes of inactivity. To prevent losing your report select 'Save' at the top of the page. If you don't have time to complete your tip-off, saving will temporarily store the report in 'My Unsubmitted Reports'. These reports will automatically be submitted seven days after the creation date.

The Report Suspected Fraud application times out after 15 minutes on a single page and the report will be lost. A time-out message will occur if this time limit is exceeded. If close to exceeding this time limit, select 'Continue' to save the report then click the 'back 'button in the report suspected fraud application to reset the timer. Each time a person selects the continue button and navigates to a new page, the timer is reset.

3

Question: How much detail needs to be recorded in a suspected fraud report?

Answer: The report should contain as much detail as possible relating to the suspected fraud and corruption. There is no need to repeat the same information throughout the report. Remember the contents of this report could be viewed under a Freedom of Information (FOI) access. See Centrelink suspected fraud examples.

4

Question: Can I record multiple suspected frauds and corruption committed by the same person on one report?

Answer: Yes. Staff and members of the public have the opportunity to record multiple suspected fraud and corruption types within the same report by selecting relevant fraud types.

5

Question: Can a fraud report be made about a business or organisation?

Answer: Yes. Staff can report suspected fraud and corruption about a business or organisation using the Report Suspected Fraud and Corruption system. At the 'Who is the report about' question add 'Business/Organisation'.

Members of the public or staff on the behalf of members of the public can submit a report about a business or organisation using the Report Suspected Fraud application. On the 'About this Service' page, choose 'Report Suspected fraud (about a business or organisation).

6

Question: Should I report suspected fraud and corruption by a child care provider and an individual in one report?

Answer: Yes. The Report Suspected Fraud and Corruption system allows child care providers (business/organisation) and individuals to be recorded in the same report. Provide details about the child care provider and those members of the public you believe may have fraudulently claimed or be claiming Child Care payments.

7

Question: What if there isn't a category for the type of fraud and corruption I want to report?

Answer: Choose the most appropriate category and enter details in the additional information box.

8

Question: Can a report be retracted once a report is submitted?

Answer: No. Once a report has been submitted, it cannot be retracted. If a member of the public requests to retract a report they have submitted or you have submitted a report on their behalf, advise the following:

'Due to privacy legislation, we are not able to provide you with any updates or outcomes about the information you have previously provided. This means that we are also unable to withdraw a previously submitted report. I am sorry for any inconvenience this may cause.'

9

Question: Can I report suspected fraud and corruption that was committed a number of years ago?

Answer: Yes. There are no time limitations on reporting suspected fraud and corruption.

10

Question: What if I am unsure whether to report a situation I think may be suspected fraud and corruption or have other complex fraud and corruption enquiries?

Answer: Follow technical support procedures relevant to your area. If your level 1 support services are unable to resolve your query, see Level 2 Report Suspected Fraud helpdesk.

4

Question: Where do I need to refer non-compliance with an EF1, s72a (failure to deduct), s72b, s120 or s161 notice?

Answer: If an organisation or individual fails to comply with an EF1, s72a (failure to deduct), s72b, s120 or s161 notice this needs to be referred to Employer Services, see Collection of child support debts through third parties (s72A and s72AC).

5

Question: What do I do if an organisation has failed to remit in line with s72A notice or Employer withholding?

Answer: Submit a report using the Report Suspected Fraud and Corruption system.

6

Question: Is it fraud if a customer fails to pay child support?

Answer: No. This is not a fraud.

7

Question: Is it fraud for a parent to provide an income that does not reflect their financial capacity?

Answer: No. This is not fraud.

8

Question: Is it fraud for a parent to provide an income that does not reflect their current earnings?

Answer: Yes, it is fraud for a parent to knowingly provide a false or misleading statement to an officer s159 Child Support Assessment Act 1989

For example, a parent lodges an estimate of $0 and advises he is no longer working for ABC PTY LTD. Two months later a Service Officer receives an s120 notice response from a bank, which shows that the parent has continued to receive regular deposits from ABC PTY LTD. This information suggests that the parent may have provided a false statement to Child Support and this should be referred for further scrutiny.

Child Support fraud definitions

Table 4

Step

Action

1

Making false or misleading statements

A false or misleading statement may include:

  • either parent deliberately omitting or under-declaring income or assets
  • an employer advising a customer is not employed despite regular payments being deposited into the customer's account from the employer
  • a customer claiming a child is in their care when this is not the case
  • either parent stating they have relevant dependants when this is not the case

For more information, see the References page for a link to the Child Support Guide 6.8.4: False and misleading statements.

2

Use of false names or documents

False or forged documents may include:

  • a statutory declaration
  • payslips
  • bank statements
  • a birth certificate
  • Non Agency Payment (NAP) receipts

3

Employee/employer collusion

Employee/employer collusion is any instance where it appears an employee and employer are working together to disrupt or avoid child support collection action. For example, if the customer is employed by a family member who has refused to comply with an employer withholding notice.

For more information, see the References page for a link to the Child Support Guide 6.8.1: Employer offences.

4

Failure to meet employer obligations

An employer may fail to comply with their obligations if they:

  • dismiss or threaten to dismiss an employee (customer) solely as a result of a garnishee request or the employee's role as the paying customer of a child support liability
  • disclose a customer's details obtained because of our request to deduct from the customer's salary or wages to other persons. Note: this offence also applies to persons employed by or performing services for an employer

For more information see the References page for a link to the Child Support Guide 6.8.1: Employer offences.

5

Impersonating an officer

Impersonating a public official (including an officer of Services Australia) is fraud. For example:

  • an employer or customer contacts us to advise someone contacted them to ascertain information and claimed to be from the agency but our records do not indicate any such contact
  • a representative from a child's school advises someone contacted them for information and claimed to be from the agency but our records don't reflect the contact attempt or any reason for such a contact

For more information see the References page for a link to the Criminal Code Act (1995) Division 148.

6

Failure to comply with legislative notices

Failure to comply with legislative notices may occur when:

  • an employer or other third party refuses or fails to comply with an information gathering notice:
    • section 120 of the Child Support (Registration and Collection) Act 1988
    • Confirmation of a Person's Employment form
    • section 161 of the Child Support (Assessment) Act 1989
  • an employer or other third party refuses or fails to deduct child support
  • an employer or other third party refuses or fails to remit deducted child support
  • a customer fails to inform a change to their circumstances in line with the requirements of a notice that has been issued listing their obligations, such as:
    • change in income
    • change in employment
    • change in care

For more information see the References page for a link to the Child Support Guide 6.8.2: Offences Involving a Failure to Comply with Notices.

See Child Support's information gathering powers for more information.

7

Offences relating to Departure Prohibition Orders

It is an offence if a customer leaves or attempts to leave Australia where a Departure Prohibition Order (DPO) is in place without having a Departure Authorisation Certificate (DAC).

For more information, see Departure Prohibition Orders (DPO) for Child Support customers. The References page has a link to the Child Support Guide 6.8.5: Offences in Relation to Departure Prohibition Orders.

Note: a statute of limitations of 12 months from the date of the offense does apply to certain sections
