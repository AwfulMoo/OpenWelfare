Vulnerable customers

Consider factors impacting vulnerable customers + Read more ...

External factors, such as long wait times, poor service delivery or lack of available information may influence customer behaviours. The service we provide can rebuild the customer’s relationship with Services Australia.

Apply a sensitive approach to recognising difficulties customers may be facing based on their circumstances. For example homelessness, financial hardship or disabilities and medical conditions.

Developing a risk-based approach to supporting customers experiencing anxiety, inadequate coping skills and illness by being prepared with relevant referrals or service offers can help prevent and de-escalate customer aggression.

Consider referrals to address any identified special needs of the customer:

  • Social worker
  • Indigenous Service Officer (ISO)
  • The Financial Information Service (FIS) Officer
  • Multicultural Service Officer (MSO)
  • Language Services (for example, translation of customer aggression letters)
  • Specialised and Intensive Services (SIS)
  • Incarcerated Customer Services
  • Job Capacity Assessment (JCA) Referral
  • Employment Services Assessment (ESAt) Referral
  • Community Engagement Officer (CEO)
  • Farm Household Case Officer (FHCO)
  • Grandparent, Foster and Kinship Carer Advisers
  • Australian Disability Enterprises (ADE) referrals
  • Personalised Services (PS)

Unfavourable decision

Communicating unfavourable decisions + Read more ...

It is important to make genuine attempts to contact a customer to give them the chance to understand the decision and provide new information or evidence where appropriate.

Clearly explaining a decision can prevent rework through complaints, reviews and appeals or objections, and repeat contact from the customer. It also gives the agency the chance to correct simple errors. Explaining decisions can help prevent or de-escalate incidents by respectfully considering the customer’s views and, if appropriate, quick referrals or follow up.

When preparing to advise a customer of a decision about their payment or claim:

  • Have a clear understanding of the relevant policy or legislation; this enables you to explain the reason(s) for the decision in a confident manner
  • Make sure any commitments are followed up
  • Anticipate the customer might interrupt. Stop talking to allow them to speak
  • Try to bring the conversation back on track if it goes off topic

If the customer expresses dissatisfaction with the decision, acknowledge their point of view, for example:

  • 'This must be very difficult and I can appreciate the steps you have taken before this point.'
  • 'It must be very frustrating for you to hear that you are ineligible.'
  • 'I can hear your frustration about not being able to challenge this.'
  • 'I hear that this is not the outcome you are looking for.'

Provide appropriate options:

  • Listen to their needs and provide referrals if appropriate
  • Discuss their review and appeal rights

If the interaction escalates. See De-escalation techniques below

De-escalation techniques

De-escalating the interaction + Read more ...

Where a customer interaction escalates, respond in a way intended to bring the discussion back to a respectful interaction. Always address the customer’s emotion before starting to explain, find a solution or fix the issue:

  • Let the customer explain their situation. Do not interrupt, correct or disagree with them
  • Build rapport using good listening skills, empathising and acknowledging their situation. Show that you hear what they are saying, ‘That would be so hard for you’ or ‘I couldn’t imagine what you are coping with right now.’
  • Maintain self-control and model behaviour you want to see/hear
  • Make it hard for the customer to be angry with you by being patient and courteous
  • When a customer starts agreeing with your empathy comments, for example by responding with 'exactly', it is hard for them to turn around and say 'No, you don’t understand' or 'How would you know what it’s like?'
  • Only move forward once the customer knows that their emotions have been acknowledged. If the customer cannot respond to the following logically, their emotions are still too high:
    • 'I want to help, perhaps we can explore some options to support you'
    • 'What can I do to make this better?'

After trying the above techniques and if the customer’s behaviour is frightening or intimidating, deliver an assertive request for them to stop. This is about stopping the behaviour, not changing the customer’s emotional state.

Deliver the first request at a slightly lower volume than you would normally speak and speak slower to create a calming effect. Address the customer’s emotion first, before starting to explain, find a solution, or fix the issue.

Helpful responses include:

  • Try to reduce swearing, yelling or abuse, by asking, 'Can we bring this down a bit?' or 'Can we slow things down a bit?'
  • 'When you continue to swear/yell/speak over me/blame/threaten/use your tone to intimidate, I can’t help you.'
  • 'I need you to stop swearing/lower your voice/slow down/use a more respectful tone so I can help you.'
  • If the customer begins escalating, do not raise your voice; get their attention by saying their name

Avoid responses that could escalate the interaction, for example:

  • ‘I understand your situation’
  • ‘I can imagine’
  • ‘Sorry I’m not allowed to’
  • ‘Unfortunately’
  • ‘I’m just trying my best’
  • ‘Please can you just calm down’
  • ‘Do you realise that the other customers don’t act like you?’
  • ‘I’m just trying to do my job'

De-escalation check + Read more ...

The customer has de-escalated when:

  • They are paraphrasing and mirroring your calm behaviour
  • Their tone, volume and pace of voice has reduced
  • They agree with you/start nodding
  • There is respectful tone/eye contact
  • They apologise for their behaviour
  • They stay on track and are solution focussed

If the customer’s emotions become high again, repeat the de-escalation techniques before continuing to resolve the reason for the contact.

End phone call

Ending/discontinuing customer phone calls + Read more ...

Sometimes de-escalation may not work. In such cases, end the call.

Do not remove headset before ending a phone call.

Call recording continues until a call is ended. Anything recorded is deemed to be information the customer has supplied to the agency. Removing the headset before the call ends means valuable information may be missed.

Do not end the call if the customer is:

  • talking about suicide
  • talking about harm (self or others)
  • making a threat (staff or agency)

Warn the customer before ending a call

Explain to the customer the call will be discontinued if their behaviour continues. Inform the customer that the agency expects its staff to be treated with respect and courtesy and it is difficult to help when a customer is not acting accordingly.

Example

‘I may not be able to continue this call with you because you are (quote behaviour). This is making it hard for me to help you. If you continue to (behaviour) I will end the call without any further warning and discuss this with my manager/team leader.’

If the behaviour continues, inform the customer the call is ending. End the call politely.

Disengage

Customer's behaviour escalates + Read more ...

If the customer's behaviour escalates further, or if a staff member feels unsafe, disengage. For Smart Centre staff, see Telephony standards procedures. Seek immediate leadership support in line with the Aggressive Behaviour Response Model (ABRM).

The team leader/manager should make a decision about the response with consideration of personal safety and the safety of people in the site, and with awareness that disengaging may escalate aggressive behaviour.

If the behaviour is aggressive, there is an intent to cause harm and/or the impact potentially endangers the safety of staff and others, this is a customer aggression incident and the response should be to disengage, and/or initiate an emergency response.

For more information, see Customer aggression - Response.

Service Australia may implement an MSP to tailor how we deliver services as a strategy to improve the safety of staff, the public and agency property. MSPs can include a full or partial restriction to a customer's access to one or more service channels.

For more information about responding to phone threats, see Customer aggression - Managed Service Plan (MSP).

Proactive Managed Service Plans (MSPs)

Proactive MSPs + Read more ...

Proactive MSPs provide a support mechanism for customers with barriers or vulnerabilities rather than as a response to an incident. A proactive MSP can act as a preventative strategy for customers who may be a threat to staff safety.

Service Australia may implement a proactive MSP to tailor the delivery of services to customers with identified vulnerabilities, barriers, aggressive or counterproductive behaviours.

A proactive MSP may or may not include service restrictions depending on the circumstances of the case, for example:

  • No service channel restrictions:
    • To support a homeless customer with health issues while they navigate the process to apply for the Disability Support pension
  • With service channel restrictions:
    • To maintain staff safety based on the Prison Liaison Officer’s advice that a prison release customer may pose a risk to staff safety

See Customer aggression - Managed Service Plan (MSP) for further detail about MSPs and for details of the relevant Customer Aggression Network Operational Contact (CANOC).

Smart Centres (including Assessment Services Branch)

Smart Centre Team Leaders and Managers should discuss MSP referrals with the Smart Centre Customer Portfolio Operations team who are responsible for submitting MSP recommendations to the relevant Zone CANOC.

If a Smart Centre Team Leader or Manager needs to contact the Smart Centre Customer Portfolio Operations team, they should email Smart Centre Escalations.

All other business areas

Contact the relevant Zone CANOC for further advice.

Service Delivery Partners MSP referrals

If a Service Delivery Partner thinks an MSP is needed a Team Leader or Manager of that site should email the Smart Centre Customer Portfolio Operations team, they will liaise with the relevant Zone CANOC.

If a business area needs to contact a service delivery partner about a customer or to discuss an MSP they should email Smart Centre Escalations

Shared Premises

If an incident occurs with a partner agency or a mutual customer in a shared premises, both Services Australia and the partner agency collaborate on an agreed response. This may include a joint agreement to implement service channel restrictions to premises and services. This may result in a different servicing outcome for each agency. For assistance, contact the relevant Zone CANOC.

For more information, see Customer aggression - Managed Service Plan (MSP).
