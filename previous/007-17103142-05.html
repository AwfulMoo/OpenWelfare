ACCS (child wellbeing) scenarios and evidence examples

Table 1

Example

Description

1

Child in formal foster care

Scenario: In 2017, Imal was removed from parental care and placed in the care of the Minister under a court order until Imal turns 18. Imal was placed in long term formal foster care. The service uploads the court order as evidence which commenced in 2017.

Outcome: As this order is more than 6 months old, a letter from a support agency / organisation to validate the care orders are still in place is needed. As Imal is in formal foster care, no additional information is needed to show Imal is ‘at risk’.

2

Family participating in an approved early intervention program

Scenario: Rafael’s family live in NSW and go to Brighter Futures, they provide early intervention support to vulnerable families. As part of the uploaded evidence, Brighter Futures provide a letter stating the family are attending their program.

Outcome: As this service is listed as an approved early intervention program for NSW within Table 2, no additional information needs to be provided.

3

Informal care arrangement

Scenario: Kungawo is in the care of a relative under an informal kinship arrangement between Kungawo's parent and the relative.

Outcome: As this is an informal arrangement, the service will need to supply evidence of the type of harm, abuse or neglect Kungawo is experiencing, to determine if Kungawo satisfies the requirements for child at risk.

4

Child is not specifically named in the evidence

Scenario: Jolly Rainbows childcare service upload a letter from a State protection agency for a child wellbeing determination for Michelle, which is being processed by Dharanish. The letter does not mention Michelle by name, but Dharanish notices there is reference to ‘3 year old child’ and Michelle is 3.

Outcome: Dharanish is able to use the evidence as he can reasonably identify the evidence relates to Michelle. This is similar where evidence names 2 children but not a third, which can often be the case for siblings.

5

Statutory Declaration for an initial determination

Scenario: Bianca’s Buzzy Bees child care service previously applied for and was granted a determination for child Amber based on a statutory declaration as evidence. The Service lodges a further determination and uploads another statutory declaration.

Outcome: The statutory declaration cannot be used for the subsequent determination. A child care provider can submit a statutory declaration for initial determinations only where they are of the reasonable belief a child meets the definition of 'at risk' for ACCS (child wellbeing).

6

Temporary Protection Orders (TPO)/Apprehended Violence Orders (AVO) / Domestic Violence Orders (DVO) as evidence

Scenario: Green Gremlins childcare service uploads an Apprehended Violence Order (AVO) for a child wellbeing determination for Jenny. No other evidence has been supplied.

Outcome: TPO/AVO or DVOs may be provided as supporting evidence of a child wellbeing determination application. These must be accompanied with a supporting letter, demonstrating the child is ‘at risk’.

7

Court Order is not current

Scenario: A court order is in place since 2 January because Logan is in need of protection under state law. It is now August and the service has uploaded the court order for evidence that Logan is ‘at risk’.

Outcome: As the evidence provided is older than six months, the provider is required to supply a letter from a supporting agency stipulating the court order continue to be in place. The court order itself is not sufficient.

8

Evidence supplied is 5 months old

Scenario: Documentary evidence from a Child Safety/ Protection Agency has been provided regarding Riley, dated 1 June 2021. The provider has lodged a determination application commencing 15 November 2021.

Outcome: Although the evidence is 5 months old in November, if the provider is applying for 13 weeks and the evidence supports a determination of 'at risk', the full 13 week determination period can be approved as the evidence is less than 6 months old from the applied determination start date.

9

Evidence supplied is 6 months old

Scenario: Evidence from a Child Safety/ Protection Agency has been provided regarding Tahlia. It is dated 6 January 2021. The provider has lodged a determination application commencing 26 July 2021.

Outcome: As the determination application period commenced in July 2021, it is reasonable to accept any evidence dated from the start of January 2021 and onwards.

10

Date on Word document displaying incorrectly as todays date

Scenario: Evidence from a Child Safety/Protection Agency is uploaded on the 28 February 2022 for child James. It assessed on 2 March 2022 by Peter. Peter notices the date on the Word document is displaying incorrectly as today’s date i.e. 2 March 2022.

Outcome: To check the currency of the evidence, in the Word Document Peter selects File and confirms the Last Modified date is 16 June 2021. Peter can now reject the determination as the evidence is more than six months old.

11

Evidence is a letter of support for a specific period of time

Scenario: Evidence from a psychologist has been provided regarding Olivia. It is dated 2 August 2022 and in the letter the psychologist states:

  • how Olivia meets the definition of child at risk, and
  • specifies they are supportive of the child wellbeing application for a period of 6 weeks

The service has submitted an application for a period of 13 weeks from 2 August 2022.

Outcome: The:

  • period of the determination is to be calculated from the issue date of the letter (being 2 August 2022) and run for 6 weeks, and
  • the number of risk weeks will need to be reduced from 13 to 6, as specified in the letter of support

12

Evidence lists the medical condition of parent/carer as the core reason for neglect

Scenario: A letter from a counsellor is uploaded as evidence for an application for Henry. The letter states Henry’s parent is experiencing mental health issues.

Outcome: Mental health issues are not sufficient to warrant the grant of ACCS (child wellbeing). Many parents/carers experience mental health issues and are able to effectively support their child/ren. Consideration needs to be given on each particular case.

For example: It may be a grant if the counsellor stated:

  • Henry’s parent had no other support and is engaging with counsellors or support workers, and
  • there would be a potential for relapse, placing Henry at risk and leading to Child Safety involvement

13

Evidence lists the medical condition of the child as the core reason for neglect

Scenario: A letter from a physiotherapist is uploaded as evidence for an application for Ruby. Within the letter:

  • Ruby’s diagnosis and physical/emotional impairments are outlined in detail, and
  • there is no information about why she is ‘at risk’

Outcome: Legislatively, the disability of a child can only be considered with other circumstances and matters relevant to determine if the child is at risk. If no other factors are mentioned, this would not meet evidentiary requirements and the claim should be rejected.

14

Evidence uses the following wording 'ACCS (child wellbeing) is required to support the family due to unintentional risk of harm and neglect' and is not specific on why

Scenario: A letter from a General Practitioner (GP) is uploaded as evidence for an application for Ethan. The letter contains the wording 'unintentional risk of harm and neglect”, however they have not stipulated how Ethan is at risk.

Outcome: This wording alone is not sufficient to warrant a grant of ACCS (child wellbeing). It does not discuss other barriers, or make linkages on why, the child meets the definition of ‘at risk’.

Acceptable types of evidence

Table 2

Item

Description

Acceptable types of evidence - Organisations

Organisations considered suitable to provide third-party evidence in support of an ACCS (child wellbeing) claim can include:

state and territory government, early intervention and support programs and services for vulnerable children and families

non-government organisations providing early intervention and family support services

state and territory or private schools

other state and territory government and non-government organisations are providers of relevant services, including those related to mental health, family violence and family law, homelessness, drug and alcohol rehabilitation, and Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander services

state and territory government child safety/protection agencies

Acceptable types of evidence - Professionals

Professionals considered suitable, depending on the circumstances of each case, to provide third-party evidence in support of an ACCS (child wellbeing) claim could include:

medical practitioners and registered nurses

hospital admission/emergency unit professionals

welfare agency personnel and social workers

psychologists and counsellors

physiotherapists, occupational therapists and speech pathologists

school principals and teachers

police officers

lawyers

This list is not exhaustive, and there may be other appropriate professionals who are able to provide supporting documentation.

Early intervention programs

  • Early intervention programs are for families who are at high risk of entering or escalating within the child protection system.
  • State and territory government agencies play a key role in referring families to these programs, or authorising access to the programs
  • Evidence of a family’s current participation in a program listed can be sufficient evidence for the purpose of ACCS (child wellbeing)

State/Territory

Program name

Program description

Western Australia

Intensive Family Support

Intensive Family Support teams work with open child protection cases where families are at imminent risk of their children coming into care. They provide time limited, intensive in-home support to create safety and enable children or young people to remain with their family.

Best Beginnings Plus

The Best Beginnings Plus service is an earlier intervention, intensive home visiting program. They work alongside families with infants or unborn babies at risk of neglect and/or abuse.

Intensive Case Management (ICM)

Service provided as part of the Family Support Networks (FSNs). FSNs provide targeted support to families:

  • with significant protection concerns; and
  • where the support of a number of services are needed

FSNs are trauma informed, culturally competent and prioritise referrals for Aboriginal families.

Aboriginal in Home Support Service (AISS)

AISS is delivered by Wungening Moort (Healing Families) a consortium of an Aboriginal Community Controlled Organisations. They provide a trauma-informed, culturally relevant intensive in-home practical support service for up to 240 high risk Aboriginal families in the Perth metropolitan area.

Victoria

Parenting Assessment and Skill Development Services (PASDS)

PASDS are specialist, intensive parenting services for children involved with child protection. PASDS are provided by community services organisations in the home, in a residential or community setting.

Family Services

Family Services targets vulnerable young people and their families who are:

  • likely to experience greater challenges - as the child development has been affected by the experience of risk factors and/or
  • cumulative harm and/or
  • at risk of concerns escalating and becoming involved with Child Protection - if problems are not addressed

Placement prevention and reunification programs

A range of placement prevention and reunification programs which provide intensive support to families involved with Child Protection where there is a risk of children or young people being placed in out-of-home care.

Cradle to Kinder

Provides intensive support to vulnerable young mothers, their families and children, proving antenatal and longer-term case work support, beginning in pregnancy and continuing until the child reaches four years old.

Parkville Institute Early Years Education Program (EYEP)

A multi-disciplinary model of intensive early childhood education and care (ECEC), infant mental health and family support for vulnerable and disadvantaged children. This is a time-limited replication trial over the period 2022/23 to 2025/26

New South Wales

Permanency Support Program Family Preservation Packages

These packages provide evidence-based wrap around supports and services to safely sustain a child or young person in their home environment to avoid the need to enter out-of-home care. This high-intensity service can be up to two years in duration.

Intensive Family Preservation

A high intensity placement prevention program designed to work with families in crisis with children who are at imminent risk of removal and placement in Out-of-Home Care (OOHC); and where it is safe and in their best interests they remain in the care of their family.

Intensive Family Based Services (IFBS)

IFBS work with Aboriginal children, young people and families where those children and young people are at risk of entering the OOHC system (Crisis/Preservation stream).

Multisystemic Therapy for Child Abuse and Neglect (MST-CAN)

A therapeutic family preservation program designed to address underlying trauma and other factors are known to be related to physical abuse and neglect within families.

Brighter Futures

A family preservation service for families in crisis with children at risk of significant harm and are at risk of accessing the out-of-home care system. The program provides tailored support to vulnerable families to respond to parent vulnerabilities and to increase child safety.

Youth Hope

Youth Hope supports children or young people aged 9-15 at risk of significant harm and are at risk of accessing the out-of-home care system. The program provides tailored support to vulnerable children, young people and their families.

Functional Family Therapy through Child Welfare (FFT-CW)

A home-based treatment model for families with substantiated cases of physical abuse and/or neglect of a child or young person, where the child is at risk of entering out-of-home care. FFT-CW® addresses the underlying trauma that results in harm to children, young people and families.

Tasmania

Intensive Family Engagement Services (IFES)

IFES provides intensive, assertive support to families to reduce the level of risk to their children enabling them to remain safely in the family home on a long term basis. IFES is premised on assertive engagement, so while the intervention is not mandated by order, it is designed to present parents with a clear understanding if they don’t work with IFES to address concerns, there may be further intervention by Child Safety Service.

Queensland

Assessment and Service Connect (ASC)

ASC partners with Child Safety to respond to children and families subject to investigation and assessments. ASC services are available state-wide, with the exception of certain remote locations within the Northern Queensland Region. The role of the ASC is to:

  • assess the family’s support needs,
  • determine the most suitable services, and
  • streamline access to support services by connecting families early in the investigation and assessment process

This partnership is referred to as an ASC co-response.

Parkville Institute Early Years Education Program (EYEP)

A multi-disciplinary model of intensive early childhood education and care (ECEC), infant mental health and family support for vulnerable and disadvantaged children. This is a time-limited replication trial, over the period 2022/23 to 2025/26

South Australia

Family Preservation Service

Comprises of in home and out of home support to preserve and strengthen family relationships to ensure children and young people can remain safely in the care of their families.

Family Preservation Services Connecting Families (fee for service)

Provides Family Preservation services comprising of assertive and intensive interventions with Children and Young People and their birth families enable Children and Young People to remain safely in the care of their families.

DCP Family Preservation Service

Family preservation case managers work with families where children are living in the family home. They work intensively and in partnership with families to strengthen and preserve the family unit to reduce the risk of future abuse and enable children to remain safely in the home.

Pilot Intensive Family Preservation Project in Northern Adelaide - Safe Kids Families Together

Services comprise of in home and out of home care to preserve and strengthen family relationships, ensuring children and young people can remain safely in the care of their families.

KWY Intensive Family Support

Aims to improve the safety of women, children and men by providing specialist knowledge and culturally appropriate services to:

  • break the cycle of domestic violence, and
  • stem the flow of Aboriginal children into the child protection systems across Australia

RASA - Family Group Conference

Provides Family Group Conferencing as a service for families where concerns for the wellbeing and safety of a child or young person have been raised. Participating a Family Group Conference is voluntary. All referrals to Ngartuitya family Group Conference service will come from the Department of Child Protection.
