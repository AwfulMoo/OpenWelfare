Compensation codes

Compensation codes and situations when used

This table describes the compensation codes and situations in which they are used.

CMS coded

Settlement Type Literal

When to use

JUD

Judgement

Judicially determined awards. See References for a link to the Guide: 4.3.7.40, Assessing the Compensation Part of a Lump Sum.

CLS

Common law settlement

Use for all common law cases with an economic loss component.

ADV

Advance payment

Use where an advance is paid in anticipation of settling a common law claim.

AOC

Arrears and other costs treated as one lump sum

Use for cases where a person receives a lump sum payment of arrears of periodic compensation included with other payments, such as an impairment payment.

SLS

Statutory lump sum

Use when the compensation is paid due to specific legislation.

SRE

Statutory redemption

Use where future periodic compensation must be redeemed or commuted by force of law for example, Future Comcare payments at below minimum weekly rate.

PSR

Partial statutory redemption

Use where periodic payments were reduced in order to obtain the redemption but have since ceased.

RED

Redemption only

Use where future periodic compensation is redeemed at customer's choice.

ROC

Redemption and other costs

Used if a redemption or commutation of future periodic compensation is included with other costs. For example, future periodic compensation and medical costs in the one payment.

NEL

Non-economic loss lump sum

Any lump sum payment other than an impairment payment where it is accepted that the payment does not contain loss of earnings or loss of earning capacity.

OLS

Overseas lump sum

Any lump sum paid outside Australia.

EXG

Ex-gratia payment

Payments where there is no statutory or common law basis for a claim to succeed.

IMP

Impairment payment

Statutory table payment for bodily impairment and pain and suffering.

For example, Vic S98A/S98C, NSW S66/S67, W.A. S24, QLD S179, TAS S71, S.A. S58, ACT S51 and NT S71.

Lump Sum Assessment Results screen

The Lump Sum Assessment Results screen provides details of what the system thinks has been determined to be the customer's correct entitlement to the compensation affected payments (CAPs) received during the estimated preclusion period.

The Lump Sum Assessment Results screen details should be correct if there have been either:

  • no debts raised during the estimated debt period or
  • debts that have been raised automatically and recovered for the calculated amount

Starting a debt automatically reduces the determination file on which the Lump Sum Assessment Results screen is based.

Check and adjust the Lump Sum Assessment Results screen:

  • If a debt has been automatically raised in the period and the estimated amount is different to the calculated amount, the Lump Sum Assessment Results screen will need adjustment by the difference between the two
  • If a debt has been finalised no debt, the estimated debt amount will need to be added back in
  • If a debt has been waived in the period, the debt remains waived unless it is a small debt waiver. The Lump Sum Assessment Results screen will need to be adjusted to add back in debts waived because they were less than $50
  • When a manual debt has been created and the determination file is not adjusted, the Lump Sum Assessment Results screen will need to be adjusted to take account of the manual debt
  • If manual payments have been issued via the Payment Adjustment (PAYJ) screen for reason system limitation (SYL), the Lump Sum Assessment Results screen must be corrected
  • If the debt is for older periods before 1998 when Newstart Common Platform commenced - add in any missing payments

Note: no adjustment is needed for debts due to internal transfers, duplicate payments, advances and Family Tax Benefit (FTB) payments.

Adjust the Lump Sum Assessment Results screen where necessary. The Payment Details Download Sheet (PS Tool) can be used as a tool to reconcile payments received with the details in the Lump Sum on Assessment Results screen if necessary.

It should be noted that the PS tool uses the payment history that is different in nature to the Lump Sum Assessment Results screen data. For further information, see Payment Details Download Sheet (PS Tool).

Explaining a lump sum preclusion period to the customer

  • "When a person receives a lump sum payment of compensation, Centrelink uses a formula to calculate a non-payment period, referred to as the ‘preclusion period’, during which there is no entitlement to most social security income support payments"
  • "During this period you are not able to receive income support from Centrelink"
  • "It is important to note that your partner does not have a preclusion period and may continue receiving an income support payments subject to the appropriate income and assets tests"
  • "It is important to note that you understand that you need to use some of the money you receive from your settlement to support you for the duration of your preclusion period and to cover all your living costs. Please refer to the back of your letter for more details"
  • "If you use some of your compensation settlement to buy assets (for example purchase or pay off a house or buy a car), you should make sure that you still leave enough aside to live on during the preclusion period. If you don’t do this and then ask Centrelink to review your preclusion period then you will be expected to use or sell the asset for income support throughout the preclusion period. Preclusion periods can only be shortened if you can show that there are unusual, uncommon, or exceptional circumstances that have caused your hardship. You will be expected to be able to support your claims with evidence"
  • "You may also be interested in talking with a Centrelink Financial Information Service Officer who can help you make decisions about your investment and financial situation. The service is free, independent and confidential. You can call 132 300 to make an appointment with a Financial Information Service Officer"

Frequently asked questions

This table provides frequently asked questions and answers to common customer questions about the impacts of their compensation.

Questions

Answer

What is a lump sum preclusion period?

"If you receive a lump sum payment, we may apply a preclusion period, which is a period of time you may not be able to receive an income support payment. This means the money you receive is to support you for the duration of your preclusion period and cover all your living expenses."

What happens if I received a Centrelink payment in the preclusion period?

"If you have received Centrelink payments during the preclusion period (non-payment period), you may have a debt. Generally, this will be deducted from the lump sum payment before it is paid to you."

How does a lump sum compensation payment affect my partner's Centrelink payments?

"It is important to note that your partner does not have a preclusion period and may continue receiving an income support payment subject to the appropriate income and assets tests."

Can I buy a house or pay off my house?

"If you use some of your compensation settlement to buy assets (for example, purchase or pay off a house or buy a car), you should make sure that you still leave enough aside to live on during the preclusion period. If you don’t do this and then ask Centrelink to review your preclusion period then you will be expected to use or sell the asset for income support throughout the preclusion period. Preclusion periods can only be shortened if you can show that there are unusual, uncommon, or exceptional circumstances that have caused your hardship. You will be expected to be able to support your claims with evidence.

You may also be interested in talking with a Centrelink Financial Information Service Officer who can help you make decisions about your investment and financial situation. The service is free, independent and confidential. You can call 132 300 to make an appointment with a Financial Information Service Officer. For more information please visit servicesaustrlaia.gov.au\What other help is available"

What payments are affected by compensation?

"Compensation affects most Centrelink income support payments. Payments impacted by compensation are called ‘compensation-affected payments’ and are defined in the Social Security Act 1991. Age Pension and Disability Support Pension customers who are blind are paid free of the income and assets tests but are affected by the compensation provisions of the Social Security Act 1991."

What payment is not affected by compensation?

"Widows Pension, Wife Age Pension, Bereavement Allowance, Carer Allowance and Mobility Allowance."

Is Family Tax Benefit affected by compensation rules?

"No. The compensation rules do not affect the Family Tax Benefit. However, compensation payments may include a component of taxable income. It is this component that is included as income for Family Tax purposes. For information on the effect of compensation payments on entitlements to Family Tax Benefit or other family assistant payments, please visit serviceaustralia.gov.au\family tax benefit"

Can I get a Health Care Card in the preclusion period?

"To test your eligibility for a Health Care Card, you should contact your local Centrelink office, which can assist in reducing your pharmaceutical costs."

What happens if I do not have any money left and I am still in my preclusion period?

"If you do not agree with a decision we have made about your payments, you have the right to ask for a formal review of the decision. For more information about review and appeal rights, visit servicesaustralia.gov.au/reviews and appeals"

What other help is available?

"For more information please visit servicesaustralia.gov.au

  • What other help is available - Financial Information Services (FIS)

Compensation notices

Preliminary Notice

Is a legal notice issued by Services Australia to a compensation payer obliging them to:

  • notify when liability is accepted, and
  • preventing them from paying the compensation, until
  • they have first repaid the debt amount to Services Australia

Preliminary Notice:

  • Continues to have effect until the compensation payer’s full liability is disclosed, and
  • The compensation payer is obliged to advise the Secretary of any further payments despite the refund already received

Recovery Notice

Is an advice sent to the compensation payer with details of the:

  • amount of a compensation affected payment made to the customer, and
  • amount to recovered from a payment of compensation

A recovery notice cannot be sent:

  • care of the Defendant Solicitor
  • overseas payer or care of their solicitor
  • care of deceased estate or deceased estate solicitors, see Actioning compensation interest checks (CLK)

Relevant divisor

The divisor is applied to the compensation part of the lump sum settlement in order to work out the preclusion period.

If the settlement is/was received on or after

20 March 1997

The compensation part of the lump sum is divided by the income - cut out amount applying at the time the lump sum is received.

The income cut-out amount:

  • is the amount above which no pension is payable to a single person under the ordinary income test

State specific examples

State

Example

Queensland:

For each work related damages settlement, the gross lump sum amount includes:

  • the amount paid under the statutory workers compensation scheme, and
  • any known legal costs

This will include:

  • medical expenses
  • impairment payments
  • periodic compensation
  • Tax and GST paid under the statutory scheme on a no-fault basis.

These amounts must be added to the proposed lump sum. The periodic compensation must be deducted before the application of the 50% rule is applied to obtain the economic loss component of the proposed lump sum.

Although some settlement documents may state that, they waive or forgo the reimbursement, the parties cannot legally contract out of their statutory obligation to reimburse the statutory compensation.

New South Wales:

Claims Assessment and Resolution Service (CARS) Assessment Lump Sum Payments. If the parties agree and the Assessor or Arbitrator is only required to sign off on it:

  • the lump sum is treated as a settlement, and
  • the 50% rule applies

If there is a dispute between the parties and the Assessor or Arbitrator is required to make an assessment, the lump sum is treated as a Judgement.

Settlement document may state ‘the economic loss is to be reduced by the section 83 payments’, CRT are to disregard this. Section 83 of the NSW Motor Accident Compensation Act 1999 – is paid for hospital, medical and other expenses.
