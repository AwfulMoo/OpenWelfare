Disputed care

If customers are advising of a potential disputed care situation or a disagreement about care, transfer the customer to the Integrated Care Team, see Customer referral guidelines for Child Support staff. An Integrated Care Service Officer will manage customers who disagree about their care or who are advising of disputed care. See the Background page in Integrated Care for information about roles and responsibilities.

Table 1: this table contains details for determining if care is disagreed or disputed.

Step

Action

1

Determine if care is disagreed or disputed + Read more ...

Review the child’s current care records to establish whether the care is based on a written care arrangement, as this will determine what evidence needs to be obtained and the type of decision that needs to be considered. If care is based on a care arrangement but is recorded as ‘actual’, care must be re-keyed to reflect the correct type of care arrangement.

Disputed care should not be confused with disagreements about care.

For the purposes of FTB and Child Support, a dispute in relation to the care of a child exists when a written care arrangement applies in relation to a child/ren and care is not in accordance with the written arrangement.

A care arrangement will be considered to apply if:

  • it is followed by the parents immediately prior to the change in care, or
  • the change in care occurs before the care provided for under the care arrangement could be established

In these cases, there is no disagreement about how much care is actually being provided for the child.

Care will be regarded as being disputed if:

  • a care arrangement applies in relation to a child/ren, and
  • no previous interim care determination has been made regarding this care arrangement, for a care event on or after 23 May 2018, and
  • the customer with less care as allocated in the care arrangement is taking reasonable action to regain the care, see Step 5 in the Contact customer and investigate care table

Disagreements about care occur where the customers disagree on the details of the care change. The percentage of care in such cases is generally determined on the basis of actual care provided see Step 1 in the Contact customer and Investigate care table.

If care is not disputed or disagreed, make an actual care determination. See Integrated Care - Making care determination.

If at any stage of the decision-making process the carers disagree about the facts, go to Step 1 in the Contact customer and investigate care table.

2

Reasonable action by person with reduced care + Read more ...

Ascertain details of any reasonable action taken by the person with reduced care. Explain disputed care and interim periods to them and discuss the decision-making process.

See the References page for a link to the Child Support Guide 2.2.4: Disputed Care Arrangements.

Examples of reasonable action for the person with reduced care include:

  • initiating or participating in court action for contravention of a court order
  • obtaining or seeking legal advice regarding the making of a court order
  • initiating mediation, through a Family Relationship Centre or other service, to re-establish the care arrangement
  • negotiating with the other parent with a view to re-establishing the care arrangement

The person with reduced care is not required to provide evidence they are taking reasonable action unless that fact is disputed by the other carer. If the other carer disputes that reasonable action is being taken, go to Step 3 in the Contact customer and Investigate care table.

Note: where the customer was not aware of reasonable action requirements and available options, this action should be taken within a specified timeframe after being advised of this process. The person with reduced care must continue taking reasonable action for the interim period to continue to apply. If reasonable action ceases, the interim period will end on that date and the care percentages will be based on the care that is actually occurring from the day after reasonable action ceases.

3

Are there special circumstances? + Read more ...

For care periods commencing on or after 1 July 2012 (22 November 2012 for WA ex-nuptial cases), a decision can be made to apply actual care immediately where special circumstances make an interim period inappropriate, see WA ex-nuptial cases.

Examine the circumstances which led to the change in care including whether the behaviour and/or actions of the parent who has less care was a cause for the change in care. These circumstances should be a significant cause for the change in care. They may include unusual or unreasonable actions and behaviour of the parent with reduced care, however they can also arise due to factors beyond the parent's direct control. In determining whether a person’s behaviour or actions have been unusual or unreasonable, consider the impact of the person’s behaviour or actions on the child or the person with increased care, and if the child or the person with increased care was placed in a situation where there was substantial risk to their physical, emotional or physiological well-being.

Examples of where a parent is withholding care due to a substantial risk to the wellbeing of the child include the person with reduced care:

  • withholding essential medical care from the child
  • substantially failing to comply with legal schooling requirements
  • neglecting the child’s basic needs

This list is not exhaustive. Special circumstances will vary with each case. Situations that may occur in the ordinary course of events will not be considered to be special. For example, changing preferences of the parents or child in relation to care will not constitute special circumstances.

See the References page for a link to the Child Support Guide 2.2.4: Disputed Care Arrangements and seek Technical support in Child Support if required.

Note: if it is identified that a customer may have experienced, or be at risk of, family and domestic violence, see Family and domestic violence.

Eligible cases must be referred to an Actual Care in Special Circumstances (ACSC) Officer for managing and making care determinations to apply actual care in special circumstances.

To refer a case to an ACSC officer, see Actual care in Special Circumstances table.

If there are no special circumstances go to Step 4

4

Interim care period + Read more ...

A person's percentage of care is generally determined according to the actual care that they have of the child. However, where care is disputed, a person's percentage of care may be determined according to a care arrangement (written agreement, parenting plan or court order) for an interim period, rather than being based on actual care. The parent with reduced care must take reasonable action to have the care arrangement complied with in order for the care percentages to be based on the care arrangement for the interim period.

Interim care decisions require two care percentage determinations, one based on the actual care and the other based on the care set out in the care arrangement, which applies for an interim period.

The length of the interim period will depend on:

  • the type of care arrangement (court order, parenting plan or written care agreement)
  • the age of the care arrangement when care changed, and
  • in some cases, whether the person with more actual care than set out in the written care arrangement takes reasonable action to participate in an approved Family Dispute Resolution process

For more information, see the Interim Periods tables in Resources.

5

Determine whether the length of the interim period could be affected by the person with increased care taking reasonable action + Read more ...

The length of the interim period may be reduced if the person with increased care takes reasonable action to participate in an approved Family Dispute Resolution process if the:

  • Court order commenced more than 26 weeks prior to the care change, or
  • Parenting plan/written care agreement commenced more than 38 weeks prior to the care change

If the length of the interim period cannot be affected by the person with increased care taking reasonable action, go to Step 1 in the Decision table.

6

Reasonable action by person with increased care to participate in approved Family Dispute Resolution + Read more ...

If the length of the interim period could be affected by the person with increased care taking reasonable action, ascertain the details of reasonable action taken by the person with increased care. Explain disputed care and interim periods to them and discuss the decision-making process.

The person with increased care is not required to provide evidence they are taking reasonable action unless that fact is disputed by the other carer. If the other carer disputes that reasonable action is being taken, go to Step 4 in the Contact customer and Investigate care table.

See the References page for a link to the Child Support Guide 2.2.4: Disputed Care Arrangements.

The person with increased care should be asked to provide the date of the change of care and the date they commenced taking reasonable action. If there is a gap of more than two weeks between these dates, the person with increased care should be asked to provide information on the reason for the delay in taking reasonable action so a determination can be made as to whether or not they have taken reasonable action within a ‘reasonable period’.

A ‘reasonable period’ will generally be within around two weeks of the change in care but it may be longer, depending on individual circumstances.

Note: the person with increased care must continue taking reasonable action to receive the benefit of a reduced interim period. If they cease taking reasonable action to participate in Family Dispute Resolution the interim period will be extended until the end of the maximum interim period or, if the interim period has already ended, the interim period will be reinstated until the end of the maximum interim period.

Go to Step 1 in the Decision table.

Contact customer and investigate care

Table 2: this table describes the process for contacting customer and investigating disputed care and disagreements about care.

Step

Action

1

Contact customers for information or evidence + Read more ...

Each customer must provide relevant information and evidence to support their claims.

Note: contact with incarcerated customers is by phone appointment only. Contact the Incarcerated Customer team to arrange an appointment using the Incarcerated Customer Appointment Email template located on the Resources page.

Contact each customer and explain the disputed care determination will be based on information and evidence that establishes care-related facts to support:

  • the care that is most likely to be occurring (including care other than nights), see the Child Support Guide 2.2.1: Basics of care
  • whether the established pattern of care has changed or not, see Resources page, Example 3 for more information
  • that the new pattern of care is likely to continue into the future (and is not a one-off minor change such as occasionally missing a weekend of care due to work or illness). See the Child Support Guide 2.2.1: Basics of care
  • for disputed care, evidence of reasonable action being taken, including the date it commenced/ended

Provide the customer with options to provide supporting documentation. The fastest way to do this is via myGov/Child Support online services. The webmaster email address can be offered if they are not eligible for online services to enable customers to supply information quickly. Customers must be advised that the webmaster email address is not a secure method of communication.

Advise both customers the care decision is a Services Australia decision about the amount of care used in the customer’s child support and/or FTB assessment. The agency does not determine where the child should live or any custody arrangements. The agency reflects the percentage of care of the child that each parent actually has, or where a written arrangement applies and the person with reduced care is taking reasonable action to have the arrangement enforced, the percentage of care established by a written care arrangement for an interim period.

The circumstances around care changes can be distressing, especially where the care is disputed or not occurring in line with a written care arrangement. Be considerate and sensitive to the customer’s experience when discussing and explaining care changes.

Note: if one of the customers cannot be contacted by phone to discuss the disagreed or disputed care and/or request evidence, follow the steps outlined in Step 5 of the Integrated care - Gathering additional information and evidence, Contact customer table and return to this process.

A care terminating event will occur for a child if the agency is notified of a care change more than 26 weeks after the date of event and as a result of the care change:

  • all eligible carers cease to be eligible carers for the child, i.e. their care reduces below 35%; and
  • a person who wasn’t an eligible carer would become an eligible carer for the child except for the date of effect rules effective from 1 July 2018

If a parent/carer’s assessment is likely to end due to a care terminating event ask them if they would like to apply today for a new child support assessment, to be used in case their care change causes their assessment for a child/ren to end. Advise the parent/carer that if they do not make an application today and their care change causes their assessment to end, the assessment can only be restarted from the day we receive an application for assessment.

If the parent/carer does wish to make an application for a child support assessment, document their response clearly in the same notepad/Pluto process that records the care notification. The restart cannot be processed until the care determination is finalised.

2

Supporting information and evidence + Read more ...

Inform each customer that the information and evidence they provide will be:

  • considered in making a care percentage decision
  • discussed with the other customer
  • made available to the other customer (including evidence a customer submits from third parties), except where:
    • this information is sensitive. This information will be considered in the care determination however unnecessary or private information will not be made available (for example, third party evidence from a Social Worker or a Women’s Refuge). Although the evidence will not be made available to the other party, the principles of procedural fairness require Child Support to advise all the relevant parties what information is being taken into account when a decision is being made. Discuss with the customer the information the decision maker will need to make a decision. Advise the customer, in the event of an Administrative Appeals Tribunal (AAT) first review, the information may be considered and exchanged with the other parent. If a customer or third party does not consent to their evidence or claim being disclosed to the other customer, it will not be considered in the decision

Inform each customer that:

  • information from children is not accepted and such evidence will be disregarded and returned to the parent who sent it
  • statements from third parties must maintain the privacy of the customers:
    • Third parties will not be contacted without the consent of the parent who provided the information and Service Officers must not imply that the third party is obliged to provide information. If a parent provides a written statement from a third party, it will be inferred they have consented to the third party being contacted. See ‘Supporting evidence’ in the Child Support Guide 2.2.1: Basics of care
  • the agency may initiate searches for evidence that will be discussed

In the event a customer does not provide information or evidence to support their claims, the agency may base the care determination on information or evidence received from the other customer or third parties. See the Child Support Guide 2.2.1: Basics of care.

If there is no care arrangement in place, go to Step 5, otherwise go to Step 4.

3

Reasonable action + Read more ...

If a care arrangement is in place, ask the customer with less care than set out in the care arrangement to provide evidence they are taking reasonable action to enforce care as outlined in the care arrangement.

See the Child Support Guide 2.2.4: Disputed Care Arrangements.

Examples of reasonable action for the person with reduced care include:

  • Initiating or participating in court action for contravention of a court order
  • obtaining or seeking legal advice regarding the making of a court order
  • initiating mediation, through a Family Relationship Centre or other service, to re-establish the care arrangement

Note: where the customer was not aware of reasonable action requirements and available options, this action should be taken within a specified timeframe after being advised of this process. The person with reduced care must continue taking reasonable action for the interim period to continue to apply. Id reasonable action ceases, the interim period will end on that date and the care percentage will be based on the care that is actually occurring. Go to step 5 for information on relevant evidence.

4

Reasonable action by person with increased care + Read more ...

If the length of the interim period would be affected by the person with increased care taking reasonable action, ask them to provide evidence they are taking reasonable action to participate in an approved Family Dispute Resolution process.

See Child Support Guide 2.2.4: Disputed Care Arrangements.

The person with increased care should also provide evidence of the date of the change of care and the date they commenced taking reasonable action. If there is a gap of more than two weeks between these dates, the person with increased care should be asked to provide information on the reason for the delay in taking reasonable action so a determination can be made as to whether or not they have taken reasonable action within a ‘reasonable period’.

A ‘reasonable period’ will generally be within around two weeks of the change in care but it may be longer, depending on individual circumstances.

Note: the person with increased care must continue taking reasonable action to receive the benefit of a reduced interim period. If they cease taking reasonable action to participate in Family Dispute Resolution the interim period will be extended until the end of the maximum interim period or, if the interim period has already ended, the interim period will be reinstated until the end of the maximum interim period.

5

Establish timeframes for submission of information or evidence + Read more ...

Establish timeframes of up to 14 days with each customer to provide them with relevant factual information and evidence that may include:

  • A copy of the care arrangement
  • documentary evidence (diary or calendar entries) recording the actual care of the child
  • contact details for day-care provider or the child’s school
  • contact details for regular extra-curricular activities
  • if customers live a considerable distance apart, enrolment details for day-care or school
  • details regarding the usual location of the child's belongings
  • evidence regarding visits to health care providers
  • evidence from the person with reduced care that they are taking reasonable action to have the care arrangement complied with
  • evidence from the person with increased care that they are taking reasonable action to participate in Family Dispute Resolution
  • proof of airline booking or passport copies
  • information from third parties

Note: customers receiving FTB must be given 14 calendar days to provide evidence that they are taking reasonable action. If 1 party to the care change is in receipt of FTB, then both must be given 14 days to provide evidence See Family Assistance Guide 2.1.1.70: Disputed Care Arrangements.

6

No conclusive evidence or information provided + Read more ...

Inform customers that in the event:

  • there is no evidence provided by 1 of the parties
  • neither customer provides conclusive information or evidence, or
  • both customers provide conflicting information that cannot be reconciled

the information received will need to be reviewed to determine whether the current care percentages recorded on the assessments are correct and if not a new care percentage determination will be made.

Go to Step 3 in the Decision table.

7

Summarise agreed actions + Read more ...

Summarise the discussion to include:

  • care-related facts that have been agreed on by the customer
  • suitable evidence customers need to provide
  • the date by which the evidence must be received by the agency
  • the customers have objection and review rights to the decision, regardless of whether the care percentage is changed
  • whether the interim period will apply for FTB purposes only or both FTB and Child Support

Note: for mutual customers, if the care change happened more than 28 days from the date of event, a different date of effect will apply to the customers’ relevant child support and family assistance assessments, see Step 5 in the Integrated Care - Recording care determination, Care determination table.

Offer the customer referrals to support services where appropriate, see Social worker and other service referrals for child support customers.

Document the conversation, (see Documenting Child Support information). Pend a disputed care record in Cuba, (see Care Cuba Process Help) or enter the disputed care details in Customer First (see Decision table, steps 4 to 6). To assess the effect on the customer’s FTB, see Assessment of care arrangements for Family Tax Benefit (FTB).

Review evidence received + Read more ...

Sufficient and reliable evidence to support decision + Read more ...

The information and evidence provided must be sufficient and reliable to enable a care determination to be made as to whether the care percentage has changed or not.   • on the care that is actually occurring, go to Step 4
  • for Actual Care in Special Circumstances (ACSC), go to Step 7
  • on the care provided by the care arrangement for an interim period, go to Step 5

3

Inconclusive or lack of evidence + Read more ...

If no supporting evidence is received from 1 of the customers, or if it is inconclusive, make the decision on the best information available. Make a care percentage determination only if satisfied care has changed. If the Service Officer is satisfied that the actual care determination have been met, determine the care percentage in line with the actual care that is occurring.

7

Review evidence for actual care in special circumstances (ASCS) officer only + Read more ...

Review evidence and information that support relevant findings of fact. Allegations of abuse, family and domestic violence or similar that are not supported by independent evidence are unlikely to result in a decision to apply actual care instead of an interim determination. A child welfare order is not a requirement for this decision to be made.

Evidence from an independent third party that may indicate special circumstances exist include (but is not limited to):

  • a police report detailing violent behaviour to a child or the person with increased care
  • an intervention order preventing contact with the child or the person with increased care
  • statement from a medical (or other relevant) professional regarding assault or abuse of the child or the person with increased care

Note: a phone call will often be required to the relevant authorities in order to obtain the required information in these situations. The Service Officer should seek support from their aligned Service Support Officer (SSO) if they are unsure what to discuss. Do not accept evidence directly from children. Where a parent provides written evidence from the child, do not consider the information and advise the parent/customer we will not be considering this for evidence purposes.

Changes within the Maximum Interim Period (MIP)

Table 4: this table describes details to assist when changes in circumstances occur within the maximum interim period.

Step

Action

1

Changes of care during an interim period - + Read more ...

Where care changes during an interim period and a customer notified of that change during the interim period, the new care percentage will take effect from the end of the interim period.

If the change in care occurs within the interim period but is notified after the end of that period, but within the maximum interim period, the change in care must be applied from the date of notification.

If a care changes occurs during an interim period and that change is notified of after the end of the maximum interim period, the normal care date of effect rules will apply. However, the earliest any change to care can be applied from is the day after the end of the interim period.

Go to Integrated Care - Recording care determination.

2

Changes of care after an interim period but within the maximum interim period + Read more ...

For court orders, the maximum interim period is the later of:

  • the end of the period of 52 weeks starting on the day the court order first takes effect, or
  • the end of the period of 26 weeks starting on the change of care day

For parenting plans and written care agreements the maximum interim period will always be the end of the period of 14 weeks starting on the change of care day.

The first change of care notified of after the end of an interim period but before the end of the maximum interim period can only be given effect from the date of notification, for both child support and family assistance purposes. System support is not currently able to reflect this rule so care changes that fall after the interim period but before the end of the maximum interim period will need to have the date of event recorded as the date of notification.

If a subsequent care change occurs, the normal date of effect rules apply for Child Support and FTB purposes.

The normal date of effect rules apply, for Child Support and FTB purposes if the care changes:

  • after the end of the interim period, and
  • before the end of the maximum interim period, and
  • is notified of after the end of the maximum interim period

Go to Integrated Care - Recording care determination.

3

Reasonable action by the person with increased care ceases before the end of the maximum interim period + Read more ...

If the person with increased care ceases to take continuous reasonable action within the interim period the interim period will be extended until the end of the maximum interim period.

If the person with increased care ceases to take reasonable action after the end of the interim period, but within the maximum interim period, the interim period will resume from the date reasonable action ceased and it will continue until the end of the maximum interim period.

For information on the processing steps required to give effect to changes in 'reasonable action' by the person with increased care, seer to the Modify Interim care record following changes in reasonable action table in Care Cuba Process Help.

Where there has been a change of care prior to the interim period resuming, a review should be set on the interim care record that matures the day after the end of the interim period. A review is needed because the legislation requires a new determination of care to be made after the end of the interim period. Although Cuba may already have the correct care percentage recorded, a new care determination must be made so a care decision letter with objection rights can be issued to customers.

4

Reasonable action by the person with increased care does not commence within a reasonable period or resumes after earlier ceasing + Read more ...

If the person with increased care did not take reasonable action within a reasonable period but later does so, including where reasonable action was taken, ceased and later resumed, the interim period will continue:

  • for court orders, for 14 weeks from the date reasonable action resumed, or
  • for parenting plans and written care agreements, for 4 weeks from the date reasonable action resumed.

In either case, the interim period may not extend beyond the end of the maximum interim period.

For information on the processing steps required to give effect to changes in 'reasonable action' by the person with increased care, see Modify Interim care record following changes in reasonable action table in Care Cuba Process Help.

5

Ending an interim period due to a change of circumstances + Read more ...

The interim period will cease if the parent with reduced care in relation to the written care arrangement stops taking reasonable action or if a new care arrangement is made. If the customers make a new care arrangement, and actual care is in line with that arrangement, make a new decision based on actual care to apply from the date the new care arrangement commences.

If a terminating event occurs for the case or the child within the interim period, adjust the interim period to end on the day before the terminating event, see Terminating events (Stage 2) (CS). For FTB purposes, see Family Assistance Guide 2.1.1.70: Disputed Care Arrangements on how to treat payments in the event an interim period ends earlier.

If the case or assessment restarts for a child, the interim period does not recommence and a new interim care determination cannot be made. A new care determination must be made from the date the assessment restarts. This is because an interim care determination may not be made if there has already been an earlier interim care determination made in respect of the care arrangement. Actual care will be used to determine the percentage of care from the date of notification.

Go to Integrated Care - Recording care determination.

Actual Care in Special Circumstances (ACSC)

1

Referral to an ACSC Officer + Read more ...

All staff must refer eligible cases to a specialised team. See Customer referral guidelines for Child Support staff using the ACSC Referral and Decision template. The ACSC Officer will make the final decision whether or not to apply actual care in special circumstances and apply this to the customer’s Centrelink and Child Support records.

2

Complete referral - Part 1-3 (Service Officer) + Read more ...

Using the information and evidence available, discuss the case with a SSO before sending the referral to your Team Leader. Consult with the SSO to ensure all relevant information is obtained and recorded

Complete Part 1-3 of the ACSC Referral and Decision template, refer to the template for details of where to forward the referral.

3

Complete decision - Part 4 (ACSC Officer) + Read more ...

Check the case is suitable for consideration of ACSC. Review the customers’ claims and identify evidence required to make a decision.

Contact both parties to discuss the evidence received and any additional information that is required, be mindful that conversations with customers may be of a sensitive nature. Speak to the Team Leader/PSM for support in managing the calls if necessary. Where appropriate, refer the customer to a Social worker and other service referrals for child support customers.

Where it is indicated by the customers/evidence received that third parties (police, child protection etc) may be able to contribute further information regarding whether special circumstances exist, make contact and gather this additional information from the third parties directly.

Evaluate conflicting information and notify and liaise with a Program Support Manager for guidance and support on policy and decision-making.

As these decisions relate to potentially sensitive issues, make a decision as soon as possible and minimise the number of times customers/carers are contacted for further information or evidence.

Consider the facts of the case and complete Part 4 of the ACSC Referral and Decision template to either accept actual care in special circumstances or set an interim period of 4-52 weeks.
