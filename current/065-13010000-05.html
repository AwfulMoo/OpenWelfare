Summary of Home Care Packages terms

This table provides a summary of home care packages terms and what they include.

Term

Definition

Age eligibility

A care recipient can either be:

  • an older person who needs coordinated services to help them stay at home, or
  • a younger person under 65 years old :
    • who has a disability that is permanent and significantly affects their communication, mobility, self-care or self-management, and
    • their care needs are not provided through other specialist services (for example, through the National Disability Insurance Scheme (NDIS))

Approved home care providers

To become an approved home care provider, the approved home care provider:

  • must meet eligibility requirements as set out in the Aged Care Quality and Safety Commission Act 2018
  • have an understanding of the approved providers responsibilities as set out in the Aged Care Act 1997, and
  • deliver care that aligns with the associated Principles made under section 96-1 of the Aged Care Act 1997

Providers must:

  • be a corporation
  • not have any disqualified individuals as key personnel
  • have experience providing aged care or other relevant forms of care
  • understand approved provider responsibilities
  • have, or will have, systems in place to meet their responsibilities
  • have, or will have, sound financial management records and practices
  • comply with financial obligations and other responsibilities as an aged care provider, if they already are one

The Aged Care Quality and Safety Commission assess and approve all applications to become an approved home care provider.

Care and services

A single list of care and services applies to all package levels. See the References page for a link to the Quality of Care Principles 2014. Note: the care and services listed is not exhaustive.

All package levels will have:

  • greater flexibility through other care and services needed to support the care recipient at home
  • access to nursing, allied health and other clinical services
  • access to a wider range of aids and equipment
  • limited number of specified exclusions

Care fees (care recipient contribution fees)

For pre-1 July 2014 care recipients:

  • May be asked to pay a Basic Daily Fee (BDF) plus
    • an Income Tested Fee (ITF) of up to 50% of the income above the basic rate of single Age Pension
  • The provider is responsible for calculating the income tested fee
  • There are no reductions to provider subsidies and supplements where the care recipient pays an income tested fee
  • The BDF will continue to be indexed on 20 March and 20 September each year in line with changes to the Age Pension
  • Hardship provisions do not apply, care recipients can negotiate lower fees directly with the home care provider

For post-1 July 2014 care recipients:

  • May be asked to pay a BDF plus
    • an Income Tested Care Fee (ITCF), which is different for everyone as based on the individual’s income
  • Services Australia is responsible for calculating the ITF
  • Provider subsidies and supplements are reduced by the ITCF amount
  • The BDF will continue to be 20 March and 20 September each year in line with changes to the Age Pension
  • Hardship provisions may apply

Pre-1 July 2019 Basic Daily fee – for all care recipients:

  • The BDF was equal to 17.5% of the basic rate of the single Age Pension across all levels of care
  • The BDF was indexed on 20 March and 20 September each year in line with changes to the Age Pension

From 1 July 2019 – for all care recipients:

  • From 1 July 2019, there are 4 levels of BDF aligning to the 4 levels of home care packages.
  • The 4 levels of BDF replaced the existing single BDF
  • Refer to the Department of Health and Aged Care’s Schedule of Fees and Charges for the current home care BDF
  • The BDF will continue to be indexed on 20 March and 20 September each year in line with changes to the Age Pension

Charter of Aged Care Rights (Charter)

On 1 July 2019, a single Charter of Aged Care Rights (the Charter) came into effect. It replaced the previous charters of care recipients’ rights and responsibilities.

The Charter protects:

  • the rights of care recipients, and
  • provides the same rights to all care recipients, regardless of the type of subsidised care and services they receive

Customer Directed Care (CDC)

Since 2015, all home care packages have been consumer directed. This means care recipients work with their aged care service to design their home care package. Care recipients have more choice over the services they receive, how and when they receive them and who provides them.

Goods and Services (GST) impacts

The supply of home care is GST free if home care subsidy is payable under Part 3.2 of the Aged Care Act 1997 or Part 3.2 of the Aged Care (Transitional Provisions) Act 1997.

See the A New Tax System (Goods and Services Tax) Act 1999 for further information (Division 38, Part 3-1, Chapter 3).

Home Care Agreement

Agreement between the home care provider and the care recipient (or their nominee).

Leave provisions (determining how long and at what rate the home care subsidy will be paid when a care recipient takes leave from a package)

Home care recipients are entitled to take leave. Their access to aged care service is not affected while they are on leave. The leave impacts on aged care subsidy and supplements depends on the leave type and leave duration. For home care recipients, leave balances reset at two specific events:

  • on 1 July each year, or
  • when their package level changes at any time

See the Department of Health and Aged Care’s Temporary leave from Home Care Packages for further information.

National Screening and Assessment Form (NSAF)

The NSAF provides approval for a care recipient’s eligibility to government-funded care services. It also outlines the level of care the care recipient is eligible for.

Aged Care Assessment Team (ACAT) assessors from My Aged Care will conduct an assessment on the recipient’s care needs. After the assessment, ACAT assessors will complete the NSAF.

Services Australia receives care approval details on the NSAF transmitted via the My Aged Care gateway.

Portability of package assignments

From 27 February 2017

Home care packages are allocated via the Department of Health and Aged Care’s national priority system. The Aged Care Approvals Round (ACAR) ceased.

Care recipients who are receiving home care from an aged care service can move to another service, taking their home care package with them. This enables home care subsidy to follow the care recipient upon Services Australia receiving the Aged Care Entry Record form (AC021) from the new service.

Prior to 27 February 2017

Home care providers applied for government-subsided home care places through the Care Approvals Round (ACAR) process.

Supplements

Dementia and Cognition Supplement or Veterans' Supplement applies to all package levels. Note: where a home care recipient is eligible for both the Veterans' Supplement and the Dementia and Cognition Supplement, the aged care service will only receive the Veterans' Supplement for the care recipient.

Oxygen and Enteral Feeding Supplement applies to home care recipients across all package levels. Note: both supplements can be claimed if the home care recipient is eligible.

Viability Supplement applies to home care services with the cost of providing services in rural and remote areas.

Aged care financial hardship assistance - overview (Hardship) – post 1 July 2014 home care recipients can apply for this supplement if they are in financial hardship.

The Aged Care Quality Standards (the Standards)

The Aged Care Quality Standards (the Standards) apply to all Australian Government-funded aged care services.

The Standards are set out in legislation. See the References page for a link to the Quality of Care Principles 2014.

Unspent home care amounts

27 February 2017:

Unspent home care amounts are the amounts that have been claimed by a service following the delivery of care to a care recipient but have not been fully utilised (spent).

  • Unspent Amounts must follow the care recipient if they move to another service (portability)
  • Unspent amounts must be returned to the care recipient and the Commonwealth, minus an exit fee if in place, when the care recipient permanently exits home care, or when the care recipient fails to re-enter care within 56 days (84 days with extension)
  • The Commonwealth (CW) portion of an unspent home care amount (CW Unspent Amount) is returned to Services Australia via the existing monthly claim reconciliation process
  • The CW Unspent Amount is advised by the service as a dollars and cents amount on the monthly claim form (paper or the Aged Care Provider Portal)

1 September 2021:

  • Unspent Amounts are accrued with Services Australia (on a care recipient’s ‘home care account’) rather than with services
  • Services have a 6 month window to opt to return Commonwealth portion of unspent amounts currently on their books
  • Unspent Amounts are recovered as part of the existing monthly claim reconciliation process
  • Any CW Unspent Amounts not returned as part of the opt-in period must be returned to Services Australia within 70 days of the care recipient’s departure date. This applies for all departures including transfers between services. Services are required to report on existing CW Unspent Amounts that they hold for care recipients
