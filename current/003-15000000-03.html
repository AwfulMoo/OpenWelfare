If a customer discloses this without asking them the Risk Identification Question.

As examples: 'I am scared and worried that I’m going to be hurt'; 'He/she won’t let me leave my home' or 'He/she won’t let me have any money so I can’t go anywhere'.

At interaction points for Centrelink, Child Support and Medicare + Read more ...

To proactively identify customers who may be affected by family and domestic violence, staff need to ask the customer the Risk Identification Question at the following interaction points:

Centrelink

  • all enquiries when a customer advises they have separated in the last 6 months
  • all Parenting Payment Single (PPS) new claims
  • when a customer requests Person Permitted to Enquire (PPE) to be set to 'no'
  • when a customer updates their address only, not their partner's
  • regular re-issue of BasicsCard, regular changing of BasicsCard PIN and/or change of daily spending limit and a customer reports a lost, stolen or damaged BasicsCard
  • appointing, reviewing and cancelling a nominee arrangement
  • claiming an emergency payment such as Australian Government Disaster Recovery Payment (AGDRP) or Disaster Recovery Allowance (DRA)

Child Support

  • applications for a child support assessment
  • when a customer elects to end a child support assessment or ends collection of arrears
  • applications to change collection method (opt in and opt out)
  • change of assessment applications
  • litigation - receiving parents only

Medicare

  • all enquiries when a customer advises they have separated
  • all enquiries when a person wants to copy/transfer themselves, or another person (for example, a dependant) from their Medicare card due to separation or family breakdown

If the customer's enquiry is an interaction point, go to Step 6.

Cues at non-interaction points + Read more ...

There are a number of signs that a customer may be affected by family and domestic violence. In all customer interactions, be alert and responsive to:

Visual cues, such as:

  • physical signs including bruises
  • attempts to hide injuries
  • mood changes (particularly over long-term interactions)
  • appearing fearful
  • exhibiting emotional distress
  • apparent heightened anxiety or withdrawal around a certain person

Conversational cues, such as:

  • unwillingness or inability to share basic information
  • customer asks questions about their privacy or worried about the security of their information
  • subtle disclosures about such things as their safety or fears, for example: ‘I feel afraid/I’m never safe’
  • sounding upset or tearful
  • tone, for example, whispering or speaking in a low or shaky voice

Administrative cues, such as:

  • unwillingness to agree to a new correspondence and/or payment nominee
  • unaware of a current payment nominee arrangement or that the payment nominee has changed
  • repeat applications or requests for help (such as disputed care)

If any of the above are identified, this is a cue to ask the Risk Identification Question. Go to Step 6.

Customer has previously identified family and domestic violence concerns + Read more ...

If FDV has been recorded on the customer’s record:

  • Do not mention the document specifically
  • Do not ask the Risk Identification Question
  • Select the document for further information, including whether the customer has previously been provided support in relation to family and domestic violence. This will help with the follow-up question

Do not conduct any follow-up with the customer if:

  • the customer is with another person who is known to them or who can hear the conversation (this does not include qualified interpreters)
  • the customer is on speaker phone, or
  • it is suspected that the customer is at immediate risk of harm

If it is safe to do so, ask the following question (or similar):

'I note that we have previously discussed safety/security concerns with you. Do you require any specific/further support?'

6

Risk Identification Question - exceptions + Read more ...

Do not ask the Risk Identification Question when the customer:

  • is with another person who is known to them or who can hear the conversation (this does not include qualified interpreters)
  • is on speaker phone
  • has stated that they perpetrate violence, or
  • has already identified FDV concerns, for example, has a Display on Access FDV Concern Identified Fast Note on their Centrelink record coded as SCC Special Contact Considerations - sensitive issue

Do any of the above exceptions apply?

  • Yes,
    • Do not ask the Risk Identification question
    • If there are concerns for the customer’s safety and the Risk Identification Question cannot be asked, these concerns can be raised with a social worker
    • Go to Step 12
  • No, go to Step 7

7

The Risk Identification Question + Read more ...

Introduce the Risk Identification Question to the customer at a suitable point in the conversation. The introduction can be paraphrased, but the bolded words must be stated:

  • 'I would like to ask you a question about your safety and wellbeing. We ask all customers going through this process this question and your answer will be treated as private.
    You don't have to answer this question. We ask so we can offer the right information, services and support to people whose safety and wellbeing may be at risk due to a partner, ex-partner or family member.'

Ask the Risk Identification Question verbatim (word for word):

  • 'Are you, or has anyone else been, concerned about your, or your family's, safety?'

Does the customer confirm they have concerns about their safety?

Customers affected by family and domestic violence may not disclose their situation or accept referral offers for a number of reasons. It is important to respect the customer’s decision if they decline to answer the question or decline a referral to support services.

  • Yes, go to Step 8
  • No, go to Step 12

8

Identify appropriate referrals

Offer referral to a social worker + Read more ...

If the customer is distressed or there are concerns about their immediate wellbeing, refer directly to a social worker with the customer’s permission. Go to Step 9

Within business hours, ask the customer if they would like to be referred to a social worker for support. If possible, it is best to complete the initial interaction and other record changes relating to family and domestic violence before referring the customer. If the customer responds yes, go to Step 13.

Note: there are a number of reasons a customer may not want to speak to a social worker. For example, they may already be engaged with one.

Offer referral to external support service + Read more ...

As well as a social worker referral, staff can offer a referral to an external support service.

If a referral to a social worker is not appropriate (for example, the customer does not wish to speak to a social worker or it is out of business hours), staff can offer a referral to an external support service.

Unless the customer is in immediate danger, or there are concerns they are at risk of harm, it is best to complete the initial interaction and other record changes relating to family and domestic violence before connecting the customer to a service. Go to Step 12.

If the customer agreed to an external referral, and needs it straight away, go to Step 14.

Offer other agency information which can support them + Read more ...

There are a number of customer details, payments and processes to check, which may support customers affected by family and domestic violence. If possible, complete these before transferring the customer to a social worker or external support service. Go to Step 10.

There are also a number of resources that can be given to customers with information on agency support and referral numbers. See the Resources page.

Customer does not accept referral or information + Read more ...

Respect the decision of the customer if they do not wish to accept a referral or information. This can be due to a number of reasons. If appropriate, tell the customer that there is more information and resources on the Services Australia website if they need it.

Go to Step 12.

Family and domestic violence and COVID-19 Vaccination certificates + Read more ...

If a customer is affected by family and domestic violence, and having proof of COVID-19 vaccination causes them safety concerns, there is support available. Services Australia can help customers access safer options for their circumstances, for example:

  • Add their digital COVID-19 certificate to a digital wallet. Their device may allow a customer to use a security pass code to use Apple Wallet or Google Pay. They can set up or change their security pass code through their Settings
  • A customer’s vaccination provider can print their immunisation history statement for them. Customers can also call the Australian Immunisation Register and ask Services Australia to send their proof to them. It can take up to 14 days for a customer’s statement or certificate to arrive in the post
  • If a customer is concerned about unauthorised activity to their online accounts, or the security of their Centrelink, Child Support or Medicare records, they can let Services Australia know. If someone else has access to a customer’s online accounts, they can change their passwords at any time

Document the concerns on the customer’s record + Read more ...

Document on the customer's record all identified family and domestic violence concerns. For example, if the customer responded Yes to the Risk Identification Question.

Do not record on the alleged perpetrator's record family and domestic violence information given by the customer.

If a perpetrator of family and domestic violence self-discloses and it is not a customer aggression incident, no documents are needed on their record.

When documenting FDV concerns, do not record the details of an Apprehended Violence Order (AVO), Domestic Violence Order (DVO), or equivalent documents.

However, if the customer provides an AVO or equivalent documents that are relevant to a claim or assessment in progress, note this in the progress document and only record the required information as per the relevant Operational Blueprint for the claim or assessment.

Information to record

If the customer confirms they are affected by family and domestic violence, record the following on the customer’s record only:

  • The presence of family and domestic violence, with the wording 'family and domestic violence concern identified'
  • Administrative action taken, for example, 'referred to social work for a child support exemption'
  • External referral action taken, for example, 'warm transferred to 1800RESPECT', 'number given for MensLine', 'gave information about FRAL'. This information is only recorded to inform future customer service

Centrelink

Is there already a Display on Access (DOA) FDVCI Fast Note on the customer’s record?

  • Yes, annotate the Fast Note to record any actions/advice. If the customer has accepted further support and has not expressed concern about follow-up questions, extend the expiry date of 6 months from today’s date within the Display on Access template
  • No, create a document using Fast Note. Select Auto Text option Generic > Update > FDV Concern Identified. Delete any pre-filled text that is not relevant to the discussion. Select the DOA box. The Fast Note will become a Display on Access for 6 months

Child Support

Record a document in the Client Profile window with the above information. Add a Sensitive Issues Indicator to the customer's record.

Medicare

Is there already a Family and domestic violence Sensitive Information Indicator in the Sensitive Information tab on the customer’s record in CDMS?

  • Yes, record any actions/advice in the Specialist Referral, Document and Comment fields. If the customer has accepted further support and has not expressed concern about follow-up questions:
    • extend the date in the End Date field to 6 months from today’s date, if no documents provided
    • key the protection period end date in the End Date field, if documents provided
  • No, create a Family and domestic violence Sensitive Information Indicator. See Sensitive Information Indicators in the CDMS for details about indicators

Procedure ends here.

16

Staff support + Read more ...

Speaking to customers about family and domestic violence can be challenging and can affect staff. These effects can be magnified if staff are or have been affected by family and domestic violence.

The agency is committed to raising awareness of family and domestic violence and giving all staff access to a network of support resources and services.

Staff who need support after speaking to a customer about family and domestic violence should:

  • speak to their manager or
  • refer to the Resources page for links to the Helping Staff intranet page and Employee Assistance Program
