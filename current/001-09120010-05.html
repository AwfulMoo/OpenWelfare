Examples of keying temporary incapacity exemptions

Table 1: This table contains examples of keying valid medical certificates and exemptions.

Medical certificate

Examples of how to key temporary incapacity exemption

Calculating 13 weeks

Jenny lodges a medical certificate stating a temporary incapacity from 10 January 2019 to 26 April 2019 (inclusive).

The medical certificate period covers 15 weeks and exceeds the maximum 13 week exemption period for a non-serious illness.

A Service Officer keys the dates for the allowable 13 week period (91 days) only, from Thursday 10 January 2019 to Wednesday 10 April 2019:

  • Week 1 is 7 days from 10-16 January 2019
  • Week 2 is 7 days from 17-23 January 2019

continuing through to:

  • Week 12 is 7 days from 28 March 2019 to 3 April 2019
  • Week 13 is 7 days from 4-10 April 2019

Multiple medical conditions

Barry lodges a medical certificate listing 3 conditions. The Service Officer notes the treating medical practitioner has stated:

  • leg fracture (primary condition) - Temporary, with prognosis 3-12 months
  • asthma (secondary condition) - Permanent
  • spinal condition (secondary condition) - Permanent
  • Barry is unable to work 8 hours per week for the next 13 weeks

The medical certificate does not provide any information indicating Barry has capacity to participate in a suitable activity, such as jobactive.

Note: an exemption cannot be granted where the incapacity for all work is not temporary.

The Service Officer:

  • determines Barry is temporarily incapacitated for all work of at least 8 hours per week, due mainly to the leg fracture
  • finds no evidence that Barry has capacity to participate in a suitable activity
  • records all conditions, and
  • grants Barry a temporary incapacity exemption
  • requests an Employment Services Assessment (ESAt) referral for Barry, as he does not have a current and valid ESAt or JCA, and needs an assessment of partial capacity to work

Multiple medical conditions - exacerbation / recurring

Susan's medical certificate lists 2 permanent conditions - anxiety and asthma. Normally the asthma does not significantly impact Susan's capacity to work but due to a sudden change in weather, Susan is severely affected and is currently hospitalised. Susan's doctor notes the following information on the medical certificate:

  • Primary condition - Asthma - (temporary exacerbation of permanent condition), with prognosis 3-12 months
  • Secondary condition - Anxiety - Permanent, with prognosis more than 24 months
  • Susan is unable to do any work for the next 8 weeks

In the box 'In order to prepare your patient for return to work or study, certain assistance may be offered. Identify any factors which may impact on participation.' - the doctor states that Susan is likely to have difficulty participating in employment services at the moment. The medical certificate does not provide any information indicating Susan has capacity to participate in a suitable activity.

The Service Officer:

  • determines Susan is temporarily incapacitated for all work of at least 8 hours per week, due mainly to the impacts of her asthma
  • records all conditions, and
  • grants Susan a temporary incapacity exemption for 8 weeks
  • does not request an ESAt referral for Susan, because her incapacity for work appears short-term, and she already has a current ESAt which confirmed that she has a partial capacity to work of 15-22 hours per week due to her anxiety

Incapacity exemption not granted and new medical certificate lodged with different dates and exemption granted

Andrew uploads a medical certificate on 18 March 2019 stating arthritis is a permanent medical condition from 18 March 2019 to 26 April 2019.

The next day, a Service Officer reviews the medical certificate. A current and valid Employment Services Assessment (ESAt) was previously completed for the arthritis and the Assessor determined Andrew has a partial capacity to work of 15-22 hours per week.

The Service Officer does not grant the incapacity exemption as the medical certificate and ESAt evidence indicates Andrew is:

  • not suffering from an exacerbation of a permanent condition, and
  • not incapacitated for all work

The Service Officer keys the information contained in the medical certificate on 19 March 2019 as follows:

  • Date of Event: 18 March 2019 (same date as Unfit to work/study from)
  • Unfit to work/study from: 18 March 2019
  • Unfit to work/study to: 26 April 2019
  • Date of Receipt (if required): 18 March 2019
  • Non-Exemption Reasons: the Service Officer selects Job seeker is not incapacitated for all work

On 2 April 2019, Andrew lodges a new medical certificate and a Service Officer determines an incapacity exemption can be granted for a temporary back injury. The dates on the medical certificate are from 2 April 2019 to 3 May 2019. The Service Officer must not amend the details keyed for the non-granted incapacity exemption when keying the new medical certificate on 5 April 2019. The Service Officer keys the medical certificate as follows:

  • Date of Event: 2 April 2019 (same date as Unfit to work/study from)
  • Unfit to work/study from: 2 April 2019. Note: overlapping dates do not impact where an incapacity exemption has not been granted for a previous medical certificate
  • Unfit to work/study to: 3 May 2019
  • Date of Receipt (if required): 2 April 2019

Difference between 'permanent medical condition' and 'long term incapacity for work'

It is important to distinguish between a permanent medical condition and a permanent (or long term) incapacity for work. A job seeker who is permanently incapacitated for all work of 8 hours or more per week (for at least 2 years) cannot generally be granted a temporary incapacity exemption.

A permanent medical condition may, or may not, cause a permanent incapacity for work depending upon circumstances. For example, a person with well-controlled asthma may be suited to undertake full-time activities for mutual obligation requirements under normal circumstances. If they have a temporary exacerbation of their asthma, they may seek an exemption from mutual obligation requirements because of a temporary exacerbation of a permanent condition.

An exemption may be granted if it is clear from all available information that the incapacity for work is likely to be temporary. For example, if the medical certificate lists the primary condition as 'permanent' and states that the prognosis (impacts on customer's capacity for work) is expected to be 3-12 months. In this case a service officer may accept that this is a temporary exacerbation of a permanent condition.

Customers should not be asked to go back to their treating medical practitioners, to change existing opinion on whether a condition is 'permanent' or 'exacerbation of a permanent condition' or 'temporary'.

Example 1: A job seeker with an amputation has a permanent medical condition, which may affect their ability to work, according to the type of amputation. Consider whether there has been a missing limb from birth, or an amputation as the result of trauma/injury. For instance, a job seeker with an amputation below the knee may no longer be able to work as a roof-tiler but may successfully be able to work/be retrained as a call centre sales person.

Example 2: A job seeker with hearing impairment may not be able to work in an environment with a lot of background noise (for example, bar work, machinery workshop) or where good hearing is critical (such as medical reception, childcare), however may successfully work in areas such as car detailing, computer programming or clerical work with appropriate workplace support.

Example 3: The impact of a mental illness can vary widely - some people may experience only a minor impact, while for others it may have a major impact on their lives. A job seeker with depression may make a good recovery with counselling and medication, and may benefit from referral to Disability Employment Services (DES). Another job seeker with the same condition may have a long history of limited benefit from various medications and treatments, and the medical history may indicate that the job seeker is unlikely to have the capacity to work full-time within 2 years.

Contacting job seeker about inviting a Disability Support Pension (DSP) claim

Only invite a job seeker lodging a medical certificate to claim DSP if there is clear evidence that they are likely to meet all medical eligibility criteria. Valid sources of evidence include:

  • a recommendation in a Job Capacity Assessment or Employment Services Assessment
  • health and allied health professional advice from Assessment Services or the Health Professional Advisory Unit, or
  • evidence that the job seeker is manifestly eligible, such as where they are terminally ill or have a list 1 or 2 condition (see Granting Disability Support Pension (DSP) (manifest))

Do not invite a DSP claim based solely on the fact that a medical condition is permanent, or on how long the symptoms are expected to affect the job seeker's capacity to work.

If a decision is made to invite a claim for DSP:

  • make at least 2 genuine attempts to phone the job seeker
  • where contact is successful and the job seeker or nominee accepts the invitation to claim DSP, run the First Contact Concessions, Carers and Seniors (FCCCS) workflow
  • if contact is not possible, run the DSP invite to claim guided procedure
  • take care to avoid requesting unnecessary additional medical evidence (for example, if an Assessor has advised that available evidence is sufficient for a manifest grant), and further medical evidence is clearly not required

Where contact with the job seeker is made, and they indicate they do not wish to make a claim for DSP, run the DSP Invite to claim guided procedure.

The DSP Invite to claim guided procedure can be used to invite suitable JobSeeker Payment (JSP), Parenting Payment Single or Youth Allowance recipients to claim DSP.

Non-Exemption reasons

Table 4: Explanation of reasons available in the Choose Non-Exemption Reasons field.

Reason

Explanation

Job seeker/participant has capacity to participate in a suitable program (activity)

Details:

  • The job seeker/participant is able to participate in a suitable activity such as job search with an employment services provider
  • This determination is based on available medical evidence, including the medical certificate completed by the job seeker's medical practitioner

Drug and/or Alcohol dependency or misuse

Details:

  • The job seeker's incapacity is wholly or mainly caused by their drug and/or alcohol dependency or misuse
  • Medical evidence indicates the primary medical condition is drug and/or alcohol dependency or misuse. Finalise the guided procedure and do not apply the exemption due to Drug and/or Alcohol dependency or misuse, to create an activity on the Activity List (AL) screen to enable a Q549 letter to be sent to the job seeker to advise that an exemption has not been applied:
    • select the activity from the AL screen, and finalise to issue the letter
  • The Non-Applied Exemption (NAE) can also be manually recorded via the Medical Conditions (MC) screen if the guided procedure is not available

Note: NAEs can only be updated or deleted if applied in error.

Customer's incapacity for work/study/participation is not temporary

Details:

  • The job seeker's or participant's incapacity for work or study or participation is expected to continue for at least the next 2 years. Only use this reason where available medical evidence clearly shows that the job seeker is expected to be incapacitated for at least the next 2 years
  • This determination is based on available medical evidence, including the medical certificate completed by the job seeker's medical practitioner (if applicable) and an ESAt report
  • If the job seeker does not have a current and valid ESAt consider requesting an ESAt
  • Consider possible entitlement to Disability Support Pension (DSP)

Incapacity for work is not temporary

Details:

  • The job seeker's incapacity for all work is not temporary
  • Evidence shows that the customer will be incapacitated for all work of at least 8 hours per week for at least the next 2 years
  • This determination is based on available medical evidence, including the medical certificate completed by the job seeker's medical practitioner (if applicable) and/or an ESAt or JCA report

Customer is able to do work or study of at least 8 hours per week

Details:

  • The job seeker is able to work or study for 8 hours or more per week
  • This determination is based on available medical evidence, including the medical certificate completed by the job seeker's medical practitioner and a current and valid ESAt or JCA report

Insufficient evidence supplied

Details:

  • Example 1 - the job seeker has provided a non-standard medical certificate which clearly doesn't provide enough information to assess an exemption request
  • Example 2 - the service officer is not satisfied with the integrity of the information on the medical certificate, including suspected fraud
  • Example 3 - the medical certificate provided by the job seeker was issued too long ago and can no longer be considered a current and valid assessment of their medical condition
    • when deciding to apply this reason, consider if it was reasonable for the job seeker to supply their medical evidence at an earlier date. For example, if the job seeker was comatose and did not have someone to act on their behalf, it would be unreasonable for the job seeker to provide the medical certificate until after they were well enough to do so
  • If the job seeker is still seeking an exemption, advise them they need to provide a valid medical certificate from their medical practitioner
