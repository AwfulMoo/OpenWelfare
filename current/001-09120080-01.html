Exempting a job seeker from mutual obligation requirements for serious illness 001-09120080

This document outlines how to assess an exemption from mutual obligation or participation requirements for a job seeker or ParentsNext participant with a serious illness. This may apply to customers receiving JobSeeker Payment (JSP), Youth Allowance (YA), Parenting Payment (PP) or Special Benefit (SpB).

Exemption circumstances

Job seekers and participants with mutual obligation or participation requirements receiving the above payments may be eligible for a temporary exemption from their requirements in certain verifiable circumstances.

Job seekers and participants suffering from a serious illness who are undergoing or recovering from intensive medical treatment can have an exemption granted for longer than the standard maximum exemption period of 13 weeks. This exemption is known as the Temp Incap - Serious Illness exemption.

The Temp Incap - Serious Illness exemption may initially be granted for more than 13 weeks and up to 12 months, as stated on a single medical certificate. Eligible job seekers will have modified reporting requirements during the exemption. Consider this exemption each time the job seeker lodges their Reporting Statement (SU19) form.

If the same serious illness condition persists at the end of the initial 12 month exemption, consider extending the exemption for up to 6 months based on further medical evidence. The exemption period must be covered by the dates on the new medical certificate.

If a job seeker is still clearly temporarily incapacitated for all work after being granted a total period of 18 months exemption for the same medical condition, they may be granted a further exemption of up to 13 weeks at a time. This is under Temporary Incapacity exemption guidelines.

If the serious illness is primarily as a result of drug or alcohol misuse or dependency, a Temp Incap - Serious Illness exemption should not be granted unless the job seeker is a Community Development Program (CDP) participant. The job seeker can volunteer to undertake drug dependency treatments or interventions. This may count towards meeting their mutual obligation and Annual Activity Requirements if they are provider managed.

Serious illness conditions

Examples of medical conditions that may indicate a job seeker or participant is seriously ill include cancer, acquired brain injury, serious burns, organ transplants, physical injury requiring long recovery periods, or severe mental health issues.

As a result of their condition, these job seekers and participants may not be able to work, study, attend training, look for work, or participate in a suitable activity for extended periods of time. These job seekers may not qualify for Disability Support Pension (DSP) because their condition is not yet fully diagnosed, treated and stabilised, or their condition is not expected to last for more than 2 years, or they may not be residentially qualified for DSP.

If a job seeker's medical evidence verifies they have a terminal illness, they may be manifestly eligible for Disability Support Pension (DSP).

Note: this exemption should not be used as a way to reduce the number of contacts with job seekers.

Medical evidence for exemptions, including Temp Incap - Serious Illness exemption

For incapacity of a short-term or temporary nature expected to last for a period of less than 2 years, the job seeker must provide a medical certificate as proof of incapacity (preferably a Centrelink medical certificate (SU415) lodged by the job seeker or a SU683 lodged electronically by the medical practitioner).

Where the job seeker meets the eligibility criteria for the Temp-Incap - Serious Illness exemption, an initial period of exemption of up to 12 months may be granted. In all cases a single period of exemption must be based on the dates of incapacity stated by the medical practitioner on the medical certificate.

If the serious illness is noted as permanent (PRM), Service Officers must determine if a non-serious illness temporary exemption applies to other temporary conditions listed. See Assessment of a medical certificate to determine exemption from mutual obligation requirements for a temporary incapacity. If no medical conditions listed are of a temporary nature, then the incapacity exemption is not applied.

If proof of incapacity cannot be provided on an SU415 or SU683, the job seeker's medical practitioner may provide sufficient evidence to grant an exemption in another format, as long as it provides all the same information as an SU415/SU683. People from rural or remote areas may have limited access to doctors, medical evidence can be completed by a community nurse.

A medical certificate may be verified if there is doubt about its accuracy or authenticity. If doubt exists, the issuer should be contacted by telephone to confirm the authenticity of the certificate and/or information about altered details.

If the medical practitioner advises they never issued the certificate or the information is different, discuss with immediate supervisor and complete a Suspected Fraud referral if appropriate.

Process the medical certificate as per normal procedures. Do not document specific details of the allegation(s) on the job seeker's record.

Escalate to Level 2 Helpdesk if further assistance is required.

Exacerbation of a permanent condition

An exacerbation of a permanent condition is considered to be temporary if it is likely that the job seeker will be able to return to work within the next 2 years.

If medical evidence shows the illness or injury is expected to affect the job seeker's ability to undertake work or study of at least 8 hours per week for an extended period (more than 13 weeks and less than 24 months), they may meet the eligibility criteria for granting the Temp Incap - Serious Illness exemption. In all cases a single period of exemption must be based on the dates of incapacity stated by the medical practitioner on the medical certificate.

Recurring medical conditions

A recurring medical condition is one that will come and go with periods of wellness or remission of the symptoms, such as asthma or multiple sclerosis.

If medical evidence shows the illness or injury is expected to affect the job seeker's ability to undertake work or study of at least 8 hours per week for an extended period (more than 13 weeks and less than 24 months), they may meet the eligibility criteria for granting the Temp Incap - Serious Illness exemption. In all cases a single period of exemption must be based on the dates of incapacity stated by the medical practitioner on the medical certificate.

Job seekers - ESAt and Temp Incap - Serious Illness exemptions

Employment Services Assessment (ESAt) are out of scope for ParentsNext participants but may be relevant for job seekers.

In all cases the Service Officer should make the decision about whether to grant an exemption, including Temp incap - Serious Illness, based on available evidence, including the medical certificate completed by the job seeker's medical practitioner.

Job seekers with a serious illness who clearly meet the criteria for the Temp Incap - Serious Illness exemption do not need to be referred for an Employment Services Assessment (ESAt) to determine whether an exemption can be granted.

If the Job Seeker Classification Instrument (JSCI) identifies the requirement for an ESAt for a job seeker who meets the criteria for the Temp Incap - Serious Illness exemption, the JCA flag may be withdrawn so the pending JSCI can be completed without referring the job seeker for an ESAt.

A referral for an ESAt can still be made to determine if a job seeker has a Partial Capacity to Work or to identify if another payment is more appropriate - see Assessment of a medical certificate to determine exemption from mutual obligation requirements for a temporary incapacity.

Current and valid assessment for job seekers

An exemption cannot be granted solely on the information in a Job Capacity Assessment (JCA) or an ESAt report. The job seeker must still meet all the eligibility criteria of the Temp Incap - Serious Illness exemption by providing a medical certificate completed by a registered medical practitioner indicating dates which reflect the full period of the exemption (preferably a Centrelink medical certificate (SU415) lodged by the job seeker or an SU683 lodged electronically by the medical practitioner.

If the job seeker has a current and valid ESAt or JCA the information in the report should also be taken into account when making a decision to grant an exemption. When considering the information in an ESAt report always ensure it is a current and valid assessment of the customer's work capacity. For example, the condition is clearly indicated on the medical certificate? in the current ESAt or JCA? Has the condition deteriorated/improved since the last assessment? Has the job seeker's work capacity changed since the last assessment?

Where a JCA or an ESAt with a current and valid assessment of the customer's work capacity exists for the medical condition, ensure all current medical evidence and circumstances surrounding the condition are taken into consideration when determining the exemption period.

Reporting requirements and Temp Incap - Serious Illness exemptions

Job seekers

Job seekers who have been granted the Temp Incap Serious Illness exemption can be manually placed on 4 weekly variable reporting and are required to lodge a modified Reporting Statement (SU19) for up to a maximum of 12 weeks.

ParentsNext

Compulsory ParentsNext participants who have been granted the Temp Incap Serious illness exemption for a period of 52 weeks are automatically placed on 12 weekly variable reporting and are required to lodge a modified Reporting Statement (SU19). Reporting requirements cannot be manually updated for ParentsNext participants.

Modified SU19

Where a customer has been placed on variable reporting due to a Temp Incap Serious Illness exemption, the SU19 is modified to include questions about their medical condition. The exemption will be reconsidered if the customer states on the SU19 their condition has significantly:

  • improved and they can now work or participate in activities for at least 8 hours per week. They should be contacted and the Temp Incap - Serious Illness exemption reconsidered. These exemptions should not be end-dated without discussion with the customer to confirm that the exemption is no longer appropriate
  • deteriorated and they are unlikely to recover within the next 2 years. This indicates they should be considered for another payment such as Disability Support Pension (DSP)

Appointing a nominee or authorising a person to act on behalf of a seriously ill job seeker or ParentsNext participant

Job seekers or participants who have been granted the Temp Incap - Serious Illness exemption may be unable to conduct business on their own behalf for extended periods.

In order for them to maintain contact with or respond to contact from the agency during a period of incapacity, job seekers and participants can request the appointment of a third party as:

  • a correspondence nominee
  • a payment nominee
  • both a correspondence and payment nominee

When a job seeker or participant identifies that their medical circumstances may prevent them from conducting their business with the agency, advise the job seeker or participant of the facility to appoint a nominee.
