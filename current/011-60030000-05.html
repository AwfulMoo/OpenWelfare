Prescription Shopping Program: Escalation Template

FAQs from prescribers about the PSP

Table 1

Item

Description

1

Question: What is this letter?

Answer: The PSP Alert Service sends a letter to prescribers containing information about identified patients who have met the Prescription Shopping Programme criteria. The letter is sent monthly and is system generated. It is designed to assist you with medication management.

2

Question: Why did I get this letter?

Answer: Letters are automatically generated for certain patients who meet the PSP criteria and assessed as high risk.. The letter you received is for information purposes only. It is designed to assist you with medication management and does not prevent you from prescribing PBS medicine to the patient.

3

Question: What is the PSP criteria?

Answer: The individual met 1 or more of 3 PSP criteria in a period of 3 consecutive months:

  • They have been supplied PBS items prescribed by 6 or more different prescribers
  • They have been supplied a total of 25 or more PBS target items (a subset of medicines)
  • They have been supplied a total of 50 or more PBS items (including non-target items)

The information used by the PSP is collected from PBS dispensing data provided to the agency by Pharmacists.

4

Question: What should I do with this letter?

Answer: The information provided is designed to assist the prescriber in medication management. You may choose to retain the letter on the patient’s file or destroy the letter if applicable.

5

Question: Why was this letter sent to my home address?

Answer: You have received this letter to your home address based on the information you provided to the agency. If you would like to update your preferred address, you can do this via health professional’s online services (HPOS). If you do not have a HPOS account please contact Medicare provider enquiries on 132 150, select option 2 and ask to update your preferred mailing address.

For information on how to update addresses via HPOS, go to Update your Personal details through HPOS on the Services Australia website.

6

Question: Can I speak with someone about this letter?

Answer: I will escalate your call to the PBS national pharmaceutical advisor line for further assistance.

7

Question: Why is there no telephone number on the letter?

Answer: The letter is provided for information purposes only to assist you with medication management.

8

Question: Are there any changes to the PSIS?

Answer: No. The PSIS is still available for you to ring and seek information on your patients should you suspect them of obtaining medicines in excess of medical need.

The letters sent are automated and an information service only. Detailed and itemised reports may continue to be requested via appropriate PSP contact channels. You are welcome to request a report, which will show current information for a patient if they are currently identified. This will be sent to you in due course.

9

Question: What if the prescriber does not believe the patient should be on the PSP?

Answer: The letters sent are automated and an information service only. Detailed and itemised reports may continue to be requested via appropriate PSIS contact channels. You are welcome to request a report, which will show current information for a patient.

The letter you received is for information purposes only. It is designed to assist you with medication management.

If you would like to discuss the information in the letter, I will escalate your call to the PBS national pharmaceutical advisor line for further assistance.

10

Question: What if the prescriber states they have never seen the patient?

Answer: The letter is sent to the practice location recorded with the agency. If you practise from a number of locations, you may have seen the patient at another practice location.

If the prescriber believes the agency has received incorrect data, escalate to the PBS national pharmaceutical advisor line.

11

Question: Can the prescriber provide the patient with a copy of the letter?

Answer: this is at the prescriber’s discretion. The patient is not provided with a copy of the letter directly from the agency as it is designed to assist you with medication management.

It is recommended that careful consideration be given before releasing this information to your patients. Some patients may become distressed.

12

Question: Can a patient see this on their MyHealth / MyGov record?

The patient’s PBS prescription information is available on their MyHealth record however, there is no indication or flag to highlight to them that they have met the PSP criteria.

13

Question: Why does the letter say that it may not be accurate or up to date?

Answer: Letters do not necessarily contain a full list of target medicines received by the patient or the patient may have been prescribed other medicines since the letter was created.

For more up to date information about the patient, the prescriber should call the PSIS.

14

Question: Can I call PSIS to report a patient I suspect is prescription shopping?

Answer: No, PSIS is an information service only. If you are concerned about your patient, you can contact the health department in your state or territory about statutory reporting requirements or other programs to help you manage your patient.

FAQs from patients about the PSP

Table 2

Item

Description

1

Question: My doctor has spoken to me about being identified under the PSP, however I do not believe I should be on the PSP.

What does this mean?

Answer: This letter is for your doctor’s information only. The agency does not provide advice on any clinical matters or care provided to you by your doctor.

The information used by the PSP is collected from prescribing and dispensing PBS data provided to the agency by Pharmacists. A record of this is can be sent to your doctor for information purposes. This helps assist your doctor’s decision-making when prescribing medications.

2

Question: My doctor has told me that I am on the PSP - why have I been flagged as having met the PSP criteria?

The information used by the PSP is collected from prescribing and dispensing PBS data provided to the agency by Pharmacists.

You must have met at least 1 PSP criteria in a period of 3 consecutive months:

  • you have been supplied PBS items prescribed by 6 or more different prescribers
  • you have been supplied a total of 25 or more PBS target items (a subset of medicines)
  • you have been supplied a total of 50 or more PBS items (including non-target items)

3

Question: My doctor mentioned I have been identified under the PSP - I do not agree with this and would like to appeal. What do I do?

Answer: we are unable to change these records at the request of a patient or their prescriber.

If you believe your information has been recorded incorrectly as a result of fraudulent activity such as, your Medicare card being stolen and misused, you can report it to the fraud team.

You can request a new Medicare card through MyGov, the general enquiries line, or at a service centre.

If you disagree with the PSP program policy, you can write to the Department of Health and Aged Care and Aged Care or contact a local member to raise your concerns.

4

Question: My doctor will no longer prescribe medications to me - what do I do?

The Prescription Shopping Programme is an information service only. The information provided, enables your doctor to make an informed decision during the consultation.

The agency does not provide advice on any clinical matters or care provided to you by your doctor. It is up to your doctor to determine whether this impacts on further treatment.

5

Question: My doctor received a PSP letter about me - will I receive this letter too?

Answer: Patients who meet the PSP criteria are not sent a copy of this letter. It is sent to prescribers only as an information service to assist in their decisions when prescribing medicines.

6

Question: If caller threatens self-harm and/or suicide at any point during call.

Remain calm, listen to the caller and take notes. Involve your team leader or another support person to assist you with the call if required.

Scenarios and actions required for callers talking about suicide or self-harm

Table 3

Step

Action

1

The caller discusses self-harm and/or suicide at any point during the call

It is important to convey a sense of calm and control, without appearing to be commanding or authoritarian. For this reason, you should not tell people to do things such as ‘calm down’ as this can be viewed as dismissive of their situation and feelings, as well as authoritarian.

Statements of suicide and self-harm are to be taken seriously and are often confronting.

Key points to remember are:

  • Remain calm and listen carefully to what the caller is saying and do not dismiss any information that they are sharing with you
  • Tell the caller that you are concerned with what they are saying and want to ensure that we provide support to them
  • Ask the caller if they are having thoughts of ending their life or harming themselves or others. If the caller confirms this, go to Step 3

See also Customers talking about suicide or self-harm.

2

The caller is in need of Social Worker assistance but your concern is not for their immediate safety

See general referral process in Social work service referral.

3

A caller talks about suicide or harming themselves or others and you are concerned for their immediate safety

Inform the caller that you will get assistance for them from a Services Australia Social Worker.

Refer the caller to a social worker:

You should transfer the customer to the Social Work Line (Services Australia Workspace social worker - high risk). Go to Step 5.

If the call is outside of normal business hours, or if the call cannot be transferred or the call occurs outside of business hours, go to Step 4.

4

Consider transferring to triple zero (000)

Ask the caller for consent to contact police or ambulance via triple zero (000).

Does the caller consent?

  • Yes, ask them for:
    • Their location
    • Phone number, and
    • Their consent to share this information with police or ambulance. When transferring the call to police or ambulance, give the caller’s location and phone number
  • No, go to Step 5

5

Escalate to Team Leader/Manager

Notify a Team Leader/Manager of any critical incident involving suicide or self-harm. Contact a Team Leader or Manager immediately to consider a release of information if:

  • a caller terminates the call before they can be transferred to a social worker
  • a caller declines a referral to police or ambulance or other support services (or withholds consent to release their location and phone number)

6

De-briefing with a Team Leader

It is important to recognise talking about suicide and self-harm can be distressing and it is normal to have a physical or emotional response.

Staff that have been affected by a difficult situation should de-brief with a Team Leader or Manager who can provide support. Staff should also have access to the national Employee Assistance Program (EAP) provider Benestar.

See also About Customer aggression - prevention and management and Reporting, recording and escalating incidents of customer aggression.
