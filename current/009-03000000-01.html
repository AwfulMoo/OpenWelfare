Carer Allowance (CA) 009-03000000

  • Background
Expand all


This document outlines Carer Allowance (CA) including eligibility, claiming and changes of circumstances.

Government intent

Carers are an integral part of Australia's health system and are the foundation of our aged, disability and community care systems. All carers and care receivers, regardless of their cultural and linguistic differences, age, disability, religion, socioeconomic status, gender identification and geographical location should have the same rights, choices and opportunities as other Australians. They should be able to enjoy optimum health, social and economic wellbeing and participate in family, social and community life, employment and education.

CA is an income supplement for people who provide additional daily care and attention for an adult or child with a disability or medical condition, or an adult who is frail aged. CA is not taxable or assets tested. To be eligible for CA, the carer’s and their partner’s combined Adjusted Taxable Income (ATI) must be under $250,000 a year. This includes deemed amount from account-based income streams if the account holder is 60 years of age or older. The same income limit applies if the carer does not have a partner.

The carer's and their partner's income does not affect the rate of CA that is paid.

CA can be paid in addition to wages or another income support payment. CA recognises the impact of a person's disability on the carer.

Claiming CA

If a carer contacts with an intention to claim CA, the following service offer must be delivered.

First offer an online claim. Explain the benefits of claiming online:

  • Help to apply for the right payment through streaming questions and early warnings if they may not be eligible
  • Less information for them to provide as the online claim will prefill details known to Services Australia
  • They can monitor the progress of their claim through their online account or the Express Plus Centrelink mobile app

If they decline an online claim, run the Assisted Customer Claim (ACC) with the carer. After completing the ACC advise the carer of any documents they still need to provide, or action they need to take to submit their claim

Assessing claims for CA

Claims for CA (child)

Claims are assessed under the Disability Care Load Assessment Determination (DCLAD) (2010), unless the child has a terminal condition or a Recognised Disability from the List of Recognised Disabilities (LoRD).This list contains disabilities or severe medical conditions that have been assessed as constantly severe enough to consistently require a sufficient level of care to allow qualification for CA.

The Treating Health Professional (THP) must state the condition is either permanent or temporary for at least 12 months and the child must still receive care and attention from the carer on a daily basis in a residence that is the home of both the carer and care receiver.

Claims for CA (adult)

Claims are assessed under the Adult Disability Assessment Tool (ADAT). The ADAT measures the functional ability and care needs of the care receiver.

The THP must state the condition is either permanent or temporary for a least 12 months and the care receiver receives care on a daily basis from the carer in a private home that is the residence of the carer and the care receiver. If the home is not shared by the carer and care receiver, but is still a private residence, the carer may still qualify for payment under section 954A of the Social Security Act 1991.

CA income test

To be eligible for CA, the carer’s and their partner’s combined Adjusted Taxable Income (ATI) must be under $250,000 a year. This includes deemed amount from account-based income streams if the account holder is 60 years of age or older. The same income limit applies if the carer does not have a partner.

The income test does not apply if the carer is exempt from the CA income test.

Income details are provided at the CA new claim stage and income may be reviewed following data matching with the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) or a change of circumstances that may affect their entitlement under the CA income test.

CA care receivers turning 16 years of age

The Carers Child to Adult Transfer (CTAT) is the process of transitioning eligible carers from child care receiver provisions to adult care receiver provisions when the care receiver turns 16 years of age.

It is not a new claim process. It is a quicker and easier process for the carer requiring less documentation than a new claim.

Eligible carers are sent a CTAT invitation letter when their care receiver turns 15 years and 9 months advising them to request a transfer by the due date if they want their entitlement to continuation of payment to be assessed under adult provisions. If the carer fails to complete the CTAT prior to the due date, their CA will be cancelled and a new claim will be required.

Carers who get their Centrelink letters in their myGov inbox will get the invitation letter and a link to the CTAT online service. Customers will also be able to access the service from their Centrelink online account. Paper CTAT forms are not included with the invitation letter but are available on the Services Australia website:

  • Child to adult transfer for Carer Payment and/or Carer Allowance (SA432)
  • Carer Payment and/or Carer Allowance Medical Report - For a person - 16 years or over (SA332A)

Carers whose CA is suspended or cancelled will need to contact to discuss their entitlement status. If the carer’s CP is suspended or cancelled at the time their child care receiver reaches the required age milestone of 15 years and 9 months, the carer will not be eligible to receive the CTAT invitation letter. The invitation letter will advise carers that their payment(s) under child provisions is stopping and to request a transfer assessment under adult provisions.

When a carer contacts about the CTAT, explain that the suspension/cancellation must be resolved before assessing their eligibility to transfer. If their CA child entitlement cannot be re-established, the carer will need to make a new claim.

Change of circumstances for CA

CA carers are obliged to advise the agency of any event or change in circumstances that could affect their payment. This includes contact details, and changes to accommodation or relationship. It also includes changes in care arrangements and cessation of care.

CA carers are also required to notify changes that may affect their eligibility under the CA income test. These include if their and partner’s Adjusted Taxable Income (ATI) for the previous financial year, including any deemed income from account-based income streams, is $250,000 or more. CA carers must also notify when they or their partner receive an account-based income stream and the account holder is 60 years of age or older.

Death of a carer

There is a need to check whether the carer was receiving Carer Payment (CP) and/or CA. A decision needs to be made about whether the deceased estate is entitled to a bereavement payment.

If the care receiver has also died, an assessment is also required on whether the deceased person was entitled to a bereavement payment (or would have been if they had not themselves died).

See Death of a carer (CLK).

Death of a care receiver

Before any action is taken, verification of death must be provided. Once verified, it is important that the death be recorded as soon as possible to ensure that carers receive their correct entitlement.

Qualification for bereavement assistance depends on whether the carer receives Carer Payment (CP) and/or CA and on the circumstances of the carer/care receiver before the death.

Overseas absences

When a carer and/or care receiver advises of an overseas absence, it may be necessary to code portability details as well as coding the respite absence.

There are separate procedures concerning respite absences, and on portability for Carer Payment (CP) and/or Carer Allowance (CA).

For help on when to code portability and/or absence details, see CA and CP interaction of portability and temporary cessation of care (respite) rules.

CA cancellations, suspensions and restorations

CA can be suspended and/or cancelled when a carer is no longer entitled to the payment as a result of changes in their or their care receiver's circumstances, or if they have failed to comply with the requirements for payment.

Before a carer's payment is suspended and/or cancelled, it must be established that the carer is not eligible for payment. If the decision is to suspend and/or cancel payment, the Service Officer must act swiftly to advise the carer of the reason and their review and appeal rights, to avoid placing them in financial hardship.

When restoring CA, it is important to re-establish the carer's eligibility and to restart payment from the date they became eligible again. If there has been a change in circumstances, update relevant details on the system before restoring payments to avoid incorrect arrears being paid.
