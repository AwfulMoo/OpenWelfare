Phone and written approvals

Table 1

Item

Description

1

Phone approvals

Service Officers should:

  • Check if the prescriber is requesting a General (S85) schedule opioid or a palliative care schedule opioid? The item selected will determine the restriction criteria/Q&As that the prescriber will need to address
  • read out the question exactly as is in Online PBS Authorities (OPA) system
  • select the relevant option based on response provided by prescriber
  • stop, pause and continue where prescribers:
    • do not let staff finish with the Q&As, or
    • provide information inconsistent with the answers required

2

Written approvals

From 1 June 2021, a new General (S85) Schedule - Opioid Authority Application Form (PB294) for general schedule items is available. Applications received beyond this date with the old application form may be used provided there is enough information to correctly answer the questions. A grace period of 3 months (until end August 2021) will be allowed for prescribers who continue to use the previous version. These applications are to be returned to the prescriber along with a letter and a copy of the new form after this time period.

Prescribers must provide sufficient information, as required to respond to Q&As in Online PBS Authorities (OPA) system to complete an assessment.

If the prescriber has not stated initial or continuing, staff may approve under initial if all other information is available.

Service Officers can accept summarised information on written prescriptions as prescribers have limited space for the PBS criteria.

Two application forms are available to assist prescribers when submitting a written application for an opioid item. The form the prescriber uses will determine which type of item Service Officers need to select.

Where the prescriber submits the application using the General (S85) Schedule - Opioid Authority Application Form (PB294); Services Officers must select an item from the general PBS Schedule.

Where the prescriber submits the application using the Palliative Care Schedule – Opioid Authority Application Form (PB306); Service Officers must select an item from the Palliative Care Schedule.

If the prescriber does not provide sufficient information to satisfy the criteria according to the form that was submitted, Service Officers must reject the application and return it to the prescriber with the relevant letter and with compliments slip.

Frequently asked questions

Changes from 1 June 2021

Table 2

1

Question: What changes have been made to the palliative care schedule from 1 June 2021?

Answer: some opioids have been added to the palliative care schedule for both short and long-term use. They are still available on the general schedule for palliative patients, however eligibility requirements are simplified for palliative care patients via the palliative care schedule.

2

Question: Is there a limit on the maximum quantity of palliative care opioids that Services Australia can approve for an authority prescription?

Answer: If the restrictions do not limit the maximum quantity, a quantity of up to 1 month treatment can be approved, with sufficient repeats for up to 3 months treatment (via self-serve OPA, or written, either by post or HPOS form upload only) if required. The previous 240 limit does not apply.

Note: Online PBS Authorities (OPA) system may reject these valid requests due to the multipliers. See Multipliers (rejection reason codes 075 and 076) below.

3

Question: Can the prescriber apply for an authority prescription for up to 3 months' supply on OPA (self-serve) or Form Upload?

Answer: A prescriber can apply via either channel for up to 3 months' supply. Some requests may reject on OPA due to quantity and/or repeat (multiplier) limitations. If a prescriber contact Services Australia to query a rejection and the request meets all other requirements, amend the authority request to override the rejection in OPA with an authority comment “Q/R within guidelines".

4

Question: Can the prescriber apply for an authority on the existing form (PB294) for opioid authorities for items on the palliative care schedule?

Answer: No.

Note: ‘palliative care’ is still part of the restriction for some general schedule opioid items, and therefore it is still part of the existing form. SOs should not reject applications simply because this option has been ticked, as it is may still a valid application. If the prescriber has provided all relevant information to process this application, SOs should approve this under a general schedule item.

5

Question: Can service officers approve a palliative care opioid when the prescriber submits the other form

Answer: Service officers must approve items based on which form the prescriber submits. The onus is on the prescriber to make the decision about which criteria their patient meets, and to use the corresponding application form.

Changes from 1 October 2020

Table 3

Item

Description

1

Question: What changes have been made to opioid restrictions from 1 October 2020?

Answer: Services Australia no longer requires the details of the:

  • medical practitioner who undertook the annual pain management review, and
  • date of the review.

The Department of Health amended restrictions to include treatment phases. These treatment phases include:

  • Initial PBS treatment after 1 June 2020 where patient has been treated with opioids for less than 12 months
  • Initial PBS treatment after 1 June 2020 where patient has been treated with opioids for more than 12 months
  • Continuing PBS treatment after 1 June 2020

If the patient has received authority approval for the same form and drug between 1 June and 30 September 2020, Service Officers need to request authority approval under continuing treatment and confirm patient’s eligibility.

Palliative care nurse practitioners can now conduct the annual pain management review for palliative care patients. This is to reduce disruptions to the prescribing of required opioid medications.

The requirements for an annual pain management review have been removed for palliative care patients whose clinical condition is such that a review is not possible.

2

Question: When was information about the latest opioid changes provided to prescribers?

Answer: The Department of Health and Services Australia provided information to the College of General Practitioners and the Australian Medical Association (AMA), as well as other peak bodies in May regarding the initial changes. Further information was provided in September for the most recent changes. In addition to this, the information is online at pbs.gov.au under ‘News’.

3

Question: Is there a limit on the maximum quantity of opioids that Services Australia can approve for an authority prescription?

Answer: If the restrictions do not limit the maximum quantity, a quantity of up to 1 month treatment can be approved, with sufficient repeats for up to 3 months treatment (via self-serve, OPA and written, either by post or HPOS form upload only) if required. The previous 240 limit does not apply.

Note: Online PBS Authorities (OPA) system may reject these valid requests due to the multipliers. See Multipliers (rejection reason codes 075 and 076) below.

4

Question: Can the prescriber apply for an authority prescription for up to 3 months' supply on OPA (self-serve) or Form Upload?

Answer: A prescriber can apply via either channel for up to 3 months' supply. Some requests may reject on OPA due to quantity and/or repeat (multiplier) limitations. If a prescriber contacts Services Australia to query a rejection and the request meets all other requirements, amend the authority request to override the rejection in OPA with an authority comment “Q/R within guidelines".

5

Question: Can Service Officers (SOs) approve more than 2 repeats depending on the daily dose?

Answer: If the restrictions allow increase in repeats, Service Officers can approve. As long as the total approved quantity + repeats is not more than 1 month’s supply via phone, or 3 months’ supply (via self-serve OPA and written, either by post or HPOS form upload only)

6

Question: There are multiple listings of Panadeine Forte® on Mainframe/OPA, which one do I select?

Answer: Select item code 1215Y.

7

Question: Can Service Officers approve if a doctor requests a quantity that would require a pack to be broken?

Answer: Yes, as long as the quantity does not exceed 1 month max supply.

Multipliers (rejection reason codes 075 and 076)

Table 4

Item

Description

1

Question: What do Service Officers do if a doctor calls querying opioid authority rejections by OPA due to multipliers?

Answer: Advise prescribers that the system will assess within regular dosage requirements, and therefore may reject due to quantity and/or repeat (multiplier) limitations. If the request meets all other requirements, Service Officers can approve the authority by overriding the rejection.

PBS restrictions requirements

Table 5

Item

Description

1

Question: If a prescriber applies for an authority for increased quantities and/or repeats (written or phone) but does not provide a reason that is in line with the restrictions will a Service Officer be able to make a decision to approve the authority?

Answer: The prescriber must provide sufficient information for staff to answer the Q&As.

2

Question: If the prescriber has not stated “initial PBS treatment after 1 June 2020” can Services Australia approve if all other information is available?

Answer: Yes. Services Australia may approve this under initial (less than 12 months or more than 12 months depending on the treatment duration stated by the prescriber).

For all other missing information, reject and send back with the Opioid With Compliments slip and the Opioids Authority Application form (along with the rejection letter).

3

Question: If a prescriber has not stated “continuing PBS treatment for this drug and form after 1 June 2020”, can Services Australia approve if all other information is available?

Answer: Yes. Services Australia may approve this under initial (less than 12 months or more than 12 months depending on the treatment duration stated by the prescriber).

For all other missing information, reject and send back with the Opioid With Compliments slip and the Opioids Authority Application form (along with the rejection letter).

4

Question: If the prescriber has stated “initial”, will OPA reject if there has been a previous approval for initial therapy?

The system will not reject. Service Officers may approve according to what the prescriber advises.

5

Question: If a prescriber asks “What is opioid-naïve?”, is a Service Officer able to provide a definition?

Answer: Service Officers may provide some guidance regarding this question. Opioid naïve refers to a patient’s previous exposure to opioids. For the purposes of PBS approval, a patient is opioid naïve if they have not had recent exposure to opioids.

6

Question: Does the prescriber have to state "daily, continuous, long term" in full?

Answer: Yes - "daily, continuous, long term" must be stated for the “initial treatment” restrictions.

This applies to opioid patches e.g. Norspan® and fentanyl. These patches are not applied daily, however, release the drug daily and continuously. If the prescriber did not state this or answered "no" to the question - reject.

7

Question: If the prescriber states that 2 of the indications apply to their patient, how does a Service Officer record that answer into OPA?

Answer: Ask the prescriber which of the 2 indications best suits their patient for this approval and record that one in OPA.

Advise that Services Australia can only approve under 1 indication.

Duration of treatment

Table 6

Item

Description

1

Question: When processing a phone request for opioids, the prescriber has stated it is less than 12 months. However, if it is obvious that it is greater than 12 months, what do Services Officers do?

Answer: Service Officers should not check patient history and must accept the information the prescriber has provided.

2

Question: If a prescriber does not know the length of opioid treatment for their patient, can a Service Officer check and provide the information?

Answer: No, the prescriber must state the length of opioid treatment. PBS restrictions refer to the use of both PBS and non-PBS opioids and Services Australia does not have visibility of non PBS opioid treatment. The prescriber needs to determine this information from the patient or the patient's previous prescriber.

3

Question: If a patient has never had an authority for opioid treatment, is their treatment duration defined as “less than 12 months"?

Answer: No, Services Australia need to take into account the total duration of opioid treatment. This may include non-PBS treatment, PBS treatment with listed amounts and PBS treatment supplied under an authority for increased quantities and repeats. Medicines such as tramadol and paracetamol+codeine combinations are considered opioid treatment and must be considered by the prescriber.

4

Question: If a prescriber states a patient is on long term methadone, is their opioid treatment duration defined as more than 12 months?

Answer: Methadone is not always prescribed to treat pain. If a patient is using methadone to treat pain, the period of treatment for pain management is all that should be taken into consideration when looking at the patient’s duration of opioid treatment.

Review of patient opioid treatment for pain management

Table 7

Item

Description

1

Question: Is an opioid or narcotic review the same as a pain management review or a clinical review?

Answer: Yes, if it is a review of the patient’s opioid treatment for their pain management.

2

Question: Do patients have to attend a face-to-face appointment for their opioid treatment review during COVID-19?

Answer: No, a telehealth consult is acceptable.

3

Question: Which health professionals can conduct the opioid review?

Answer: An Ahpra registered medical practitioner (doctor) must conduct the review. For palliative care patients only, a palliative care nurse practitioner can conduct the review.

4

Question: If a patient has not had an opioid review in the last 12 months, can Services Australia approve an application for increased quantities and/or repeats for opioids?

Answer: If a patient has been on opioid treatment for less than 12 months, a review is not required.

A review is only required if the patient has been receiving PBS and non-PBS opioid pain treatment for longer than 12 months.

In certain circumstances an authority for increased quantity and repeats can be approved:

  • If a patient has proven malignant neoplasia (note: not all opioids have this option)
  • If a review is planned in the next 3 months, but ONLY in cases where the patient had exceeded 12 months treatment prior to 1 June 2020
  • If the patient is a palliative care patient and they cannot attend a review due to their condition

The authority application must not be approved if:

  • the patient’s opioid treatment duration has exceeded 12 months
  • they have not had a review in the last 12 months, and
  • a review is not planned in the next 3 months

The prescriber may write a PBS prescription for the listed quantity.

5

Question: Where a review is required, does the prescriber need to provide the review details for every authority request?

Answer: From 1 October 2020, review details are no longer required to be provided to Services Australia, however, the prescriber must keep the details of the review on the patient’s file, and reviews must still be conducted once every 12 months.

6

Question: Does an oncologist or other specialist consult count as a review?

Answer: If the specialist assessed the patient's pain management during the consult, Services Australia considers this a review.

7

Question: Are aged-care patients (e.g. nursing home patients) exempt from an opioid (narcotic) review?

Answer: No.

8

Question: If a prescriber writes a STREAMLINED authority for listed quantity 28, is a review of opioid pain management required within the next 3 months if one has not been done in the last 12 months?

Answer: Streamlined authority items for listed quantities do not require an opioid (narcotic) review.

9

Question: Can the prescriber calling for the authority request be the reviewing prescriber?

Answer: Yes, as long as they are not the patient's regular prescriber.

10

Question: If a prescriber has taken over care of a patient and will be their regular doctor in the future, can they be the reviewing prescriber?

Answer: Yes. After 12 months, a different prescriber will need to do a new opioid (narcotic) review.

11

Question: What are the options for GP working by themselves in a solo practice who is not able to refer their patient to any other GP for an opioid (narcotic) review?

Answer: Services Australia cannot approve an authority application for increased quantity. The prescriber may write a PBS prescription for the list quantity, noting that reviews can be done via telehealth and this may be an option for the patient.

12

Question: How should prescribers conduct opioid pain management reviews?

Answer: The prescriber must tell Services Australia at the time of the authority application if an opioid (narcotic) review has been conducted for their patient. Services Australia does not question or record how the prescriber did it.

13

Question: If a prescriber is requesting an authority approval for increased quantity and repeats for tramadol which did not require a review prior to 1 June but patient has been on it for more than 10 years, which criteria do Service Officers select?

Answer: The prescriber must make the decision as to which criteria applies to their patient. They could possibly fit into the restriction for where they are planning a review within the next 3 months. The restrictions are available on https://www.pbs.gov.au/pbs/home.

Authority Required (STREAMLINED) issues

Table 8

Item

Description

1

Question: Can authority requests for Authority Required (STREAMLINED) opioids for the listed quantities and repeats be processed by Service Officers?

Answer: The prescriber must do all streamlined opioid requests that are for listed quantity and repeats. The prescriber must decide if their patient meets the streamlined criteria.

2

Question: If the prescriber's software has not been updated with the opioid changes, can they still write valid prescriptions for opioid treatment?

Answer: The prescriber may handwrite the script on an Authority prescription pad. They should also contact their software provider urgently to request an update.

Dispensing issues

Table 9

Item

Description

1

Question: Do prescribers need an authority for Endone quantity 20?

Answer: No, it is a Restricted Benefit for list quantity 20. Pharmacists must select the correct item (quantity 20 listing) when dispensing. If they select the quantity 10 listing, their software will say an authority is required.

2

Question: If a patient has received an authority approval for increased quantities and repeats between 1 June and 30 September, should a prescriber request an initial or continuing authority prescription?

Answer: If the patient has received authority approval for the same drug and form between 1 June and 30 September 2020, prescribers should request authority approval under continuing treatment. However, Service Officers are not required to check patient history to ensure an authority approval for the same drug and form was approved. If the prescriber has requested it under the initial restriction, Service Officers may approve under initial as long as all requirements have been addressed.

3

Question: Can Service Officers process a written authority request with date of prescribing from 1 October that contains pre-1 October restriction criteria?

Answer: If the prescriber has not stated “initial or continuing PBS treatment after 1 June 2020” on a written prescription but all other information is available. Service Officers may approve this either as:

  • Initial less than 12 months if the prescriber stated less than 12 months
  • Initial more than 12 months if the prescriber stated more than 12 months or provided pain management review details

For all other missing information, reject and send back with the Opioid With Compliments slip, rejection letter and the Opioids Authority Application form applicable to the items requested.
