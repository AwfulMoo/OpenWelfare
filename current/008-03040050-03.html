This page contains the process for applying the Impairment Tables introduced on 1 January 2012 and how to assign ratings.

Applying the Impairment Tables and assigning ratings

Step

Action

1

Determine whether the Impairment Tables should be applied + Read more ...

To determine whether the Impairment Tables should be applied, first consider the following question:

Does the customer have a permanent medical condition?

  • Yes, go to Step 2
  • No, do not apply the Impairment Tables. Procedure ends here

Note: information including self-reported symptoms without corroborating evidence and non-medical factors must not be taken into account when applying the tables.

2

Determine if the medical condition is fully diagnosed, treated and stabilised + Read more ...

Is the medical condition fully diagnosed, treated and stabilised and more likely than not, in light of available evidence, to persist for more than 2 years?

  • Yes, go to Step 4
  • No, go to Step 3

3

Identify medical or other compelling/acceptable reasons not to proceed with treatment + Read more ...

Consider if there is medical or other compelling and acceptable reasons for not proceeding with reasonable treatment, including the following:

  • The person has religious or cultural beliefs prohibiting treatment (for example blood transfusions)
  • The person lacks insight or the ability to make appropriate judgements due to their medical condition and is unlikely to comply with treatment (for example a person with a severe psychotic illness or dementia), or
  • There is an extended waiting list for treatment

The person's views (the subjective test) and all available information on treatment options, risks etc. (the objective test) must be considered by the Assessor/Government contracted Doctors (GCD) in such situations.

Are there medical or other compelling and acceptable reasons for not proceeding with reasonable treatment?

  • Yes, go to Step 4
  • No, procedure ends here

4

Select the relevant Table(s) + Read more ...

Table selection depends on the function affected:

  • identify the function affected/loss of function
  • refer to the appropriate Table related to the area of function
  • identify the correct rating

Determine how many impairments or conditions need to be rated

If the customer has:

  • a single impairment, from a single condition, or multiple impairments from multiple conditions, each impairment is to be rated on individual Table(s)
  • multiple impairments from a single condition:
    • a single medical condition may result in multiple functional impairments which can be assigned ratings from more than one table
    • all functional impairments should be assigned an impairment rating from all relevant tables
  • 2 or more medical conditions with a common impairment:
    • only one relevant table should be applied and a single impairment rating assigned to reflect the combined impairment

Note: it would be inappropriate to assign a separate impairment rating for each medical condition as this would result in the same impairment being assessed more than once (double counting). Double counting is not allowed and must be avoided.

5

Assigning an Impairment Rating + Read more ...

The following rules must be applied when assigning impairment ratings for each impairment:

  • Impairment ratings can only be assigned in accordance with the rating points in each table
  • Ratings cannot be assigned in excess of the maximum rating specified in each table
  • If an impairment rating is considered as falling between 2 ratings, the lower of the 2 ratings is to be assigned and the higher rating must not be assigned unless all the descriptors required for that rating are fully met

The References page contains a link to the Guide to Social Security Law (3.6.3) for links to the Guidelines to the pre- and post-1 January 2012 Impairment Tables.

Descriptors hierarchy

The descriptors in each table follow a consistent, incremental hierarchy using terms such as occasionally, regularly and frequently.

Meaning of 'most' in the descriptors

For the purpose of applying the tables, 'most' means more than 50%. For example:

  • if there are 3 examples in the descriptor, 'most' means 2
  • if there are 4 examples, 'most' means 3
  • if there are 6, 'most' means 4

Functional impact

In deciding whether an impairment has no, mild, moderate, severe or extreme functional impact, all the descriptors in a specific table should be read and compared before a decision is made to apply an appropriate impairment rating:

  • no functional impact - 0 points
  • mild functional impact - 5 points
  • moderate functional impact - 10 points
  • severe functional impact - 20 points
  • extreme functional impact - 30 points

Note: a person's impairment must be assessed on the basis of the person's usual abilities and not what the person chooses to do, or not to do, or what the person is accustomed to having another person do for them.

Procedure ends here.
