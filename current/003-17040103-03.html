Secondary Claimants + Read more ...

A primary claimant is able to claim for a secondary claimant who is unable to qualify in their own right, if the secondary claimant is:

  • the spouse of the primary claimant
  • a dependent adult who lives with, and is financially dependent on the primary claimant, and does not qualify in their own right, or
  • a child for whom the claimant is the primary carer

Spouse has the same meaning given by section 1207A of the Social Security Act 1991 and includes a person who is a member of a couple.

  • Spouse, in relation to a person who is a member of a couple, means the other member of the couple
  • The spouse does not need to be a participant in the Australian taxation system
  • The spouse can be of any nationality

Finalise the claim, as normal, for the primary claimant and any dependent children.

If the primary claimant is also claiming for secondary claimant/s, after finalising the primary claimant/child claim, document the secondary claimant details and place the keyword EXGDEP on the work item.

6

Serious injury as a direct result of the disaster + Read more ...

A person is considered to be seriously injured if, as a direct result of the disaster:

  • they were admitted to hospital, or
  • under normal circumstances, they would have been admitted to hospital

Evidence may be requested for verification purposes. Evidence may include admittance, treatment and/or discharge papers from the hospital.

Was the claimant seriously injured as a direct result of the disaster, or a principal carer of a dependent child who was seriously injured as a direct result of the disaster?

  • Yes, run the Disaster Recovery Payment Claims workflow. Procedure ends here
  • No, go to Step 7

7

Immediate family member killed + Read more ...

A person is considered to be killed as a direct result of the disaster if:

  • the person was killed in a way consistent with the impacts of the disaster
  • due to the impacts of the disaster the person was unable to seek medical attention
  • the disaster aggravated an existing condition which caused death
  • the person is assessed as a missing person

This is an initial list of potential situations in which the person may have died as a direct result of the disaster and is not intended to be a comprehensive list that covers all possible scenarios.

Verification of a death of an 'immediate family member' should be provided by the claimant. Social workers are allocated as standard practice when a claimant advises death of an immediate family member. The social worker can assist in obtaining verification.

If this is not available, a self-declaration by the claimant may be accepted. Is the claimant an immediate family member of an Australian who was killed as a direct result of the disaster, or a principal carer of a dependent child whose Australian immediate family member was killed as a direct result of the disaster?

  • Yes, run the Disaster Recovery Payment Claims workflow. Procedure end here
  • No, go to Step 8

8

Principal place of residence destroyed as a direct result of the disaster + Read more ...

A principal place of residence will be considered destroyed if the residence has been destroyed as a direct result of the disaster, or it has been damaged to the extent that it must be demolished.

Was the claimant's principal place of residence destroyed as a direct result of the disaster, or was the claimant a principal carer of a dependent child whose principal place of residence was destroyed as a direct result of the disaster?

  • Yes, run the Disaster Recovery Payment Claims workflow. Procedure ends here
  • No, go to Step 9

9

Principal place of resident sustained major damage as a direct result of the disaster + Read more ...

The following elements establish whether a principal place of residence has suffered 'major damage' when, as a direct result of the disaster:

  • at least one-quarter of the interior of the residence has been affected by the disaster, or
  • the residence has been declared structurally unsound as supported by evidence, ◦ evidence could include, but is not limited to a builder's or engineer's report, or
  • the residence has been breached, exposing at least one-quarter or more of the rooms and/or surface floor area of the residence of the interior of the residence to the elements, or
  • the interior of the residence has experienced sewerage contamination

'One-quarter' requires at least 25 per cent or more of the interior of the residence to be affected by the disaster.

'One-quarter' can be calculated by assessing either the proportion of the number of rooms affected or the proportion of the surface floor area of the residence.

The size of the area affected can be calculated by assessing either the proportion of the number of rooms affected or surface floor area of the residence.

Consideration should be given to the floor area that could possibly be affected. For example, in some circumstances, the ground floor of a multi-storey home can be considered affected.

The Operational Guidelines contain a full description of this criteria. See References for a link to the guidelines.

Did the claimant's principal place of residence sustain major damage as a direct result of the disaster, or was the claimant a principal carer of a dependent child whose principal place of residence sustained major damage as a direct result of the disaster?

  • Yes, run the Disaster Recovery Payment Claims workflow. Procedure ends here
  • No, claimant is not eligible. Another social security payment may be payable to meet the needs of the claimant. See Table 5
